DESCRIPTION OF THE PROBLEM
A common question throughout the public comment process for the proposed ordinance amendment
was whether a problem exists that needs solving. The problems with the current FR and FA ordinances have been
manifest in the Hi Country Estates area (a zoning map of this area can be found at the following link: _____________,
but they involve ordinances that affect other areas of Unincorporated Salt Lake County. First, there is confusion
about what permits are required (if any) in Hi Country Estates to have horses and other animals, what the process
is to obtain such permits, and why the process is different depending on what zone a property owner is in. It
appears that the understanding among Hi Country Estates property owners is that all properties are “horse”
properties. However, in the FR zone, where horses are a conditional use, there are currently only nine conditional
use permits (and over 190 lots that are zoned FR, albeit all of these do not have horses); in the FA zone, where
horses are a permitted use, there are currently only 1 or 2 permits (and approximately 301 lots that are zoned FA,
albeit all of these do not have horses). Unfortunately, Code Enforcement staff is placed in a situation that puts the
County at risk of selective enforcement claims and creates the potential of hostility between neighbors when
some property owners are following County ordinance, while others are not. The proposed ordinance provides a
streamlined process for property owners to obtain a permit in a way that addresses concerns about water quality
and nuisances to neighbors who have complained to Code Enforcement staff for years.
Second, there are significant water quality problems in areas of Unincorporated Salt Lake County zoned
FR and FA caused by animals near streams which is a major contributor to the water quality problems. The
County’s failure to address those problems via ordinance will place Salt Lake County at significant risk of financial
penalties in the future. Attached as Appendix A to this Staff Report is a thorough summary of this problem
completed by Salt Lake County Public Works. The proposed ordinance provides for stream setbacks and other
measures to address these water quality concerns, while grandfathering existing improvements and permit
holders.
Third, there has been a long history of complaints to Code Enforcement staff by neighbors of horse
owners regarding nuisances caused by horse owners. The proposed ordinance requires a manure management
plan and other measures to address these concerns.
Finally, it should be noted that many complaints to Code Enforcement staff have focused on commercial
uses related to horses, such as boarding and trail riding. After receiving significant public input on this issue, the
MSD Planning staff is of the opinion that the existing zoning ordinances are adequate to address these concerns.

In summary, if the County does nothing to update its FR/FA ordinances, the following will likely
occur: 1) property owners in one zone (FR) having to go through a more expensive, time-consuming process to
obtain a permit than another zone (FA). The expensive, time-consuming process becomes an incentive to not
come into compliance, resulting in some property owners who are compliant, others that are not and contributing
to neighbor disputes over this situation, and the County at risk for selective enforcement; 2) horses and other
animals housed in close proximity to streams, and heightened risk to the County of significant penalties imposed
by the federal and/or state government for not adopting standards to mitigate those impacts; and, 3) continued
complaints to Code Enforcement and MSD Planning staff regarding animal nuisances.

DESCRIPTION OF THE PROPOSED ORDINANCE UPDATE
________________________________________________________________________________________________________________

There was significant public input received throughout the Mountainous Planning District and Salt Lake County
Planning Commission recommendation process, and many revisions of the original proposed ordinance were
made in response to the public input received. The draft ordinance attached to this Staff Report is the latest draft
with these revisions. The draft ordinance provides for the following:

Modify current FR ordinance as follows:
1. Horse/animal use a permitted (not conditional) use, which is a more streamlined process than the
current conditional use process.
2. Add more objective criteria to the current ordinance, 2 horses per acre limit unless there is a stream
present, in which case a maximum of 4 horses is allowed.1 See Appendix B for explanation of how MSD
Planning Staff arrived at 2 horses per acre and its response to public requests for more horses per acre.
The horses per acre calculation excludes from acreage those improvements/areas that do not support
animals, namely driveways, the footprint of a home and accessory structures that do not house animals,
and the 100’ setbacks referenced in #3.

3. If streams are present on or adjoining a property, a 100’ setback between streams and areas that house
animals is required, with the following explicit exception: existing structures that lawfully exist now may
remain as legal noncomplying structures. The 100’ setback is necessary to address the water quality
problems outlined in Appendix A, but the noncomplying structure exception holds harmless lawfully
existing structures.

4. Manure management plan is required with each application, which addresses mitigation of stormwater
runoff, odor, and flies, and shall include the following:
a. Clean-up of manure in stalls and off-site deposit of the same on a weekly basis. Manure storage
or composting is not allowed except as Salt Lake County Health Department General Sanitation
Regulation explicitly permits composting.
b. Siting of stalls to protect surface water, drainageways, wellheads, streams, and irrigation
ditches.
c. Bedding system such as stall mats, hay, wood shavings, and/or wood pellets
5. Each water system or district must approve of having animals within their respective jurisdictions.
6. Current permits recognized notwithstanding ordinance changes.
Keep current FA ordinance as is (including streamlined permitted use process and 4 horse limit for personal
use), with the following additions:
1. 100’ setback if stream present per #3 above
2. Manure Management Plan per #4 above
1 The Mountainous Planning District Planning Commission’s recommendation for approval did not include this 4 horse cap

on properties with streams. However, Staff recommends this 4 horse cap because the more horses near streams the greater
risk of impact on water quality, even with a 100’ setback. The 4 horse cap is consistent with the cap on horses in the FA
zone.

Planning Commission and Staff Recommendations
The Salt Lake County Planning Commission recommended denial of the attached draft ordinance,
apparently responding to opposition to the draft ordinance from residents of Hi-Country I and II. The
Mountainous Planning District Planning Commission recommended approval of the attached draft ordinance,
with the exception noted in footnote 1 of this staff report, apparently responding to the water quality concerns
raised by Staff. Staff wishes to emphasize this staff report’s (including Appendix A’s) outline of water quality
concerns and the risks to Salt Lake County for failure to address those concerns in an ordinance update. That,
combined with ongoing enforcement problems, compels Staff to recommend approval of the draft ordinance
update.
