Community Council

At our board meeting last night the League of Unincorporated Community Councils (LUCC), we discussed the
"Proposed Parley's Canyon Mine," and the changes by the Petitioner for the mining operations permit. It looks
as though a Notice of Intent (NOI) for a Small Mining Operation is being considered once again and possibly, a
Notice of Intent (NOI) for a Large Mining Operation up Parleys Canyon, West of the Mount Aire area, in Salt
Lake County. Our boards discussion once again came to the same conclusions for our recommendations to the
Utah State Board of Oil, Gas, and Mining.
As a organization that represents the opinions of the surrounding property owners and/or stake holders, of
this area. We believe that mining this area poses to many risks and damage to the health, safety, and
environment of the eco system, its wildlife, and surrounding water systems for this area.
Therefore, it is the position of the League of Unincorporated Community Councils (LUCC) to recommend that
these permit(s), "NOI for a Small Mining Operation Permit" and/or "NOI for a Large Mining Operation Permit,"
be DENIED. Our decision is based on the overwhelming outcry of concerns by our community residents and
stakeholders that live or own property near, and/or around this area in question. The property owner's, have
asked LUCC, a 501c-4 non-profit organization that, "Exercises, promotes andprotects the privileges and
interests of the residents ofSLCo," as a recommending body for the Unincorporated areas to local, State, and
Federal governments, to support their position in denying the NOI permits for this area.
Parleys Canyon: Parleys Canyon, with its scenic beauty and majestic features, serves as one of Salt Lake
County, Summit County, and Salt Lake City's most precious gems. Parleys Canyon serves as a gateway for our
visitors to the capital city and our famous Olympic facilities, local ski resorts, and Park City.

Willow
Canyon

Health and Safety:
Residents and property owners living or staying near and/or around the proposed area of mining, have
concerns that they will be subject to toxic materials and waste, as a result of the proposed mining activities.
Environmental Damage: We believe that mining this area will pose too many risks and damage to the
surrounding environment, eco system, its wildlife, and water systems for this area. Residents and property
owners in the unincorporated areas surrounding this mine have communicated their concerns for their
watershed. The watershed "zone" for this proposed area of mining is also of concern, especially since the State
of Utah has been in such a drastic drought situation that will most likely get worse with increased populations.
We believe that this is not an acceptable mining location for any of our unincorporated citizens residing in or
owning property around, the Mount Aire area, in Salt Lake County. Please do not allow or permit this mining
operation to move forward.

I appreciate all the work and consideration given by the Division of Oil, Gas and Mining and the Board
of DOGM in light of all the current whirlwind of actions, motions and comments. There is an important
need for reviewing, updating and reforming mining policies, rules and regulations as they concern

support Utah’s growth. The March 23, 2022 hearing contained many interesting and important points and

opportunity to engage with the Division and Board under new policies and rules to vet, evaluate, and
mitigate the impacts of small and large mining operations, especially in Class 1 and 2 counties.
This will also allow time for the Division and the Board to file a new Motion of Police Guidance; examine
and discuss these issues, changes and revisions; have a formal hearing meeting; and then have a vote.
The mission of the Utah Division of Oil, Gas and Mining is to regulate the exploration and development
of coal, oil and gas, and other minerals in a manner which:
1. encourages responsible and reasonable reclamation and development in light of land uses;
2. protects associated rights;
3. prevents waste and the degradation of the ecologic and hydrologic environments; and
4. protects human health and safety, the environment, and the interests of the state and its citizens.
Please use the laws and powers provided to you and issue an emergency order while policies and rules are
reviewed and resolved.
Thank you for your time and consideration.

Dear Director Baza,
I am writing this email to request the DOGM enact an immediate Emergency Order for a moratorium on small mine
applications. Per the Utah Mine Reclamation Act, DOGM is obligated to minimize or prevent future hazards to public
safety and welfare. It is my understanding that the small mine application process does not currently consider the public
safety of those who live within the Mt. Air community (150 plus residential home owners). Some of these residents live
less than ½ a mile away from the mining area. They will be directly impacted by this mine whether it is small or large.
There is no consideration in the small mine application process that addresses the extreme fire danger for those who live
up this 1 lane (15 mph road). Please note that this canyon has no exit. A fire starting on Tree Farm land with winds
blowing up the canyon would potentially trap hundreds of people up a canyon with no exit. Please clarify how the small
mine application process will address this future hazard to public safety.

Also, it is my understanding that the small mine application process does not address the increased avalanche risks to
the Mt. Air Community. To understand these risks, I would propose the DOGM and board members take a field trip up the
canyon to understand the unique challenges of this canyon and the enhanced avalanche risks that the mining explosions
will create. As stated above, this unique canyon has only a one lane road because the mountains rise steeply on both
sides of this very narrow canyon. There is one spot in the canyon called Rabbit Head. Each year the section of road that
passes through Rabbit Head has at least one small avalanche. Our property is approximately 1 mile above this area. We
have the remains on our property of a cabin destroyed by an avalanche years ago. Please clarify how the small mine
application process will address enhanced avalanche risks from mining explosions.

In conclusion, I would argue that each property is unique and as such would need to be individually analyzed to
determine how a mining operation (no matter whether it is small or large) would affect neighboring properties and the
surrounding communities and what needs to be done to prevent future hazards to public safety and welfare. For these
reasons, I would strongly encourage the DOGM to enact an immediate Emergency Order for a moratorium on small mine
applications until these public safety hazards can be addressed through the small mine application process.

Sincerely,

Angela McGuire

The fire is in an area where a blaze destroyed 1,000 homes last year in
unincorporated Boulder County and suburban Superior and Louisville.
Superior town officials told residents in an email that there were no
immediate concerns for the community.
The 2021 blaze burned Alicia Miller's home, where she could see smoke from
Saturday's fire rising in the background. She posted a photo on Twitter and
referenced climate change, which has made the U.S. West warmer and drier in

the past 3o years and will continue to make weather more extreme and
wildfires more destructive, according to scientists.
Miller said her neighbors helped her escape along with her husband, Craig,
their three adult sons and two dogs, Ginger and Chloe. She said the hardest
losses from the blaze were things they didn't look at much, like baby shoes,
family pictures and letters from her grandmother.
"I feel exhausted by all of this, and I just feel like enough as far as these fires
and disasters," she said. She pointed to a recent Texas wildfire that left a
deputy dead and homes destroyed. " ... So I'm standing there and it's just kind
of a repeat."
Saturday's fire started around 2 p.m. and burned protected wildland near the
National Center for Atmospheric Research, Boulder police said. Authorities
have called it the NCAR fire and its cause is not yet known, said Washburn.
