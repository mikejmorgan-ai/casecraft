STANDARD OF REVIEW
Under Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(3)(a), the Court reviews final county land use
decisions to determine “whether or not the decision, ordinance, or regulation is arbitrary,
capricious, or illegal.” Though there is a presumption of validity, the land use decision is only
valid if the decision “is supported by substantial evidence in the record and is not arbitrary,
capricious, or illegal.” Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(3)(c). Illegality is determined under a
correctness standard of review, which provides only “some level of non-binding deference to the
interpretation advanced by the land use authority.” Fox v. Park City, 2008 UT 85, 200 P.3d 182
(internal citation omitted).
ARGUMENT
I.

THE COMMISSION’S REMAND DECISION WAS ARBITRARY AND CAPRICIOUS

The Utah Supreme Court has declared that a LUDMA land use authority, such as the
Planning Commission, must “produce findings of fact capable of review on appeal,” or its decision
is arbitrary and capricious. McElhaney v. City of Moab, 2017 UT 65, ¶ 41, ___ P.3d ___. The
Court reasoned that without specific findings of fact, a land use decision cannot be supported by
substantial evidence because “in the absence of explicit findings of fact and conclusions of law,
the reasoning behind the [land use authority’s] decision is an amorphous target” that is unamenable
to “meaningful appellate review.” Id. at ¶¶ 31, 41.

14

In this case, the Planning Commission—as the land use authority—failed to provide a
written decision or any explicit findings of fact supporting its decision not to revoke the Amended
CUP. Accordingly, the P&Z Decision could not be affirmed under McElhaney. The Planning
Commission is the only entity authorized to make factual findings. See Zoning Ordinance § 6-10;
UTAH CODE § 10-9a-707(3). In fact, the Commission and the Court are expressly prohibited from
affixing the amorphous target created by the P&Z Decision. See SAN JUAN COUNTY ZONING
ORDINANCE 2-2(2)(e); McElhaney, 2017 UT 65, ¶¶ 40-41. Any attempt to do so is reversible error.
Nevertheless, the Commission upheld that decision in its Remand Decision, instead of
remanding to the Planning Commission for findings of fact and conclusions of law capable of
appellate review. The Commission did not know what documents the Planning Commission
consulted when it made the P&Z Decision, much less what the rationale for that decision was.2
Upholding the unwritten and unsupported P&Z Decision is arbitrary and capricious. Id.
Consequently, this case must be remanded to the Planning Commission for a written decision with
express findings of fact after allowing NMA to present evidence and testimony.

insufficiencies in the P&Z Decision, the Commission has failed to do so. The Remand Decision is
devoid of any findings of fact or rationale for upholding the P&Z Decision. The Commission
merely states that “We cannot say that the Planning Commission’s decision not to revoke the

Furthermore, as sPower readily admits, “the single largest component of flicker and sound
impact is proximity to the turbine locations.” (R1894.) sPower moved the turbines significantly
closer to the NMA Property after the Amended CUP was approved. (Compare R1064—the 2012
sound analysis map—with R0600—the 2013 sound analysis map.) Because proximity is the single
largest component of Sound and Flicker impact, it is necessarily true that sPower has increased,
not mitigated the Sound and Flicker on NMA Property—no matter what Turbines it used. Thus,
there is no way the Planning Commission or the Commission could reasonably conclude that
sPower has provided as much Sound or Flicker mitigation as possible.
Finally, sPower touts that it negotiated the number of required red safety lights from 27
down to 14. That claim may seem plausible on its face. But sPower has not presented evidence
regarding which Turbines have lights and which do not. Without evidence on that issue, it is
impossible to say whether NMA Property will be less impacted by the light from the current
Turbine locations than it would have been by the light from the approved Turbine locations. If the
14 Turbines closest to the NMA Property all have red safety lights, then the negative impact of
light on NMA Property may be worse now that the Turbines are closer to the Property than when
all the Turbines had red lights but were farther away.
Additionally, there nothing in the record, excepting bald assertions in letters from sPower,
that indicates that sPower actually negotiated with the FAA to install fewer red lights than required
by FAA regulations. sPower has not provided the federal regulations or any documentation of their
negotiations with the FAA. There is, accordingly, no evidence that sPower has provided Light
mitigation relative to the NMA Property.

19

The County’s treatment of NMA Property is the epitome of arbitrariness. Turbine #9 was
relocated over a mile from the original location approved by the Amended CUP. That turbine is
now within 270 meters of the NMA Property. sPower has admitted that this relocation increases,
not mitigates, the Flicker, Light, and Sound affecting the NMA Property. (R1894.) Yet, the County
has ignored negative effects to the NMA Property.
If Turbine #9 had been relocated the same distance to the south-east, it would now be in
the middle of Monticello. Undoubtedly, the County would require abundant up-to-date mitigation
studies and reports regarding every piece of property that could conceivably be negatively affected
by that relocation. The County is not entitled to indiscriminately ignore the negative effects of the
relocation simply because the Turbines were moved closer to NMA Property rather than closer to
town. The requirement to undertake “as much flicker, light, sound, mitigation as possible” is meant
to protect all property rights, not just property rights in an incorporated municipality.
II.

THE COMMISSION’S AMENDED DECISION IS ILLEGAL AS VIOLATIVE OF THE DUE
PROCESS CLAUSE OF THE UNITED STATES’ CONSTITUTION
The Remand Decision is also illegal, as it violates the Due Process Clause of the United

States Constitution. The Fourteenth Amendment states that “[n]o State shall make or enforce any
law which shall . . . deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.”
U.S. CONST. amend. XIV, § 1.
If a party’s property interest is at stake, “[d]ue process requires, at a minimum, adequate
notice and ‘an opportunity to be heard in a meaningful manner.’” Salt Lake City Corp. v. Jordan
River Restoration Network, 2012 UT 84, ¶ 111, 299 P.3d 990 (quoting Salt Lake Legal Defender
Ass'n v. Atherton, 2011 UT 58, ¶ 2, 267 P.3d 227). “Typically, a hearing must provide parties the
20

opportunity to present ‘evidence, objections, and arguments, to the end that the ... court may be
enabled to fairly and intelligently pass upon and determine the questions presented for decision.’”
Id. (quoting McGrew v. Indus. Comm'n, 96 Utah 203, 85 P.2d 608, 616 (1938)).
NMA Members have a property interest in the unrestricted use and enjoyment of their
Property. See United States v. James Daniel Good Real Prop., 510 U.S. 43, 54 (1993)
(acknowledging a property owner’s interest in “the right to unrestricted use and enjoyment” of its
property). A decision not to revoke the Amended CUP in the face of sPower’s continuing failure
to comply with the conditions of the Amended CUP to mitigate the impact of the Wind Facility on
the NMA Property is depriving NMA Members of any economically viable use of their Property,
as well as peaceful enjoyment of their Property.
Accordingly, the County must provide NMA a meaningful opportunity to be heard—
including an opportunity to present evidence, objections, and arguments to the Planning
Commission as to why the Amended CUP should be revoked. The County has consistently denied
NMA this opportunity.
It is insufficient that NMA be given an opportunity to argue before the County Commission
or the District Court because those entities can only review the Planning Commission’s decision
based exclusively on the evidence and arguments presented to the Planning Commission. UTAH
CODE ANN. § 17-27a-801(3)(a)(ii); ZONING ORDINANCE 2-2(e). These reviews have a much more
deferential standard favoring the holder of the CUP than that of the Land Use Authority, where
the standard of review is whether or not the CUP should be revoked based on preponderance of
the evidence. See ZONING ORDINANCE 6-10.

21

Simply put, the Planning Commission is the only entity that can provide NMA any
meaningful opportunity to present its cause—especially where the Commission must “presume
that the [Planning Commission’s] decision applying the land use ordinance is valid . . ..” SAN JUAN
COUNTY ZONING ORDINANCE § 2-2(2)(e). This opportunity has never occurred.
Before the Planning Commission, NMA will be able to provide evidence concerning the
detrimental effects sPower’s actions have had on NMA’s Property. As discussed above, there is
no meaningful data in the record regarding NMA’s Property in all of sPower’s mitigation evidence.
That is a direct result of NMA’s being barred from presenting evidence or argument before the
Planning Commission. If NMA had been permitted to participate in a hearing before the Planning
Commission, the record would contain all of NMA’s evidence, and sPower would have to rebut
that evidence with up-to-date studies and reports specific to the NMA Property. Otherwise, sPower
would fail to demonstrate that it is compliant with the requirements of the Amended CUP. sPower
would also have to explain why, after committing to the Planning Commission to purchase the
NMA Property if it could enter into an agreement to do so, sPower failed and refused to exercise
the Option Agreement.
Similarly, NMA’s arguments before the Commission and this Court have been necessarily
limited by NMA’s lack of a meaningful opportunity to be heard. For example, the Commission
and this Court have refused to hear arguments about Ice Throw, financial mitigation, sPower’s
failure to utilize the Dark Sky Technology that Latigo/Wasatch Wind committed to install
(R1635), and building permit errors because the Planning Commission apparently limited its
decision to whether sPower had provided “as much flicker, light, sound, mitigation as possible.”

22

That is a direct and unacceptable result of the Planning Commission’s decision to permit only
sPower to participate in the revocation hearing, effectively gagging NMA and its Members.
Unsurprisingly, sPower limited the discussion before the Planning Commission to the
specific mitigation requirements mentioned in the Planning Commission’s minutes recognizing
the approval of the Amended CUP. sPower did not address the general requirements placed on
every CUP by county laws or the other mitigation requirements articulated in the hearing where
the Amended CUP was approved. See SAN JUAN ZONING ORDINANCE § 6-4(1); see also
R0776-0862. This violates due process where NMA Member’s property interests are at stake. In
owning directly affected property, NMA is entitled to meaningfully raise its arguments regarding,
inter alia, the safety hazards and other pitfalls resulting from the relocation of the Turbines. And
it will only have the opportunity to do so by making its arguments to the Planning Commission.
By upholding the Planning Commission’s decision to not permit NMA’s participation in
the revocation hearing, the Commission has wrongly limited NMA’s ability to meaningfully
present its full panoply of evidence, objections, and arguments.

The Commission has,

consequently, violated NMA’s right to Due Process.
III.

THE COMMISSION’S REMAND DECISION WAS PROCEDURALLY IMPROPER AND
PROHIBITED BY LAW

Under Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-701 and Sections 2-3 and 6-7 of the Zoning Ordinance,
the Commission had authority to hear and act on NMA’s appeal of the P&Z Decision. Pursuant
to that authority and in accordance with Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-708, which provides that “[a]
decision of an appeal authority takes effect on the date when the appeal authority issues a written

23

decision,” and that “[a] written decision … constitutes a final decision under Subsection 17-27a801(2)(a),” the Commission rendered its Final Written Decision on December 3, 2015.
Rather than petitioning for review with the district court, which was the only remedy
available to sPower following the Final Written Decision (R0041), sPower sent a Request for
Reconsideration to the Commission demanding reconsideration and amendment to the Final
Written Decision and threatening legal action if the Commission did not acquiesce to sPower’s
demands by “4:00 p.m. Monday, December 7, 2015”. (R0025–0029)
Neither LUDMA nor the Zoning Ordinances allow the Final Written Decision to be
reconsidered or amended following the issuance of a Final Written Decision by the Commission.
Indeed, pursuant to Utah Code Ann § 17-27a-801, the only further relief that may be sought by an
adversely affected person is not to demand that the Commission reconsider and amend its Final
Written Decision, but to “file a petition for review of the decision with the district court within 30
days after the local land use decision is final.”
Unlike the Administrative Procedures Act, which recognizes state agencies’ authority to
reconsider their own decisions, Utah Code Section 63G-4-302, LUDMA does not recognize or
grant the same authority to land use appeal authorities—like the Commission. And while the Utah
Supreme Court has previously stated that “[i]nherent in the power to make an administrative
decision is the authority to reconsider a decision,” Clark v. Hansen, 631 P.2d 914, 915 (Utah 1981),
it was speaking about a state agency’s review, which is an administrative decision. A LUDMA
appeal authority is not an administrative agency and its decisions on appellate review do not
constitute administrative decisions.

24

Nevertheless, on December 7, 2015, in a closed meeting, the Commission capitulated to
the threat of legal action from sPower and reconsidered the Final Written Decision. (R0001–06.)
The next day, the Commission illegally amended the Final Written Decision and removed the
requirement that the Planning Commission, with appropriate technical assistance, determine if
sPower was actually complying with the requirement in the Amended CUP to implement “as much
flicker, light, sound, mitigation as possible.” Id.
Because neither the reconsideration of the original Final Written Decision nor the issuance
of an amended written decision were authorized by law, the Commission’s actions were ultra vires
and void. Indeed, by taking such action, the Commission illegally arrogated to itself this Court’s
exclusive authority to review the final decisions of LUDMA appeal authorities.
On the subsequent appeal, his Court remanded the case back to the Commission and
directed the Commission to hold new proceedings on sPower’s request for reconsideration because
“NMA should have been heard in opposition” to the request. Memorandum Decision at 8. The
Commission held a non-evidentiary hearing on sPower’s request on January 3, 2017, and it issued
its Remand Decision on February 21, 2017.
The Commission noted that “the purpose of our rehearing was solely to consider sPower’s
request for reconsideration based upon its complaint that, contrary to our finding, it had indeed
provided evidence of mitigation beyond its bare representations.” Remand Decision at 8. In other
words, the Commission was once again reconsidering its original Final Written Decision.
Accordingly, its Remand Decision was based exclusively on that reconsideration.
The Final Written Decision can no longer be reviewed by this Court because the 30-day
period for applying for review has long since passed. Moreover, the 30-day period should not be
25

tolled because sPower was explicitly told what they needed to do to appeal the Final Written
Decision in the Decision itself. Nevertheless, sPower decided to take different unauthorized action
instead. sPower should not benefit from its clear disregard of the proper legal procedure.
Consequently, because sPower never asked this Court to review the Final Written Decision, the
Court should revoke the Remand Decision and permanently restore the Final Written Decision.
IV.

CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, the Court should grant NMA’s Motion for Summary Judgment
and issue a writ of mandamus ordering the Commission to direct the Planning Commission to
revoke the Amended CUP for sPower’s failure to comply with the conditions of the Amended
CUP, or in the alternative, the Court should permanently reinstate the Final Written Decision,
which directs the Planning Commission to conduct further proceedings on the revocation of the
Amended CUP.

AMENDMENT TO WRITTEN DECISION

Commissioner Phil Lyman Dissent:
Mitigation is a central point in the two appeals filed by Northern Monticello
Alliance (NMA) and Summit Wind. On February 8, 2013 Latigo Wind Park, LLC (Latigo)
paid NMA $1,250 per acre for an option to purchase all of the land of the NMA for
$10,450 per acre. The agreement was signed by Christine Watson Mikell as Manager
for Latigo and, as near as I can tell, by all the land owners in the NMA, by Dan Bingham
as the official representative of the NMA, and by Daniel Anderson for Anderson Oliver
Title Company. (see tab 4 of the black binders given to the Commissioners at the
November 10, hearing).
That option agreement said that "each Option Payment shall be non-refundable
to Latigo Wind and shall not be applicable to the "Purchase Price". It also

was

contingent on, among other things, NMA withdrawing its appeals before February 25,

1 .
R14

2013 and then cooperating with Latigo by not filing appeals for two years thereafter.
(Item 8 of the option agreement).
I do know all the specifics of the negotiations between Latigo and NMA, but it is
clear that the option was not exercised. I am troubled that Latigo has since criticized
NMA for not filing timely appeals. Whether there were formal appeals in place prior to
February 25, 2013, I don't know, but from my observation, NMA at least complied by not
filing additional appeals during the time agreed upon in the Option Agreement.
Also included in the black binders from November 10th is a string of emails
between Sean McBride, legal counsel for Latigo, and Dan Bingham. That string of
communications chronicles the back-and-forth negotiations between the two entities
following the lapse of the option agreement, but the purchase price range had dropped
to between $1,000 and $9,000 per acre.
When it comes to a fair mitigation settlement between a private business and
private individuals, it would be inappropriate for the County insert themselves into that
process other than to the extent that the Planning and Zoning Commission made
financial mitigation a condition of the Conditional Use Permit.
Clearly the payment of the $1,250 per acre was for an option. This option is a
separate financial instrument. It was purchased, and it was allowed to expire
unexercised. It was specifically not "applicable to the Purchase Price." That $1,250 was
a cost incurred in the course of doing business on the part of Latigo. It allowed them to
move forward in developing the Project. Had they been unable to ultimately bring the
project to fruition then they would not exercise the option and would not be stuck with 80
acres of land. The option was a hedge against that potentiality. Since the project did

2

move forward Latigo could be certain that they would not be taken advantage of and
their Project held hostage by the NMA land owners because they had a solid option
agreement in place. By letting the option lapse, they gave up that insurance, and are
now faced with striking some sort of mitigation deal with the NMA landowners.
In relation to the Summit Wind/Kimberly Ceruti appeal, the matter is a bit more
direct. Summit claims that Latigo obtained their CUP and Building Permit without having
leases in place, or claiming leases on parcels that were actually under lease with Mrs.
Ceruti.
