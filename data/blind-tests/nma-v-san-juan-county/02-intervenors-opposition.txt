I.

SUMMARY
On appeal for the second time, NMA asks this Court to disregard the law of the case, to

ignore the certified administrative record; and to excuse NMA’s failure to carry its burden before
the County Commission and before this Court to demonstrate that the County Commission’s
Decision is arbitrary and capricious because it is unsupported by substantial evidence.
On September 9, 2016, this Court issued its decision remanding NMA’s 2016 appeal to the
San Juan County Board of Commissioners (“County Commission”). This Court determined that:
(1) the County Commission’s Amended Decision issued on December 3, 2015, is supported by
substantial evidence and was not arbitrary or capricious; and, (2) that the County Commission has
the inherent authority to “reconsider a decision and correct its mistakes” through the issuance of its
Amended Decision.

But, this Court also determined that notwithstanding the County

Commission’s authority to correct its mistakes, the County Commission must observe procedural
due process requirements in doing so. This Court determined that County Commission’s Amended
Decision was illegal because the County Commission did not provide NMA with notice and the
opportunity to be heard on sPower’s request for reconsideration and amendment of the County
Commission’s Written Decision issued on December 3, 2016. The matter was remanded back to
the County Commission to provide NMA with opportunity to respond to sPower’s request.
On January 3, 2017, the San Juan County Commission (“Commission”) considered briefing
and heard oral arguments on sPower’s request for reconsideration. sPower argued that the County
Commission’s original Written Decision was issued in error because it did not acknowledge or
consider substantial evidence presented to the County Commission demonstrating sPower’s CUP
compliance. Consistent with this Court’s decision, sPower also argued that the County had the
authority to reconsider its original Written Decision and to issue an amendment thereto. sPower
1

requested that the County Commission exercise its authority and reconsider its decision in light of
substantial evidence presented by sPower demonstrating CUP compliance.
Predictably, NMA did not use its “opportunity” to respond to sPower’s request for
reconsideration. NMA did not challenge sPower’s request. Moreover, NMA did not dispute
County Commission’s authority to issue an amended decision. NMA did not dispute that sPower
previously presented the County Commission with two three-ringed binders that included evidence
demonstrating that sPower implemented as much “flicker, sound and light mitigation as possible.”
In fact, NMA’s pre-hearing brief fails to make a single reference to sPower’s request for
reconsideration. Instead, NMA took the opportunity to relitigate its appeal and claim that the
Planning Commission’s decision was arbitrary and capricious, and that evidence provided sPower
did not demonstrate CUP compliance. Further, NMA claimed that the only possible way for
sPower to comply with the CUP was for sPower to satisfy non-existent “financial mitigation
conditions” and purchase NMA members’ property at 360% above the appraised market value.
On February 21, 2017, the County Commission issued its “Amended Written Decision on
Remand.” The Commission granted sPower’s request and considered evidence properly before the
Commission. The County Commission held that sPower presented substantial evidence supporting
CUP compliance to the County Commission and to the Planning Commission. The Commission
determined that NMA failed to meet its burden on appeal to demonstrate that the Planning
Commission’s was arbitrary or capricious of that sPower did not comply with its CUP. The
County Commission, again, vacated its Written Decision issued in error and issued its Amended
Written Decision on Remand.
sPower respectfully asks this court to deny NMA’s Motion based on the substantial and
undisputed evidence that the Board’s decision to issue an amended decision on remand upholding
2

the Planning Commission’s compliance determination is not arbitrary or capricious and is
supported by substantial evidence and is not illegal.

III.

SUMMARY JUDGMENT STANDARD
“A district court should grant summary judgment only when, viewing all facts and

reasonable inferences therefrom in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, there is no
genuine issue as to any material fact and … the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter
of law.” Suarez v. Grand County, 2012 UT 72, ¶18 (quoting Morra v. Grand Cnty., 2010 UT 21,
12). Where the moving party would bear the burden of proof at trial, the movant must establish
each element of his claim in order to show that he is entitled to judgement as a matter of law.
Orvis v. Johnson, 2008 UT 2, ¶10, 177 P.3d 600.
In an appeal of a county’s land use decision, “[t]he courts shall: (i) presume that a decision,
ordinance, or regulation made under the authority of this chapter is valid; and (ii) determine only
whether or not the decision, ordinance, or regulation is arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.” Fuller v.
Springville City, 2015 UT App 177, ¶ 17 (quoting Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(3)(a)). A decision
is valid if it is supported by substantial evidence in the record and is not arbitrary, capricious, or
30

illegal. Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(3)(a). Substantial evidence is defined as “that quantum and
quality of relevant evidence that is adequate to convince a reasonable mind to support a
conclusion.” Bradley v. Payson City Corp., 2003 UT 16, ¶ 10. A determination of illegality
requires a determination that the decision violates a law, statute or ordinance at the time the
decision was made. Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(3)(d). “Because a determination of illegality is
based on the land use authority’s interpretation of zoning ordinances, [courts] review such
determinations for correctness, but we ‘also afford some level of non-binding deference to the
interpretation advanced by’ the land use authority.” Fox v. Park City, 2008 UT 85, ¶ 11 (quoting
Carrier v. Salt Lake County, 2004 UT 98, ¶ 17).
IV.

ARGUMENT
The Court must deny NMA’s Motion for the reason that NMA cannot meet its burden of

proving the County Commission’s decision was arbitrary and capricious. In fact, the record
supports the opposite conclusion—that the County Commission’s decision was well reasoned and
based on substantial evidence.
Notwithstanding binding decisions previously rendered by this Court and the County
Commission during the course of NMA’s successive appeals, NMA’s Complaint and Motion
attempt to relitigate claims and factual allegations as matters of first impression. Many of NMA’s
allegations are either outside the scope of this review, or have already been rejected by the County
Commission and this Court. Further, sPower disputes facts and legal conclusions put forward by
NMA. In this case there is a fundamental dispute between NMA and sPower as to (1) facts alleged
by NMA; (2) the “final decision” actually on appeal; (3) the appellate authority of the County
Commission; (4) the issues decided by the County Commission on remand; (5) the scope of the

31

District Court’s appellate review; (6) and, the District Court’s authority to provide the relief
requested by NMA.
A.

The County Commission’s Remand Decision issued on February 21, 2017,

33

McElhaney does not establish any bright line rule applicable to all land use decisions.
Rather, the scope McElhaney is limited to final land use decisions on appeal before the District
Court under Utah Code Ann. §§ 10-9a-801 and 17-27a-801. Under §17-27a-801, the District
Court may only review a “final decision” that is brought after administrative remedies have been
exhausted. A “final decision” is a written decision issued by a county appeal authority designated
under §17-27a-707. The McElhaney Court determined that the “[a] final decision of a land use
authority or an appeal authority is valid if the decision is supported by substantial evidence in the
record and is not arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.” 1
The McElhaney case largely turns on the fact that the Moab City Council decision to deny
McElhaney’s CUP was the only decision and the “final decision” reviewed by the District Court.
In McElhaney, the “final decision” consisted of council members’ “yay” or “nay” votes announced
at the conclusion of a public hearing. Municipal Code implemented by Moab City does not
establish a municipal appeal authority. As the Court is likely aware, Moab’s zoning ordinance
identifies the City Council’s decision on the CUP as the City’s “final decision” that “may be
appeal[ed] to the Grand County District Court.” MOAB, UTAH, MUN. CODE §17.09.530(M)(1).
After being denied a CUP by a 3 to 1 vote, McElhaney did not have the ability to appeal the City
Council’s decision to a municipal appeal authority. Unlike the Petitioners in this case, McElhaney
did not have the benefit of further review or fact-finding under a municipal appeal process that
must be exhausted before seeking remedies from the Court.

In dismissing the petitioner’s action, the Utah Physicians Court held that it is statutorily
bound to review the UDEQ’s final order, not the initial permit decision issued by UDAQ. The

“The law of the case doctrine encompasses several different principles related to the
binding effect of a decision on subsequent proceedings in the same case.” IHC Health Services,
Inc. v. D & K Management, Inc., 2008 UT 73, ¶26, 196 P.3d 588. “Simply stated, under the law of
the case doctrine, a decision made on an issue during one stage of a case is binding in successive
stages of the same litigation.” Id. There are three “exceptional circumstances in which the law of
the case doctrine does not apply: (1) when there has been an intervening change of controlling
authority; (2) when new evidence has become available; or (3) when the court is convinced that its
prior decision was clearly erroneous and would work a manifest injustice.” Id. at ¶34.
Here, the County Commission was not required to examine NMA’s claims that (1) that the
conditional use permit included unarticulated “self-imposed conditions;” (2) that sPower was
required to purchase NMA property at 360% market value based on such conditions; (3) that
substantial evidence did not demonstrate sPower’s CUP compliance; and (4) that the Planning
Commission’s decision to uphold sPower’s CUP was arbitrary or capricious.

In issuing its Remand Decision, the County Commission was not required to reconsider its

limits their access or use of their property in the winter. sPower is not required to mitigate for
unidentified ice-throw risks to vacant property under the CUP.

C.

The County Commission’s Remand Decision does not violate due
process rights protected under the Fourteenth Amendment.

The Fourteenth Amendment of the United States Constitution provides that no state shall
deprive any person of property without due process of law. 3 See U.S. Const. amend. XIV, sec.
1. While the Constitution guarantees due process before the deprivation of property interests,
such interests are not created by the Constitution. Rather, property interests “are created and
their dimensions are defined by existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent

source such as state law.” Board of Regents v. Roth, 408 U.S. 564, 577, 92 S.Ct. 2701, 2709, 33
L.Ed.2d 548 (1972).
Generally, the Utah Supreme Court has held that local land use decisions do not trigger a
14th Amendment Claim under 42 U.S.C. §1983. Patterson v. American Fork City, 2003 UT 7,
¶25, 67 P.3d 466 (“absent invidious discrimination, such as proof of racial animus, the
‘conventional planning dispute is a matter primarily of concern to the state and does not
implicate the Constitution.”); see also Crown Point I, LLC v. Intermountain Rural Elec. Ass'n,
319 F.3d 1211, 1217 (10th Cir. 2003) (“[W]here the governing body retains discretion and the
outcome of the proceeding is not determined by the particular procedure at issue, no property
interest is implicated.”). Under the due process clause of the 14th amendment, “to state a
cognizable substantive due process claim, plaintiffs must first allege sufficient facts to show a
property or liberty interest warranting due process protection. Patterson, 2003 UT 7, ¶23, 67
P.3d 466. In order to have a valid property interest in a state-created right, a plaintiff must have
more than “an abstract need for or a unilateral expectation of a benefit.” Heideman v.
Washington City, 2007 UT App 11, ¶17, 155 P.3d 900. Instead, the plaintiffs must have a
“legitimate claim of entitlement to it.” Patterson, 2003 UT at ¶23.

Here, NMA’s 14th

Amendment claim is wholly unfounded.
NMA has not demonstrated that is has a protectable property interest within scope of 14th
Amendment protection and has not demonstrated that it has been deprived of such an interest by
the County Commission’s final decision. Citing to United States v. James Daniel Good Real
Prop., NMA argues that its members have a “property interest in the unrestricted use and
enjoyment their property.” 510 U.S. 43, 54 (1993). But the court in James¸ referred to the

“unrestricted use and enjoyment” of an individual’s home. James does not stand for the idea that
real property ownership conveys a right of “unrestricted use and enjoyment.”

NMA has

previously claimed that its members intended to subdivide their property and develop the area for
residential use. But, NMA properties are zoned for Agricultural A-1 and are undeveloped.
Development contemplated by NMA requires that its members obtain a CUP. None of NMA’s
members applied to subdivide their property. Moreover, none of NMA’s members have applied
for any kind of building permit. NMA’s property remains uninhabitable, undeveloped land that
is not zoned for residential subdivisions. NMA’s future hope to develop such a property is not a
protectable property interest under the Fourteenth Amendment. See Crown Point I, LLC v.
Intermountain Rural Elec. Ass’n, 319 F.3d 1211, 1216 (10th Cir. 2003) (holding that a
developer’s expectation to prevail before a land use authority and block transmission line
construction through his property was not a “constitutionally protected interest”)
Further, NMA’s procedural due process right to participate in a land use hearing is not
protected under the 14th Amendment. See id. (“State procedural failure alone does not create a
violation of constitutional proportions.”); Jacobs, Visconsi & Jacobs, Co. v City of Lawrence
Kan., 927 F.2d 1111, (10th Cir. 1991) (“The Supreme Court has recognized that the mere
existence of an entitlement to hearing under state law without further substantive limitations,
does not give rise to an independent substantive liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth
Amendment.”). Even if, however, NMA had such an interest, it has not demonstrated how the
County’s compliance determination constitutes property deprivation. There is no evidence that
NMA members cannot, with necessary approvals, develop their property for residential use. The
Planning Commission may issue a building permit or CUP to NMA at its discretion. Further,

because there has been no development on the property, NMA members current use and
enjoyment has not been impacted by the County Commission’s decision to uphold sPower’s
CUP.
Case law cited by NMA demonstrates that its due process rights have been met. “Due
process requires, at a minimum, adequate notice and an opportunity to be heard in a meaningful
manner.” Salt Lake City Corp. v. Jordan River Restoration Network, 2012 UT 84, ¶111, 299
P.3d 990 (internal citations omitted).

“The type of hearing required depends on the

circumstances of each case.” Id. “Typically, a hearing must provide parties the opportunity to
present ‘evidence, objects and arguments to the end that the court may be enabled to fairly and
intelligently pass upon and determine the questions presented for decision.” Id. Such a hearing is
not always required, however, as the San Juan County Code recognizes.
Here, proceedings before the County Commission have satisfied NMA’s procedural due
process right. NMA has been given proper notice of all proceedings. Further, NMA has been
given multiple opportunities to be heard in a meaningful manner before the County Commission,
and before this very Court. NMA has been given the opportunity to present evidence, testimony
and argument before the County Commission. NMA cannot, therefore, argue that it has been
deprived of any procedural due process right.
D.

The Commission’s Remand Decision was procedurally proper and lawful.

NMA’s claim that the Commission’s Remand Decision is procedurally improper and
illegal has already been litigated before this court and is barred under the law of the case
doctrine.

“The law of the case doctrine encompasses several different principles related to the
binding effect of a decision on subsequent proceedings in the same case.” IHC Health Services,
Inc. v. D & K Management, Inc., 2008 UT 73, ¶26, 196 P.3d 588. “Simply stated, under the law
of the case doctrine, a decision made on an issue during one stage of a case is binding in
successive stages of the same litigation.” Id. There are three “exceptional circumstances in
which the law of the case doctrine does not apply: (1) when there has been an intervening change
of controlling authority; (2) when new evidence has become available; or (3) when the court is
convinced that its prior decision was clearly erroneous and would work a manifest injustice.” Id.
at ¶34.
Here, NMA claims that San Juan County’s Amended Written Decision is illegal because
the County Commission does not have the authority to reconsider and amend its decision under
Utah Code Ann 17-27a and Sections 2-3 and 6-7 of the San Juan County Zoning Ordinance.
This claim is identical to NMA’s claim that was heard and decided by this Court. Previously, on
February 10, 2016, NMA Filed an Amended Complaint with this Court (Case No. 160700001)
claiming that the San Juan County Commission did not have the authority to amend or
reconsider its “Final Written Decision” and issue an Amended Decision. This Court rejected
NMA’s claim and held that the County Commission’s authority to reconsider a decision is
“inherent in the power to make an administrative decision.” Memorandum of decision page 7.
Further, NMA’s claim presents none of the exceptional circumstances in which the law of
the case doctrine does not apply. There has been no intervening change in the controlling
authority. NMA has not presented any new evidence. NMA does not make any legal argument
or factual allegation that would suggest this court’s prior decision was clearly erroneous. This

Court is therefore bound by its prior decision holding that the County Commission has the
internet authority to “fix its own mistakes.” Id.

CONCLUSION
The Court must deny NMA’s Motion for Summary Judgment. NMA’s purported facts

are merely allegations in their appellate briefs and other filings that are directly contradicted by
evidence contained in the administrative record. The County Commission erred in issuing its
Written Decision of December 2, 2016. The record demonstrates that the County Commission’s
decision to grant sPower’s request for reconsideration and to issue an amended decision is
supported by substation evidence. sPower complied with all conditions imposed under the CUP.
It selected a quieter wind turbine with a shorter hub height to minimize shadow flicker, light and
sound impacts to adjacent residents and businesses and the City of Monticello. sPower
implemented substation lighting plans to minimize light pollution generated on site. Where
sPower could not mitigate all impacts of light, sound and flicker to existing residences and
businesses, sPower provided financial mitigation above and beyond that which was required
under the CUP. Further, sPower offered, although not required to do so, to provide financial
mitigation to offset impacts to speculative residential development on NMA property in the
future, or to compensate NMA for FMV property loss based on a certified appraisal.
The County Commission’s final decision is supported by substantial evidence and is not
arbitrary or capricious. Moreover, the County Commission’s decision satisfies the standard set
forth in McElhaney v. City of Moab, 2017 UT 65. It includes findings of fact and conclusions of
law that provide for meaningful appellate review. Further, the County Commission’s decision
was issued pursuant to proceedings in which NMA was afforded full due process.
