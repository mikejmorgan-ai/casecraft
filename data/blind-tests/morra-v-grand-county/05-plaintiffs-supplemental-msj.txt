ARGUMENT

This Court should determine that the Council’s land use decision adopting Ordinance
454 on May 8, 2007, was administrative and remand Citizens’ appeal to the Board. As noted in
other similar cases, “[t]his is not a case in which we are attempting to second-guess the County’s
lawful exercise of legislatively delegated discretion.” Sandy City v. Salt Lake County, 827 P.2d
212, 218 (Utah 1992). Rather, this case concerns the Council’s administrative land use decisions
for a specific project and interpretation of the appeal provisions of CLUDMA. See Utah Code
Ann. § 17-27a-801. The determination of whether Ordinance 454 was administrative or
legislative addresses the threshold issue concerning the jurisdiction of this Court and also
identifies the standard of review that must be applied to the Council’s land use decision. Because
the Council’s action was administrative, this Court should to direct that Citizens’ appeal first be
heard by the Board. If, arguendo, this Court nevertheless determines that it has jurisdiction to
hear Citizens’ claims, this Court should determine that the Council’s decision to approve
Ordinance 454 was illegal.

I. Procedural Posture and Standards of Review.

As there is no precise procedure for “on the record appeals” at the district court level,
summary judgment is the common tool utilized to bring CLUDMA appeals to the Court’s
attention for ruling. However, unlike the typical motion for summary judgment, this Court’s
decision is based on the record created at the County level and not on undisputed material facts.
The recitation of facts by Citizens is simply to direct this Court’s attention to the facts found in
the Record.

arbitrary, capricious, or illegal. See Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(3)(a)(ii). While courts have
interpreted arbitrary and capricious to mean “supported by substantial evidence” for appeals of
administrative decisions and “reasonably debatable” for appeals of legislative decisions, see

Il. The County Council’s Approval of Ordinance 454 Was An Administrative Act.

A. Ordinance 454 was Not a “Land Use Ordinance.”

CLUDMA distinguishes between “land use ordinances” and other land use decisions
which may be implemented by ordinance. A “land use ordinance” is defined as “a planning,
zoning, development, or subdivision ordinance of the county,” Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-
103(19). A “land use ordinance” has broad application and regulates “the use and development
of land within all or any part of the unincorporated area of the county.” Jd. at § 17-27a-
502(1)(c). As more fully described below, the adoption of Ordinance 454 consisted of a series of
administrative decisions, each of which were very limited in scope, regarding the changes
Cloudrock proposed to its existing development approvals and thus does not meet the
requirements of a “land use ordinance.”

1. Land Use Decisions May be Legislative or Administrative.

Land use decisions range from legislative to administrative. As the Utah Supreme Court

4852-6758-6822.MO006.001 2

has observed, “[z]oning changes fall on a continuum between major changes in zoning law, such
as the original enactment of zoning ordinances, at one end of the spectrum and simple
implementations of zoning plans, such as exceptions and variances, at the other.” Citizen's
Awareness Now v. Marakis, 873 P.2d 1117, 1122 (Utah 1994). The substance of the land use
action is critical to determine whether the action is legislative or administrative.

2. The Substance of a Land Use Decision Trumps its Form.

Utah courts have held that “[t]he formal label of a city council’s action is not
determinative; courts must look to the substance of the city council’s action to determine if it is
legislative or administrative.” Low v. Monticello, 2002 UT 90, 24, 54 P.3d 1153. Further, “what
an action accomplishes determines if it is legislative or administrative in nature.” Citizens for
Responsible Trans. v. Draper City, 2008 UT 43, 12, 190 P.3d 1245. This rule arose in the
context of referendum cases, where the distinction between legislative and administrative land
use decisions determines whether such decision may be subject to voter approval. See id. That
same rule, however, is no less applicable to this case.

Unlike the state and federal levels, where there is a true separation of powers between the
legislative and executive branches, local governing bodies, such as the Council, have and
exercise both legislative, administrative, and quasi-judicial powers powers. See Bradley, 2003
UT 16 at 910. Therefore, if form, i.e., passage of an ordinance to effect its administrative land
use decisions, trumped substance, every county or municipality could obtain the much more

deferential “reasonably debatable” standard of review for what is in reality an administrative

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 3

decision by simply adopting an ordinance to implement its administrative actions.” Such homage
to form would eviscerate the legislative/administrative distinction in CLUDMA and numerous
opinions of this and the Utah Supreme Court. See e.g., id. at 913, 14 (discussing the distinction
between legislative and administrative land use decisions).

When reviewing a land use decision, a district court must “determine only whether or not
the decision, ordinance, or regulation is arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.” Utah Code Ann. § 17-
27a-801(3)(a)(ii). Land use decisions, ordinances, or regulations “involving the exercise of
legislative discretion [are] valid if it is reasonably debatable that the decision, ordinance, or
regulation promotes the purposes of [CLUDMA] and is not otherwise illegal.” Jd. at § 17-27a-
801(3)(b). This provision of CLUDMA explicitly acknowledges that not all ordinances involve
the exercise of legislative discretion.® CLUDMA further explains that other land use decisions,
ordinances, or regulations are valid if “supported by substantial evidence in the record.” Id. at §
17-27a-801(3)(c). Thus, the substance of Ordinance 454, not its label as an ordinance will
determine whether it is administrative or legislative.

4. The Council’s Discretion in Approving a PUD Application is Limited.

It is Citizens’ position that Cloudrock’s application does not constitute a PUD

amendment. However, even if, arguendo, the Council’s actions are considered to be an

amendment to the PUD zone, its actions were still administrative. The Utah Court of Appeals

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 11

has held that “in cases deciding whether PUD approval is legislative or administrative, the
distinction is based upon the extent to which a municipality’s PUD enabling ordinance limits the
municipality’s discretion over PUD approval.” Harmon City, Inc. v. Draper City, 2000 UT App
31, 23, 997 P.2d 321. Thus, land use decisions to amend a PUD are administrative if the PUD
enabling ordinance limits the municipality’s discretion.

In the County, the underlying zone district of a PUD remains, limiting and controlling
not only density but also the uses that are allowed within the PUD. See Applicable LUC, Art.
III.R.4.and 5. [R. at GC00068.] Thus, the amendment of a PUD in Grand County does not result
in a rezone or the creation of a new zoning classification, but rather modifies the existing
underlying zone district to “provide flexibility to accommodate mixed-use, non-traditional and
innovative developments . . . and the preservation of natural and scenic qualities of open spaces”
subject to limitations and constraints. Applicable LUC, Art. ITL.R.1. [Jd. at GC00066]

The Council’s discretion to approve a PUD is limited by the Applicable LUC in the
following ways. First, the Council must receive a recommendation from the Planning
Commission, give notice and hold a public hearing before it can “authorize the creation of the
Planned Unit Development Districts.” Applicable LUC, Art. III.R.2. [R. at GC00067.] Second,
if the County requires an architectural and landscaping plan, that plan must include requirements
for “mitigating the visual impacts of the development.” Applicable LUC, Art. IIIL.R.2.a. [Jd.]
Third, the Applicable LUC requires the Council to make three specific findings before a PUD
may be approved. Applicable LUC, Art. III-R.2.b. [Jd.] Fourth, in order to achieve the purpose
and intent of the PUD, “variation may be permittéd with respect to the minimum lot area,

setbacks, lot width, and height[, but] [t]he maximum density shall be no greater than that

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 12

permitted in the underlying zone district prior to PUD approval.” Applicable LUC, Art. III.R.4.
[Id. at GC00068.] Fifth, the Council’s discretion concerning “conditional, use-on-review,
permitted, accessory and temporary uses” within a PUD is also is limited by the uses allowed in
“the underlying zoning district.” Applicable LUC, Art. III-R.5. [/d.] Finally, the Applicable LUC
limits the Council’s discretion by requiring a minimum amount of open space to be set aside in
each PUD. Applicable LUC, Art. IILR.6. [/d.] Therefore, under Harmon City, the Council’s
decision to enact Ordinance 454, even if it is considered a PUD amendment, was administrative,
not legislative because the Applicable LUC places significant limitations on the Council’s
discretion.’

Ill. The County’s Approval Of Ordinance 454 Was Illegal Because The County
Failed To Follow Its Ordinances.

Pursuant to Utah law, cities and counties “must strictly comply with the statute delegating
them the authority to act. . . . Consequently, ‘[flailure to strictly follow the statutory
requirements in enacting the ordinance renders it invalid.’” Hatch v. Boulder Town Council,
2001 UT App 55, 47, 21 P.3d 245 (quoting Call v. City of West Jordan, 727 P.2d 180, 182 (Utah
1986) (further citation omitted)). Because substantial evidence in the Record demonstrates that
the County failed to strictly comply with the Applicable LUC, its approval of Ordinance 454

was illegal.

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 14

A, The County’s Failure to Comply with the Applicable LUC Invalidates the
Adoption of Ordinance 454.

In Springville Citizens, the Utah Supreme Court, treating the approval of a PUD
application as an administrative decision, held that “[m]Junicipal zoning authorities are bound by
the terms and standards of applicable zoning ordinances and are not at liberty to make land use
decisions in derogation thereof.” Springville Citizens for a Better Cmty. v. Springville, 1999 UT
25, 930, 979 P.2d 332. “Application of the substantial compliance doctrine where the ordinances
at issue are explicitly mandatory contravenes the unmistakable intent of those ordinances.” Jd. at
429. Counties are “not entitled to disregard [their] mandatory ordinances.” Jd. at 430.

1. Utah Law Requires Strict Compliance.

Utah courts have held that a municipality must strictly comply with statutory
requirements when enacting a zoning ordinance or the enactment is invalid. See Hatch, 2001 UT
App 55 at §7. The Hatch decision did not require that the appellants show prejudice in order to
prevail, but instead relied upon the holding of an earlier decision of the Utah Supreme Court. See
id. at 12 (citing Call v. City of West Jordan, 727 P.2d 180, 183 (Utah 1986)) In Call, an
inadequately noticed public hearing on a zoning ordinance failed “to strictly follow the statutory
requirement,” making the ordinance “invalid and void ab initio.” Call, 727 P.2d at 183. Because
the undisputed facts demonstrate that the County failed to strictly comply with its ordinances
and statutory requirements, this Court should determine that Council’s decision to approve
Ordinance 454 was illegal.

2. The Council’s Decision was Illegal Because the 2002 Approval of Cloudrock’s
Preliminary Plat had Lapsed.

Since Cloudrock’s Original Preliminary Plat had expired, the Council could not legally
approve the amendments to the expired plat proposed by Cloudrock. In adopting Ordinance 454,
the Council attempted to amend an invalid plat and thus acted illegally. In accordance with the
Applicable LUC, the Council should have required Cloudrock to go through the process outlined
in its own ordinances for submitting an application for a new preliminary plat.

3. Ordinance 454 is Illegal Because the Approvals Granted Therein Do Not Comply
with the Grand County General Plan

Compliance with the Grand County General Plan (the “General Plan”) is a fundamental

requirement for the approval of various land use applications under the Applicable LUC,

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 17

including a use-on-review, Applicable LUC, Art. VI.L.6.f, [R. at GC00127, Exh. 3.], a PUD,
Applicable LUC, Art. IILR.2.b, [id. at GC00067], and a zoning map amendment Applicable
LUC, Art. VI.B.5.e, [id. at GC00104]. Cloudrock’s Application included changes to its use-on-
review “wilderness lodge.” [R. at GC00603, Exh. 75.] Therefore, the Planning Commission and
the Council were required to determine whether Cloudrock’s Application was consistent with
the General Plan as part of the approval process.

Under both the Original and Amended Development Agreements, the County and
Cloudrock agreed that the Development would comply with the 1996 General Plan. [R. at
GC00286, Exh. 19; GC00627, Exh. 77.] As the 1996 Public Facilities Analysis is part of the
General Plan [GC00858, Exh.82], any use-on-review, PUD, or zoning amendment application
must also be consistent with the Public Facilities Analysis. The County’s build out projections
contained in the 1996 General Plan assumed no commercial or other non-residential land use
within state-owned land, including lands owned by the School and Institutional Trust Lands
Administration (“SITLA”). [See Public Facilities Analysis at p. 30, Tab A.] Yet, Cloudrock’s
Application clearly contemplates significant commercial and other non-residential development
on land which is currently owned by SITLA. [R. at GC00505-003, Exh. 65.] Thus, as Lee Nellis,
the County’s consulting planner for the 1996 General Plan, noted, the County could not legally
approve any amendment to Cloudrock’s Development until the 1996 Public Facilities Analysis
was updated to include a recalculation of the proposed build out of the area and related impact
fees. [GC00258-042, Exh. 16.] This never occurred.

In addition, the Zoning Map Amendment Guidelines (the “Guidelines”) of the 1996

General Plan consist of a checklist of questions to be answered by the County in reviewing a

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 18

land use application. [GC00864-8868, Exh. 82.] Among other things, the Guidelines provide
that (a) consideration is to be given as to whether development at the maximum permitted
intensity will affect water quality protection, (b) zoning map amendments within the recharge
area for an existing public water supply are to be reviewed by the water supplier, and (c)
regarding compatibility with surrounding lands and uses, specific consideration is to be given to
whether proposed commercial uses will have their “principal access through a predominantly
residential area.” [Jd.] These guidelines were not followed.

Based on the foregoing, it is evident that Cloudrock’s Application is inconsistent with the
1996 General Plan and Public Facilities Analysis with regard to build out projections and related
impact fees. In addition, the County planning staff reports prepared in connection with
Cloudrock’s Application contain no evidence that the County reviewed Cloudrock’s Application
against the Zoning Map Amendment Guidelines. [R. at Exh. 34; Exh. 36; Exh. 44; and Exh. 65.]
Thus, the Record is absent of any evidence that the Council considered and ensured that
Cloudrock’s Development was consistent with the 1996 General Plan as required by the
Applicable LUC prior to adopting Ordinance 454, rendering it illegal.!?

B. The Council Failed to Comply with the Applicable LUC Requirements
Governing PUD Applications.

If, against Citizens’ objections, this Court concludes that in reality the PUD was
amended by the adoption of Ordinance 454, then even more illegalities arise. If Ordinance 454
was a PUD amendment, it was illegal because the Council failed to strictly comply with its own

requirements for PUD amendments, making Ordinance 454 void ab initio.

3 The foregoing analysis properly calls into question the basis and veracity of the statement in Ordinance 454 that
Cloudrock’s Application and the Amended Development Agreement were consistent and in conformity with the
General Plan and zoning provisions of the Applicable LUC. [R. at 603, Exh. 75.]

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 19

1. Cloudrock’s Application was Incomplete and Should have been Rejected.

Under the Applicable LUC, approval of a PUD or amendment to a PUD and approval of
zoning amendments share similar substantive and procedural requirements, including
compliance with the General Plan. See Applicable LUC, Art. III.R.3; id, Art. VIB. [R.
GC00067.] However, the County, the Council, and Cloudrock failed to adhere to these
requirements with regard to approval of Cloudrock’s Application, if it is considered to be a PUD
amendment, making the approval thereof contrary to law (ie., illegal). Because Cloudrock’s
Application failed to comply with mandatory County land use code requirements, the Council
was required to reject the application. Its refusal to do so was illegal.

Cloudrock’s Application was incomplete because it was unaccompanied by a map, a title
report, a treasurer’s report regarding the status of taxes, a certified boundary survey, a list of
adjacent owners, or a statement of the applicant, all required for a zone change application under
the Applicable LUC. See Applicable LUC, Art. VI-B.2.a-.h. [R. at 102-103.] The Applicable
LUC makes it clear that such information, at a minimum, shall be included in all applications to
amend the zoning map. Applicable LUC, Art. VI.B.2. [Jd.] According to the Applicable LUC,
“It]he word ‘shall’ is always mandatory.” Applicable LUC, Art. II.A.8. [R. at GC00016.] Thus,
compliance with the requirements of Art. VI.B.2. “was, under the [County’s] own legislatively
enacted standard, mandatory.” Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25 at §26. The County is not
“entitled to disregard its mandatory ordinances.” Jd. at {30.

The County’s stated policy for such incomplete applications is that they “WILL NOT BE
ACCEPTED.” [R. at GC00315, Exh. 25.] If this Court concludes despite all of the evidence in

the record to the contrary, that Cloudrock’s Application constituted a PUD amendment, this

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 ; 20

Court must also conclude that Cloudrock’s Application was incomplete and was therefore
illegally approved by the Council.

2. The County and Council Failed to Strictly Comply with the Notice and Hearing
Requirements of the Applicable LUC.

PUD amendments also have notice and hearing requirements that were not met.
Assuming, arguendo, that Cloudrock’s Application to be a PUD amendment, the notices and
hearings held by the Planning Commission and the Council were illegal because they failed to
comply with the requirements of the Applicable LUC. Notice of a hearing on a PUD or zoning
amendment before the Planning Commission must “state the time and place of such hearing and
the nature of the subject to be considered.” Applicable LUC, Art. VI.B.3.a.(1)(a). [R. at
GC00103.] The same is required for hearings before the Council with the additional requirement
that posted notice must include “a map showing the land area proposed for rezoning.”
Applicable LUC, Art. VI.B.4.a(1)(a) and (b). [Jd. at 103-104.] CLUDMA further requires that
when a zoning map is amended the planning commission must first “prepare and recommend to
the legislative body a proposed land use ordinance or ordinances and zoning map.” Utah Code
Ann. § 17-27a-502(1)(c).

While the Planning Commission and County Council held public hearings on
Cloudrock’s “Amended Master Plan and Amended Preliminary Phase 1 Plat,” there is no
evidence in the Record, including the notice, agenda, or minutes of those hearings, to support a
finding that a zoning map amendment was considered at those hearings. The Notice of Public
Hearing for the December 13, 2006 Planning Commission requested “public comment on the
Johnson’s Up-on-Top Mesa Amended Master Plan and Amended Preliminary Phase 1 Plat,” not

a PUD zone change application. [R. at GC00325, Exh. 29.] The minutes of that meeting indicate

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 21

the same. [R. at GC00326, Exh. 30.] The notice published on January 25, 2007, called for a
public hearing to be held on February 6, 2007, on Cloudrock’s “Johnson Up-on-Top Mesa
Amended Master plan and Amended Preliminary Phase 1 Plat.” [R. at GC00357, Exh.35.] The
agenda for the February 6, 2007 public hearing identified the Cloudrock Application only as a
“Proposed Ordinance for an Amended Preliminary Plat Phase 1 for Johnson’s Up-on-Top Mesa
Amended PUD.” [R. at GC00374, Exh. 42.] The minutes of that meeting explain that the
application before the Council was “submitted by [Cloudrock] . . . to amend the Johnson’s Up-
On-Top Mesa Master Plan and Preliminary Phase I Plat.” [R. at GC00385, Exh. 45.] None of
these notices indicated to the public that Cloudrock’s Application was a PUD or zoning change
application. This failure to provide notice, as required by the Applicable LUC, rendered the
approval of Ordinance 454 illegal.

In addition, a proposed amended zoning map was not presented at those public hearings
as required by CLUDMA and applicable case law. See Utah Code Amn. §17-27a-503; see also
Hatch v. Boulder Town Council, 2001 UT App 55, 99, 21 P.3d 245. The Applicable LUC
requires the Planning Commission to prepare and recommend to the County Council a map,
“which accurately reflect[s] the text of its zoning ordinance.” Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-
502(1)(c); Hatch, 2001 UT App 55 at §13. There is no evidence in the Record to establish that
the Planning Commission fulfilled this requirement. “A map that does not illustrate the text of
the ordinance, accurately portraying all zoning districts, does not provide notice to the public of
the scope and intent of the proposed zoning ordinance.” Hatch, 2001 UT App 55 at 413.

Though Cloudrock maintains that Ordinance 454 constitutes a land use ordinance under

CLUDMA, the lack of a zoning map together with the other missing requirements mandated by

4852-6758-6822.M0006.001 22

the Applicable LUC indicate otherwise. Even the map submitted by Cloudrock as evidence of an
amendment to the County’s Zoning Map was created after Ordinance 454 had passed and is
incomplete and inaccurate. [R. at GC00814, Exh. 80.] In addition, the zoning map obtained by
Citizens from the County that was in existence at the time Ordinance 454 was adopted does not
show any approved PUDs. [R. at GC00815, Exh. 80.] In light of the foregoing, it is clear that
adoption of Ordinance 454 did not follow the mandatory requirements of CLUDMA and the
Applicable LUC for a zoning map amendment.

Counties are required to strictly comply with their own ordinances, and substantial
compliance is simply not good enough. See Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25 at 30. Since the
Council did not strictly comply with the Applicable LUC, Ordinance 454 not only failed to
effect a rezone, it was also illegal.

CONCLUSION

The facts of this case demonstrate that when the Council enacted Ordinance 454 on May
8, 2007, its action was administrative in nature, not legislative. Because its action was
administrative, the Board should have heard Citizens’ appeal and this Court should grant
Citizens’ motion for summary judgment. Even if this Court does not require that Citizens’ appeal
be heard by the Board, the facts contained in the Record demonstrate that the Council’s decision
to approve Ordinance 454 was arbitrary, capricious, and illegal. Therefore, Citizens respectfully
request that this Court grant their motion for summary judgment and declare that the Council

acted illegally in enacting Ordinance 454.

INTRODUCTION

The City of Moab and the Spanish Valley portion of Grand County are experiencing rapid growth.
This growth is expected to continue at a relatively rapid rate for the next few years. Increasing
demands will be placed on City and County facilities and services as growth continues.
Improvement of existing facilities and construction of new facilities will be required to meet this

I.

BUILD OUT PROJECTIONS

A.

INTRODUCTION

Build out projections forecast residential and non-residential growth within an area from
the. present time until all available land has been developed to the extent realistically
permitted by the terrain and local zoning regulations. This condition is described as "built
out". The purpose of such a projection is to help County and City council members as
well as other local decision makers understand the extent of the demand for public facilities
and services they must ultimately be able to provide.

Residential build out projections are measured by potential residential units which is
directly linked to the projected population of the area. Non-residential build out
projections are measured for each land use by potential square footage that can be
developed within the area. Square footage is a function of available acreage for
development. In the case of hotels and motels, the projections are measured by the
number of rentable rooms which, again, is a function of available acreage.

Build out projections are not time dependent. The time it will take a community to reach
build out will vary depending on many factors, not least of which are the inevitable
economic swings of a region. For this reason, this analysis does not attempt to predict
when build out will occur nor does it predict yearly growth rates,

For purposes of planning and budgeting for needed public facilities, it is advisable to make
short term projections (from three to five years). However, a community should not lock
in to such predictions, but instead should monitor its growth and the subsequent demands
on its public facilities constantly and make adjustments in its facility planning annually.

Public facility planning is a dynamic process which begins with an accurate assessment of
potential build out scenarios. The steps to develop accurate build out projections are:

1) Define the area of interest which generally is termed the "Area of Impact" or as
most commonly used in this report as the "Study Area;”

2) For residential projections, measure the number of existing residential units and
calculate the existing population; —

3) For non-residential projections other than hotels and motels, calculate the total
square footage of existing buildings; For hotels and motels, count the total number
of existing rooms; and

4) Estimate the residential and non-residential build out projections. This estimate is
based on a set of land use assumptions provided by the City and the County.

The following definitions are intended to assist in the understanding of the
methodology used in determining future dwelling units:

Vacant/Under-utilized Acres - The Vacant/Under-utilized Acres is the total non-
constrained area that is either vacant or has existing development that is anticipated
to be removed and redeveloped in the future in accordance with the existing
zoning. For example, a single family house on several acres of land fronting
Highway 191 that is designated as commercial zoning will likely eventually convert
to a commercial use. This land is considered underutilized and it is assumed that

it will be developed commercially. A Vacant and Under-Utilized Land map is
provided as Exhibit 5 on page 11.

Constrained Vacant Acres - Constraints are conditions existing on a piece of land
that inhibit the development of the property. Constraints include steep slopes,
easements, floodways, etc. A property can either be entirely or partially
constrained. It is assumed that there is no existing development on constrained
land. A Constraints Map is provided as Exhibit 6 on page 12.

Within the City of Moab, the identified constraints include steep slopes, major rock
outcroppings and the City's FC-1 Zone (the City’s flood control zone). Slopes
between 15 and 30 percent are assumed to have a development potential of 50 -

percent. Slopes greater than 30 percent are assumed to be. entirely constrained and
have no development potential.

In Spanish Valley, constraints include steep slopes, major rock outcroppings and
the 100 year flood plain boundary. Like Moab, 15 and 30 percent slopes are
assumed to have a development potential of 50 percent and those greater than 30
percent are entirely constrained.

Vacant Build Out Density Factor - This is the ratio of dwelling units per acre
anticipated to develop on the vacant/under-utilized lands. These density factors are
based on the underlying zoning and the actual densities that have been achieved by
existing’ developments within a particular zone. This factor will be different
depending on the jurisdiction, the underlying zoning and actual density
measurement of existing projects. The factors used will be discussed under the
description of the methodology and results of the analysis for each area affected.

Constrained Build Out Density Factor - This is the ratio of dwelling units per

acre that is anticipated for the future development of the partially constrained lands
described above.

CONCLUSION

This Public Facilities Analysis has provided Grand County, the City of Moab, and the Spanish
Valley Water and Sewer Improvement District with the information needed to ensure that land use
and public investment decisions are based on an accurate assessment of the current availability of,
and future demand for, public facilities and services. The findings of the report show that most
of the existing facilities analyzed meet current demand with the exception of library facilities,
police and sheriff officers, and water storage for the unincorporated areas of Spanish Valley, The
individual chapters of this report provide the details regarding the existing facilities, any facility
deficiencies and the future demand on facilities as well as cost estimates to meet existing and
future demands.

The impacts assumed by future development are based on the existing zoning designations and the
current census data available. Changes to the zoning designations could occur in the future to
reflect the desires of future residents or political decision makers. These changes may lead to
different impacts to public facilities. In addition, changing demographics may also result in
different impacts to public facilities. Development trends and demographic changes will continue
to evolve and shape the development and growth of the study area. To compensate for these
changes, the public facilities analysis should be updated on a regular basis to maintain the validity
of the report and to effectively plan for public facilities in the Spanish Valley area.

‘NEXT STEPS

The information presented in this report will help the Grand County and City of Moab decision
makers understand the projected extent of their community’s growth and anticipate the costs of
providing the public facilities needed to accommodate that growth. This report is a working
document that can be used as the basis to develop a capital improvement program and to meet the
requirements of a "Capital Facilities Plan” for the collection of impact fees. ,

The facilities planning process should continue with the preparation and adoption of capital
improvements programs for the County, the City, and the Spanish Valley Water and Sewer
Improvement District. The capital improvements program (CIP) adds a specific time dimension
to the analysis presented here by setting priorities for capital projects over a period of five or six
years. The CIP cam also set priorities beyond the foreseeable future based on anticipated facility
improvements needed, The CIP then links those proposed projects with specific funding sources
or strategies. For example, a CIP may dedicate the revenue from a certain funding source to a
project, direct the city or county administrator to apply for certain grant funds, call for the
creation of an improvement district, or point out the need for an impact fee.

After numerous public hearings and debate during the previous years, on May 8, 2007,
the Grand County Council convened and enacted Ordinance 454, by which the Council approved
the rezoning of real property for the amended Johnson’s Up-On-Top Preliminary PUD (“PUD”)
and the amended Development Agreement submitted by Cloudrock Development Company,
LLC (“Cloudrock”). Grand County Council adopted Ordinance 454 after considering the
concerns of various players, some of whom include Moab City, Grand County Special Service
Water District, School and Institutional Trust Land Administration (SITLA), Cloudrock, other
developers and concerned citizens. On or about May 24, 2007, counsel for a small contingent of
persons opposed to the Cloudrock PUD (“Contingent”) requested an appeal application for the
Grand County Board of Adjustment (“BOA”) from Mary Hofhine of the planning and zoning
office. On June 6, 2007, Contingent filed their appeal with the BOA. Cloudrock objected to the
appeal in a Memorandum dated June 22, 2007, addressed to the Grand County Attorney, based
on BOA’s lack of jurisdiction. Contingent defended the jurisdiction of their appeal in a
Memorandum of Opposition dated July 12, 2007. Cloudrock responded to the opposition in a
Reply Memorandum dated July 25, 2007. Contingent submitted a Sur Reply Memorandum

dated August 16, 2007.

Memoranda submitted by both Cloudrock and Contingent dealt with the disputed
jurisdiction of the BOA over the appeal, and whether application of Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-
801 (2007) barred the appeal. Review of the facts and memoranda of points and authorities
confirms that only the District Court has jurisdiction to hear the appeal. The plain language of
the statute requires the filing of a petition within 30 days in the District Court to review a
legislative act such as Ordinance 454. Case law interpreting the statute supports this position.
Bradley v. Payson City Corp., 2003 UT 16, § 23, 70 P.3d 47, 54 (Utah 2003). The records and
findings of the numerous public hearings and debate concerning the Cloudrock PUD support the
Council’s decision to enact Ordinance 454 in furtherance of the general welfare of all citizens of
Grand County, even though a small and vocal group of persons oppose the development. The
clear import of Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801 (5) is to limit the venue and time deadline for a
challenge to any legislative action taken by Grand County Council. The BOA has no authority
to overturn an ordinance enacted by the Grand County Council. The elected Grand County
Attorney represents both the BOA and the Council,’ and it is the opinion of the Grand County
Attorney that the BOA has no jurisdiction over the appeal filed by Contingent; the proper venue

is the District Court.

Title 16. Article 6.Decision-making Bodies and Officials
Sec. 6.2. Board of Adjustment

Sec. 6.2. Board of Adjustment _ .

6.2.1 Board of Adjustment Created
There is hereby created a board known as the Board of Adjustment.’

6.2.2 Appeal Authority

The Board of Adjustment is hereby designated as the appeal authority for purposes of performing the
powers and duties describe in this section.

6.2.3 Number and Appointment

The Board of Adjustment shall consist of five (5) members who shall be appointed by the County Council.
Not more than two (2) members may be current members of the Planning Commission. No member of
the County Council shall serve on the Board of Adjustment.

A. Term of Office

1. The term of office the Board of Adjustment shall be staggered so that two (2) members’
terms expire in each of two (2) successive years and remaining member's term expire in
the 3rd successive year. As the term of each member expires, the vacancy thus created
shall be filled by a majority vote of the County Council for a term of three (3) years.

2. All terms begin on February 1st and expire on January 31st of the third year following the
year of appointment. At the expiration of said term, including any terms expiring after the
adoption of this section, the successor shall be appointed by the County Council so as to
maintain the succession of staggered terms of service. All Board members shall continue
to serve as voting members of the Board until replaced by appointment of the County
Council.

3. Ifa vacancy occurs other than by expiration of term, the County Council by majority vote
shall appoint a new member to fill the unexpired term.

4. Commission members may be removed for cause from office by two-thirds (2/3) vote of the
County Council prior to the expiration of the appointed term.

B. Method of Appointment

1. In November of each year (early as possible), the Zoning Administrator shall cause notice
of appointment(s) to be given by publication in a newspaper of general circulation (with
distribution of the notice to all other local news media without requirement for publication)
in Grand Country. Such notice shall state the nature and term of the appointment(s) to be
made, the qualifications for such appointment, request written statements of interest and
qualifications, and establish a deadline for submittal of such statements, which time shall
not be earlier than 15 days from the date of publication.

2. Each appointee shall be chosen by the County Council from the list of two (2) persons
submitted by the Planning Commission. If the Planning Commission has not notified the
County Council of its recommendation by February 1st, the County Council may appoint
any qualified applicant(s). ,

C. Qualifications
Each Board of Adjustment member shall:

1. Bearesident and a registered voter of Grand County for at least two (2) years prior to
serving on the Commission;

2. Have a genuine interest in the planning and zoning of the County; and
3. Have the ability to work with the public and time to devote to this service.
D. Officers and Procedures
1. Members of the Board shall elect from their members a Chairman to serve for a term of
one year and may adopt such rules as may be necessary for the conduct of its business.

2. The Chairman shall preside over meetings. In the event, a question over procedures arise,
Robert's Rules of Order shall apply.
