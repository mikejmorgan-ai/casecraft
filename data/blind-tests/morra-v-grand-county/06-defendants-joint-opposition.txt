ARGUMENT
I. THE ENACTMENT OF ORDINANCE 454 WAS A LEGISLATIVE ACT

In the May 13, 2008 order granting summary judgment to Cloudrock, this court ruled that
“[t]he enactment of Ordinance 454 was a legislative act of the Grand County Council because it
approved an amendment to a planned unit development zone, which constituted a zoning
amendment.” Since May 13, 2008, two Utah Supreme Court opinions have confirmed that
ruling. Friends of Maple Mountain, Inc. v. Mapleton City, 2010 UT 11, 26, 228 P.3d 1238
(holding that “adoption of a new zoning classification is per se legislative action”); Save Beaver
County v. Beaver County, 2009 UT 8, § 20, 203 P.3d 937 (explaining that the labeling an
ordinance “legislative” estops a county from claiming otherwise later). In other words, legal
developments over the last two years provide further support for this court’s original ruling.

In Mapleton City, a city and developer entered into an agreement to settle a longstanding
dispute over the use of more than sixty acres of land known as the “donut hole.” 2010 UT 11,
42. Under that agreement, the city rezoned the donut hole to a “[s]ite specific zoning
designation entitled Planned Development-2” to allow denser development. Id. A group of
citizens sought to bring a referendum to challenge the rezoning as a legislative act. Id. 5. The
trial court refused to permit the referendum on the ground that the rezoning was “administrative
in nature and therefore not subject to referendum.” Id. The Utah Supreme Court reversed,

holding that rezoning the donut hole constituted the “adoption of a new zoning classification

11942148

[and] is per se legislative action.” Id. 26 (emphasis added).” Here, the enactment of Ordinance
454, which approved the PUD’s reclassifying more than 1,935 acres for zoning purposes, also is
a per se legislative act.

In Save Beaver County, a developer had obtained certain development approvals from
Beaver County in the form of a development agreement adopted by land use ordinance. 2009
UT 8,43. Some county residents argued that the ordinance was legislative and therefore subject
to referendum. Id. §§ 4, 6-7. The Utah Supreme Court agreed, holding that “Beaver County
clearly intended to act legislatively” when it used language in the ordinance stating that it was
engaging in “a legislative action.” Id. J 18. Moreover, Beaver County’s refusal to allow an
appeal to the board of adjustment for “lack of jurisdiction” because the ordinance was legislative
in nature confirmed that the county “had acted legislatively.” Id. 4 19. The court clarified that
the county’s labeling the ordinance as legislative was dispositive, and, therefore, only if a county
fails to “label its action either legislative or administrative, then a determination under the test we
set forth in Citizen’s Awareness Now v. Marakis, 873 P.2d 1117 (Utah 1994) would be
appropriate.” Id. § 20. That rule makes sense, as it requires a county to “choose its poison” in

labeling the nature of its decision. On the one had, a legislative decision is subject to referendum

function when passing a zoning or rezoning ordinance.”’).

Ordinance 454 as legislative subjected it to referendum, it also triggers a more deferential
standard of review by this court rather than the board of adjustments.

Beaver County and Mapleton City therefore provide two additional reasons to rule that
Ordinance 454 was a legislative act: (i) it rezones more than 1,935 acres of the development;

Despite the recent precedent confirming this court’s original ruling, Mr. Suarez contends
that Ordinance 454 is administrative because (i) the underlying lands remain part of the Range
and Grazing zoning district and (ii) only the 2007 Development Agreement states that the
council was acting legislatively, not Ordinance 454 itself. This court should reject both
contentions.

First, enactment of a PUD constitutes a rezoning of property under the county’s code
which identifies fourteen zoning districts, one of which is a PUD zoning district. See 2000 LUC
Art.IIIA. While a PUD zoning district is an overlay zoning district that is applied over a base
zoning district, that feature does not change the fact that it is still a zoning district that can only
be approved by the legislative body, the County Council. See 2000 LUC Art.III.R.3. The
county’s ordinances which describe this legislative process, combined with the County Council’s

declaration that it was “acting in its legislative capacity” in the 2007 Development Agreement,

11942148

confirm that the County Council believed it was acting legislatively in enacting Ordinance 454
and therefore the decision will be reviewed as a legislative act.

Second, the county’s choice to enact the PUD with an ordinance is sufficient to
demonstrate that it was exercising legislative discretion. The section of Land Use, Development,
and Management Act (““LUDMA”) that describes the review of legislative land use decisions
refers to a “decision, ordinance or regulation;” whereas, the section describing the review of an
administrative land use decision’ refers only to a “final decision of a land use authority,” not an
ordinance. Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(3) (2007).

In sum, the decision to enact Ordinance 454 is one where the county decided (i) to enact
an ordinance, not merely apply an existing ordinance; (ii) expressly stated that it was exercising
“legislative discretion;” and (iii) amended a zoning map to reflect the new boundaries for the

PUD zoning district.* Thus, enactment of Ordinance 454 was a legislative act.

II. THE COUNTY DID NOT ACT ILLEGALLY WHEN IT ENACTED
ORDINANCE 454

Mr. Suarez’s only argument challenging Ordinance 454 as a legislative act is that it is
illegal. Under LUDMA, a land use ordinance is illegal only if it does not comply with applicable
ordinances, statutes and laws. Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(3)(b) (2007). For illegality to
warrant reversal, however, one must show prejudice, or, “in other words, how, if at all, the
[county’s] decision would have been different and what relief, if any, they are entitled.”
Springville Citizens for a Better Cmty. V. City of Springville, 1999 UT 25, § 31, 993 P.2d 252.
The prejudice requirement ensures there is a substantial likelihood the county council would not
have enacted the ordinance before the council is required to go through the process a second
time. The reason for the prejudice requirement is obvious and compelling: were no showing of
prejudice necessary, any citizen could bog down local government by looking for the slightest
failure to follow “strictly” the procedural letter of every ordinance. The prejudice requirement
prevents government from violating its laws while protecting government from hyper-technical
challenges aimed only to slow action, not ensure compliance.

Here, Mr. Suarez ignores the prejudice requirement and urges this court instead to adopt a
“strict compliance” standard with regard to zoning ordinances. The two cases he cites in support
are inapposite. Call v. City of West Jordan, 727 P.2d 180, 182-83 (Utah 1986); Hatch v. Boulder
Town Council, 2001 UT App 55, § 10, 23 P.3d 245.

In Call, a city had imposed an impact fee without first having held a public hearing or

provided public notice of the new fee, as required by state law. 727 P.2d at 182-83. And in

11942148

11

Hatch, a town council had divided an entire town into nine different zoning districts without first
providing any map depicting the proposed changes. 2001 UT App 55, { 10. Both cases were
decided pre-LUDMA, which was adopted in 2005. Moreover, both cases involved
circumstances where the challengers were denied notice or an opportunity to be heard, core due
process concerns that taint the entire process and make it impossible to assess the effect on the
outcome. Here, Mr. Suarez knew, as did his fellow challengers, the location of the Cloudrock
project and that developer was seeking to make a less than routine change to the approvals it had
previously obtained. Mr. Suarez attended and argued against the passage of Ordinance 454 at
numerous public meetings held before its enactment. (See GC 00386, Ex. 49, GC 00349, Ex. 33;
GC 00532, Ex. 69.) Because no issues related to due process are present in this case, the
prejudice requirement governs and cannot be satisfied. For this reason alone, this court should
reject Mr. Suarez’s illegality arguments. In addition, each of the illegality arguments fails
regardless of prejudice.

C. The County Provided the Required Notices Before Enacting Ordinance 454
and Complied with the Applicable Map Requirements

Mr. Suarez links two complaints regarding notice and a map to challenge the legality of
Ordinance 454. First, he claims that “[n]Jone of [the public] notices indicated to the public that
Cloudrock’s Application was a PUD or zone change application.” This claim is directly refuted
by the very first County Council notice (provided more three months before the enactment of
Ordinance 454) which stated that public hearing item “P” for the February 6, 2007 meeting was
“Proposed Ordinance for an Amended Preliminary Plat Phase 1 for Johnson’s Up-on-Top Mesa

Amended PUD.” (GC 00374, Ex. 42.)

restated Johnson’s Up-On-Top Mesa Development Agreement that are on the agenda for

D. The Application Was Complete

Finally, Mr. Suarez argues that the county acted illegally when it accepted an allegedly
deficient application. Again, Mr. Suarez’s claim ignores the ample evidence in the record which
confirms that Cloudrock, or its predecessor, had submitted all of the documents required in order
to have Ordinance 454 enacted by the county and for the creation of the PUD zoning district over
the lands described in the 2007 Development Agreement.

Mr. Suarez claims Cloudrock’s application lacked a map, but in fact there was one. (GC
00775). He claims no title report, but the record discloses a title opinion offered by a Utah
attorney which references a title report disclosing SITLA’s ownership of the property. (GC
00164e, -164f.) While there is no report from the treasurer regarding the status of taxes for the
property, SITLA, as a state agency, and owner of the project’s land, does not pay property taxes.
Mr. Suarez also claims there is no certified boundary survey, or a list of adjacent owners. The

record, however, reveals that there was an updated survey provided before enactment of

11942148

Ordinance 454 (GC 00451) and that the submissions did include an identification of adjacent
owners. (GC 00164d.)

As the record confirms, Cloudrock provided many documents to accompany its
application. If these documents were truly deficient, it was for the county to complain, not Mr.
Suarez. The county’s decision to accept the application cannot now be challenged, particularly
after the enactment of Ordinance 454. If there was something that should have been submitted
and was not, Mr. Suarez should have raised that issue prior to enactment of the decision that is
being reviewed in this case. The record confirms that the applications were complete and that
the County Council correctly determined, in the recitals to Ordinance 454, that “the application,
including, and as supplemented by, the Agreement, are consistent and in conformity with the
General Plan and zoning provisions of the Code.” Mr. Suarez fails to show that this statement is
not true.

CONCLUSION

This court should reach the same conclusions it did in it prior May 13, 2008 order
granting summary judgment to Cloudrock. No relevant facts have changed and the law has
developed only to provide further support for this court’s ruling. In particular, two Utah
Supreme Court cases have confirmed that (i) zoning changes, like those in Ordinance 454, are
per se legislative and (ii) a local government’s characterization of its action as legislative, like
the County Council’s characterization of its act of enacting Ordinance 454 here, is afforded great

deference. In addition, Mr. Suarez cannot demonstrate any illegality, let alone the required

judgment to Cloudrock and uphold Ordinance 454.
