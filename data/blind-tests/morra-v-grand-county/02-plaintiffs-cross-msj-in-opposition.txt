A. Plaintiffs Are Entitled To a Declaratory Judgment and An Order
Remanding This Matter to the Grand County Board of Adjustment.

Plaintiffs ask this Court to remand this entire matter back to the Board of Adjustment so
that Plaintiffs may exercise their right to an administrative appeal before that body. Unlike a
tuling on the merits of this lawsuit, deciding whether this matter should be remanded to the
Board of Adjustment does not require review of the Record. The County’s denial of Plaintiffs’
appeal to the Board is, as further described herein, unlawful, inequitable, tainted by obvious
conflicts of interest, and contrary to the County’s own precedent in this very same matter.
Contrary to Cloudrock’s assertions, the gravamen of Plaintiffs’ appeal is not the legislative
creation of a new zone, but rather the blatant errors made by the County in administering its own
land use ordinances and Utah code. Thus an appeal to the Board is proper. Plaintiffs also
continue to assert that is was improper for the County Attorney, who was acting as counsel for
the party whose decision was being challenged (i.e. the County Council) to act as a self-
appointed gatekeeper and thwart the appeal without even letting the Board of Adjustment

consider its jurisdiction in a public forum. Furthermore, in 2002 the Board of Adjustment took

B. The Court Cannot Rule on the Merits of Plaintiffs’ Petition for Judicial
Review Without the Record Which the County Has Failed to Produce.

Cloudrock asks the Court to declare “checkmate” on the merits of Plaintiffs’ petition for
judicial review when all of the chess pieces are not even on the board yet. The Utah statutes are
clear: the County must produce and transmit to the Court the Record of the administrative
proceedings, and the Court’s review is “limited to the record provided” by the County, unless the
Record provided is incomplete. § 17-27a-801(8)(a). Despite Plaintiffs’ lawsuit being on file for
nearly 8 months, and despite repeated requests from the Plaintiffs, the County still has not
produced the Record in this matter as required by Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-801(7).” Thus, the
Record, which is by law the factual basis that this Court must rely upon, is not in place. Plaintiffs
are concerned, based on past experiences with the County in this matter, that the Record when
produced will be incomplete and require supplementation. In any event, to date the County has
not provided the Record, and has not indicated when it will do so.

the Record but to a binder of documents, many of which were created by Cloudrock or its

ARGUMENT

A. The Board of Adjustment Has Jurisdiction to Hear Plaintiffs’ Appeal and this
Matter should be Remanded to the Board of Adjustment.

This Court should remand this matter back to the Grand County Board of Adjustment.
Plaintiffs, who are required to exhaust their administrative remedies before seeking review in this
Court, attempted to do just that but were denied by the County. Cloudrock’s assertion that the
adoption of Ordinance 454 is a legislative matter which cannot be reviewed by the Board of
Adjustment is incorrect. Unlike Plaintiffs’ claim for judicial review under Utah Code Ann. § 17-
27a-801, which must be decided based upon the Record that is yet to be provided by the County,
Plaintiffs request for remand under Rule 65B and the declaratory judgment act is a threshold
issue which can and should be decided now.

1. Plaintiffs Are Entitled to Relief Under Utah’s Declaratory Judgment Act.

In accordance with the Declaratory Judgment Act, Title 78, Chapter 33, Part 2, Utah
Code Annotated 1953, as amended, this Court has the power to declare rights, status and other
legal relations:

“[a] person . . . whose rights, status or other legal relations are effected by a

statute {or] municipal ordinance . . . may have determined any question of

construction or validity arising under the . . . statute [or] ordinance . . . and obtain

a declaration of rights, status or other legal relations thereunder.”

Plaintiffs ask this Court to declare their right to a hearing before the Board of
Adjustment. The statutes and ordinances upon which Plaintiffs rely are set forth below, and

essentially provide that the Board of Adjustment is the “appeal authority” to which decisions

applying the County land use code may be appealed. The jurisdiction of the Board to hear

4834-38 18-1634MO006-001 33

Plaintiffs’ appeal was challenged by Cloudrock, and after an exchange of written materials, the
County Attorney rendered an opinion that the Board of Adjustment lacks jurisdiction to hear
Plaintiffs’ appeal. The Board itself, however, took no action on this issue. This was
inappropriate because the County Attorney, who acted as the gatekeeper to the Board, had an
obvious conflict of interest. She was acting as counsel to the party (the County) whose decision
was being challenged on appeal, and thus had an inherent bias in deciding whether the appeal
should go forward.

In addition, the County’s denial of Plaintiffs’ appeal is in direct contrast to the similar 2002
appeal on this same development by a group of concerned citizens, where the Board of
Adjustment held a hearing to determine whether it had jurisdiction and concluded that it did.
Moreover, the Board of Adjustment apparently was misinformed by County staff to the effect
that Plaintiffs did not want to go forward with an appeal to the Board. For all of these reasons,
Plaintiffs are entitled to relief under Utah Code Ann. § 78-33-8 of the Declaratory Judgment Act
and an Order that (a) declares that the Board of Adjustment has jurisdiction to hear Plaintiffs’
appeal; and (b) orders the Board to hear Plaintiffs’ appeal forthwith, and stays this action

pending that appeal.

2. Plaintiffs Are Entitled Extraordinary Relief Under Utah Rule of Civil Procedure
65B(d)(2) (“Rule 65B”).

The Utah Rules of Civil Procedure expressly allow relief in the circumstances presented
here, where an administrative body has refused Plaintiffs enjoyment of a right, namely to a

hearing before the Board of Adjustment, to which they are entitled. Rule 65B provides:

4834-3818-1634MO006-001 34

“(appropriate relief may be granted: .. . (B) where an .. . inferior court,

administrative agency, corporation or person . . . has failed to perform an act

required by law as a duty of office, . . . [or] (C) where an... inferior court,
administrative agency, corporation, or person... has refused the petitioner the

use or enjoyment of a right .. . to which the petitioner is entitled; ....”

Under Section 6.2.2 of the Grand County Land Use Code (Jan. 2007) (the “2007 Land Use
Code”),

“(t]he Board of Adjustment is... designated as the appeal authority for purposes

of performing the powers and duties described in [Section 6.2 of the 2007 Land

Use Code].”

Section 6.2.4 of the 2007 Land Use Code provides that the Board of Adjustment shall have the
powers:

“(t]o hear and decide appeals where it is alleged there is error in any ... decision

or determination made by an administrative board or official in the enforcement

of this [2007 Land Use Code] pursuant to .. . Title 17-27(sic)-704 Utah

Annotated Code, 1953, as amended.”

As described more fully below, the adoption of Ordinance 454 was an administrative
decision of the County Council which is subject to review by the Board of Adjustment. When the
jurisdiction of the Board of Adjustment was challenged by Cloudrock, the County Attorney,
representing both the County and the Board of Adjustment, rendered an opinion that the Board
lacked jurisdiction to hear Plaintiffs’ appeal. As a result, the Board effectively refused to hear
Plaintiffs’ appeal, a right to which Plaintiffs are entitled under the 2007 Land Use Code as
described above. Plaintiffs are entitled to extraordinary relief under Rule 65B for the failure of

the Board to perform an act required by law and for refusing Plaintiffs the use of a right to which

they are entitled.

3. Ordinance 454 was a Decision of a “Land Use Authority” which is Properly Subject
to Review by an “Appeal Authority” such as the Board of Adjustment.

Under Utah law and the Applicable Land Use Code, the County Council is a “land use
authority” (as that phrase is defined in Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-103(18)) and is authorized to
approve, among other things, an application for, and amendments to, a planned unit development
(a “PUD”), a use-on-review permit, a special exception for road and design standards, and a
preliminary plat amendment. See Art. III-R.2, Art. VI.L.5, Art. VI.Q.2.a and Art. VI.D.5 of
Exhibit H to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. All of these decisions involve administering or
applying County land use ordinances. When acting in this capacity, the County Council is acting
as an “administrative board,” and its decisions are subject to review by the Board of Adjustment.

Under LUDMA, counties are required to establish one or more “appeal authorities” (as
defined in Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-103(2)) to hear and decide appeals from decisions applying
land use ordinances. See Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-701(1). LUDMA further provides that “any
person adversely affected by [a] land use authority's decision administering or interpreting a land
use ordinance” may appeal that decision to the appeal authority. See Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-
703. Under §§2.2 and 6.2.2 of the 2007 Land Use Code, the Board of Adjustment is designated
as the appeal authority for an appeal of a land use application. In adopting Ordinance 454, the
County Council not only approved proposed changes to a PUD, it also made land use decisions
regarding (a) changes to a use-on-review, (b) special exceptions, and (c) changes to a preliminary

plat and preliminary master plan, all of which involved “administrating or interpreting a land use

4834-3818-1634IMO006-001 36


ordinance” with respect to the Cloudrock Development. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ appeal to the
Board of Adjustment should have been heard.

Cloudrock argues that Ordinance 454 is a “land use ordinance” under LUDMA and as
such an appeal of Ordinance 454 can only be filed in district court. This argument is without
merit. First, labeling a decision of the County Council an “ordinance” does not make that
decision legislative. The Utah Supreme Court has held that the formal label of a governmental
body’s action is not determinative and courts must look to the substance of that body’s action to
determine if it is legislative or administrative. See Low v. City of Montecello, 54 P.3d 1153,
1160 (UT 2002). “In general, to be legislative, an ordinance must make a new law; to be

administrative, an ordinance must execute or implement an existing law. . . .” Id. See also

Citizen's Awareness Now v. Marakis, 873 P.2d 1117, 1122 (Utah 1994). It is clear that the
County Council in this instance was merely executing and implementing the County’s existing
zoning laws.

Second, LUDMA provides that “no person may challenge in district court a county's land
use decision . . . until that person has exhausted the person's administrative remedies as provided
in Part 7 [of this chapter].” See Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801. Thus, LUDMA contemplates that
some land use decisions are indeed administrative acts for which administrative remedies are
available. The fact that Ordinance 454 may satisfy LUDMA’s definition of a “land use
ordinance” is not determinative of whether that ordinance is a legislative or administrative

decision.

new law or policy or was otherwise an exercise of the County Council’s legislative powers.
Cloudrock offers no legal authority to support a finding that the approval of an amended master
plan, an amended preliminary plat or an amended development agreement is a \egislative act.
Furthermore, as more fully described below, the Amended Master Plan and Amended
Development Agreement, both of which were approved by Ordinance 454, consist of numerous
administrative decisions that interpreted and applied various existing land use ordinances with
regard to the proposed changes to the Cloudrock development, all of which are subject to review
by the Board of Adjustment.

support its argument that Ordinance 454 “is the product of a legislative deliberation by the
County Council exercising its powers under LUDMA and other provisions of the Utah Code to
regulate the use of property within the County.” As noted above, under LUDMA a county land
use decision can be administrative and a county can regulate land use within its boundaries by
implementing, executing or applying existing law use ordinances, which are administrative, not
legislative, functions.

It is significant to note that the Applicable Land Use Code provides that the “Planned
Unit Development District may be applied in all zone districts.” (Emphasis supplied.) See Art.
II.R.1. of Exhibit H to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. Thus, in accordance with the plain language
of the Grand County ordinances, the approval of a PUD involves the application of existing law
to a proposed PUD, an administrative function, and is not the enactment of a new land use law or
policy or the exercise of the County Council’s legislative powers.

To take an example, in accordance with the Sketch Plan prepared for the Cloudrock

Development, Cloudrock’s PUD originally consisted of clustering the wilderness lodge and

4834-38 18-1634MO006-001 40


residential condominium units in one area of the Mesa, thus preserving vast amounts of open
space undisturbed except for intermittent single family homes. See page 2 of Exhibit D and page
3 of 10 to Exhibit E to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. In accordance with its Amended Preliminary
Plat Application (see Exhibit K to Cloudrock’s Memorandum), Cloudrock reallocated the density
of the PUD from the wilderness lodge to residential units located further out on the Mesa. This
reallocation of density was permitted by the terms of the Original Development Agreement. See
Section 1.1 of Exhibit G to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. Accordingly, this change to the PUD did
not require the County Council to enact a new land use law or policy or otherwise exercise its
legislative powers.

Similarly, in accordance with the terms of the Amended Development Agreement,
Cloudrock effectively changed the use-on-review for the wilderness lodge by reducing the
number of lodging units and adding accessory facilities. Changes to a use-on-review are
governed by Art. VI.L of the Applicable Land Use Code and do not require the County Council
to enact a new land use law or policy or otherwise exercise its legislative powers. See Art. VI.L.
of Exhibit H to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. These changes required only that the County Council
apply and implement the requirements of existing applicable law to the proposed changes to the
Cloudrock Development.

Furthermore, under the principles set forth in the Marakis decision, supra., the adoption

of Ordinance 454 was an administrative, not a legislative, act on the part of the County Council.

While the Marakis decision involved a referendum issue, its principles are helpful in analyzing

whether the adoption of Ordinance 454 was an administrative or legislative decision. In applying

4834-3818-1634MO006-001 41

the framework of the Marakis ruling to Ordinance 454, the first question to be addressed by the

Court is whether the ordinance falls within the general purpose and policy of the original County

zoning ordinances. Marakis at 1124. If the approvals underlying Ordinance 454 fall within the

general purpose and policy of the original zoning law, the adoption of Ordinance 454 amounts to
an administrative decision. Id.

There is no dispute that the property to be developed by Cloudrock is zoned RG, Range
and Grazing District (the “RG District”), under the County land use ordinances. Permitted uses
within the RG District include residential uses and use-on-review, including a wilderness lodge.
See Art. TII.J.3. and 4.of Exhibit H to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. In addition, under the
Applicable Land Use Code, all subdivisions in a RG District are subject to the requirements of a
PUD, Planned Unit Development District, unless the subdivision qualifies for a waiver. See Art.
III J.2. of Exhibit H to Cloudrock’s Memorandum. Under the Applicable Land Use Code, the
PUD is designed primarily:

“to provide flexibility to accommodate mixed-use, non-traditional and innovative

developments and renewal. It is further intended to promote more economical and
efficient use of the land while providing a harmonious variety of housing choices,

a higher level of urban amenities, . . . and the preservation of natural and scenic
qualities of open spaces.” See Art. IILR.1. of Exhibit H_ to Cloudrock’s
Memorandum.

As described above in more detail, the changes to the PUD proposed by Cloudrock
included a reallocation of residential density and changes to a use-on-review for a wilderness
lodge. All of these changes were consistent with and contemplated by the existing RG and PUD

zoning ordinances and are within the general purpose and policy of existing Grand County

4834-38 18-1634MO006-001 42

fa} les

zoning law. Thus, in accordance with the Marakis test cited above, the adoption of Ordinance
454 was an administrative decision.
Assuming arguendo that Ordinance 454 does not comport with the general purpose and

policy of the original County RG and.PUD zoning ordinances, the Marakis test requires the

Court to next consider whether the ordinance varies so materially from existing law to warrant a
finding that its adoption was a legislative decision. Marakis at 1124. Under the “material
variance” prong of the Marakis analysis, the Court must decide whether Ordinance 454:

“constitute[s] such a material variation from the basic zoning law of the

governmental unit as to constitute . . . the making of a new law rather than merely

... implementing a comprehensive plan and adjusting it to current conditions.” Id.

““A minor change which merely implements the comprehensive plan and adjusts it

to the current conditions rebuts the legislative presumption and requires the court

to rule that the change was administrative.” Id.

The changes proposed by Cloudrock in the Amended Master Plan and Amended
Development Agreement were in response to recommendations made by Duany Plater-Zyberk &
Company, Architects and Town Planners (“Duany Plater-Zyberk’”’) and consultants to Cloudrock.
Those recommendations included reducing density in the wilderness lodge and shifting more
density out onto the Mesa, creating thereby a series of compact, pedestrian neighborhoods, but
retaining the highest density clustered in much the same location as originally planned. See page
2 of Exhibit N to Cloudrock’s Memorandum.

The PUD changes intended by the adoption of Ordinance 454, including those proposed

by Duany Plater-Zyberk, were either authorized by the Original Development Agreement or

governed and contemplated by existing County ordinances. Moreover, the property to be

developed by Cloudrock had already been approved in 2002 for uses similar in density and
nature as proposed by the Amended Master Plan and Amended Development Agreement and
approved by Ordinance 454. Thus, the changes to the Cloudrock PUD merely implemented the
County’s RG zone and PUD law, and adjusted them to accommodate the current
recommendations of Cloudrock’s consultants. In light of the foregoing facts, the changes to the
PUD approved by Ordinance 454 do not vary so materially from existing law to satisfy the

material variance prong of the Marakis test and are therefore administrative in nature, not

legislative.

In contrast to Resolution 2002-2568 pursuant to which the County Council initially
approved the Johnson’s Up-On-Top PUD, Ordinance 454 makes no reference to a rezone. This is
because the PUD rezoning was done in 2002, if it was done at all. Moreover, while the
Applicable Land Use Code provides that a PUD district shall be considered an amendment to
the zoning map, the Applicable Land Use Code also requires compliance with the zoning map
and text amendment procedures thereof in connection with a PUD approval. Nothing in the
documents referred to by Cloudrock in Cloudrock’s Memorandum support a finding that the
County Council complied with the zoning map and text amendment provisions of the Land Use
Code. While the Planning Commission and County Council held public hearings on Cloudrock’s
“Amended Master Plan and Amended Preliminary Phase 1 Plat,” neither the notice, the agenda
nor the minutes of those hearings support a finding that a zoning map amendment was
considered at those hearings. In addition, a proposed amended zoning map was not presented at

those public hearings as required by LUDMA and applicable case law. See Utah Code Ann.

4834-3818-1634MO006-001 44

§17-27a-503 and Hatch v. Boulder Town Council at 248. Accordingly, the adoption of
Ordinance 454 was not a rezoning decision of the County Council.

Cloudrock relies heavily on the idea that any zoning change is a legislative act to argue
that Ordinance 454 was a legislative decision of the County Council. This analysis is without
merit here for the following reasons. First, not every rezone is a legislative decision. For
example, the Utah Supreme Court has held that the rezoning of property from residential to
commercial use in accordance with current zoning ordinances is an administrative act and not

legislative. See Bird v. Sorenson, 394 P.2d 808 (Utah 1964) and Wilson v. Manning, 657 P.2d

251 (Utah 1982). Second, in light of the facts of this case, it is obvious that there was no
amendment of the Grand County zoning ordinances as a result of the adoption of Ordinance 454
because the County and Cloudrock failed to comply with the provisions of both state and local
law required to effect a valid zoning amendment. Thus, without a valid zoning amendment,
there is no basis whatsoever to find that Ordinance 454 was a legislative decision.

Under Utah law, the approval of an amendment to a PUD is not synonymous with a
legislative act. In fact, the Utah Supreme Court has treated a PUD approval as an administrative
act requiring the application of a standard of review that is different from the standard of review
required in a case involving a legislative act. See Springville Citizens for a Better Community v.
City of Springville, 979 P.2d 332, 336 (Utah 1999). The Utah Supreme Court recently revisited
the Springville Citizens decision in Bradley v. Payson City Corporation, 70 P.3d 47 (Utah 2003).
In reviewing the basis for treating the PUD approval in Springville Citizens as an administrative

decision, the Bradley Court (a) noted that in Springville Citizens the municipality acknowledged

4834-3818-1634MO006-001 45

evn ews

that the PUD approval was an administrative decision; and (b) did not dispute the contention that
all of the issues addressed in Springville Citizens “arose from the administrative processing of
the PUD application pursuant to the standards set forth in city ordinances.” Bradley at 53-54. In
light of the foregoing, there is controlling legal precedent that the PUD Amendment as approved
by Ordinance 454 was an administrative decision and not legislative.

The approvals granted in accordance with Ordinance 454 did effect changes to the PUD
for the Cloudrock Development. In light of the foregoing, however, those approvals did not
involve a valid rezone or the enactment of a new land use law or policy and did not require the
County Council to exercise any legislative powers. In addition, the approval of Ordinance 454
included numerous administrative decisions on the part of the County Council. Thus, the
adoption of Ordinance 454 was not a legislative decision and is subject to review by the Board of
Adjustment. |

D. The County’s Approval of Ordinance 454 Was Arbitrary, Capricious, and
Illegal Because the County Failed to Follow Its Ordinances.

For the reasons set forth above, Ordinance 454 was not a legislative decision of the
County Council. Accordingly, the standard of review cited by Cloudrock in Cloudrock’s
Memorandum is not correct. Under LUDMA, only decisions “involving the exercise of
legislative discretion” are subject to the highly deferential “reasonably debatable” standard of |
review. See Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(3)(b). Even then, legislative decisions are valid only
if they are not otherwise illegal and are not entitled to any deference as to legality. Id. An

administrative decision, such as Ordinance 454, however, is valid only if the decision is

4834-3818-1634MO006-001 46

supported by substantial evidence in the record and is not arbitrary, capricious, or
illegal.(Emphasis supplied.) Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(3)(c). As pointed out herein, without
the Record before the Court or parties, it is impossible to know whether substantial evidence
exists in the Record which would support the County’s decision. Plaintiffs assert, however, that
upon production of the Record and full briefing and argument, the law and fact will show that the

County acted arbitrarily, capriciously, and illegally in approving Ordinance 454.

Cloudrock’s Statement of Undisputed Facts in Cloudrock’s Memorandum does not
support a finding that no genuine issue of material fact exists with regard to the validity of
Ordinance 454. For example, Cloudrock argues that in adopting Ordinance 454 and approving
the proposed amended Cloudrock PUD, the County made all the findings required by law. In
support of this argument, Cloudrock simply refers to the statement of findings contained in the
ordinance itself. Plaintiffs contend, however, that certain of those findings are clearly contrary to
the County’s General Plan and other documents and comments submitted by Plaintiffs and other
members of the public to the County Council for its consideration in connection with the

approval of the proposed changes to Cloudrock Development.

Similarly, Cloudrock argues that the Grand County zoning map was amended in
connection with the adoption of Ordinance 454 as required by law. As discussed previously
herein, Plaintiffs maintain that no public hearing was held with regard to a zoning map
amendment and no proposed zoning map was presented to the County Council as required by
law. Counties are required to strictly comply with their own ordinances, and substantial

compliance is simply not good enough. See Springville Citizens, 979 P.2d at 337-338

(“Municipal zoning authorities are bound by the terms and standards of applicable zoning
ordinances and are not at liberty to make land use decisions in derogation thereof.”). Thus, the

Plaintiffs also take issue with Cloudrock’s arguments regarding the site plan and density

CONCLUSION

Before deciding the merits of Plaintiffs’ petition for review of an administrative decision,
the Court must decide the threshold issue of whether Plaintiffs are entitled to a hearing before the
Board of Adjustment. For the reasons stated herein, this matter should be remanded to the Board
of Adjustment. Even if the Court were to deny Plaintiffs’ request for remand, the Court should
still deny Cloudrock’s motion for summary judgment on both procedural and substantive
grounds. The Court is required by the controlling Utah statutes to decide the merits of Plaintiffs’
petition for review based upon the Record compiled and provided by the County. Here,
however, the County has not provided the Record, leaving this Court no basis upon which
Plaintiffs’ claims may be ruled. A binder of select documents compiled by Plaintiffs’ litigation
opponent is no substitute for the Record which is required. Finally, even if the Record were in
place, and the issues were fully briefed on the merits, the Court should still deny Cloudrock’s

motion because the County’s approval of Ordinance 454 was arbitrary, capricious, and illegal.
