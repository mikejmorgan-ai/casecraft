I. SUMMARY

The pending motions for summary judgment raise two issues that must be decided by the
court. The first issue is whether this court has jurisdiction to hear appeal of Ordinance No. 454,
Series 2007 (“Ordinance 454”)' and the second issue is whether the passage of Ordinance 454
was arbitrary, capricious or illegal. As to the first issue, it is Cloudrock’s position that this court
does have jurisdiction over the appeal because Ordinance 454 is a land use ordinance under
LUDMA.’ As such, only this court has jurisdiction to hear a challenge to the enactment of a land
use ordinance. As for the second issue—whether Ordinance 454 was arbitrary, capricious or
illegal—the critical question is, again, whether Ordinance 454 is a legislative enactment. This is
because this court must grant substantial deference to a legislative enactment and consider it
under a reasonably debatable standard of review.

Ordinance 454 was a legislative decision addressing land use because it approved
rezoning of property from range and grazing to planned-unit-development (“PUD”) zoning. Asa
matter of law, zoning decisions are fundamentally legislative acts. Therefore, this court should
grant Cloudrock summary judgment and rule that Ordinance 454 is not arbitrary, capricious or

illegal. Plaintiffs fail to allege that there is no reasonable debate that Ordinance 454 promotes

II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

A. Reply to Plaintiffs’ Response to Cloudrock’s Facts.

In responding to Cloudrock’s facts, Plaintiffs make a repeated objection regarding the
lack of an “administrative record.” As purported authority for the alleged requirement that there
must be an administrative record before this court can consider Cloudrock’s motion for summary
judgment, Plaintiffs give two citations. Both are inapplicable because both assume an issue to be
decided—whether Ordinance 454 is an administrative, rather than a legislative, act. The first
citation is to Grand County Land Use Code § 7.13.2 (Jan. 2007) (“2007 LUC”). That provision
states that an officer should submit a record to the board of adjustment where an appeal is of a
decision by an “administrative officer.” See id. The Grand County Council’s (“Council”)
enactment of Ordinance 454 is not an act by an administrative officer. The second citation is
also inapplicable. Utah Code section 17-27a-801(7)(a) applies only to decisions of a land use
authority or appeal authority. Those two bodies make administrative decisions, not legislative

ones. Compare Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)(c) with Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)(b).

I. ARGUMENT

Cloudrock’s Motion for Summary Judgment and Plaintiffs’ Cross Motion for Summary
Judgment raise two related issues for decision. The first is whether this court has jurisdiction or
whether the BOA should have first reviewed Ordinance 454. The second issue is whether the
Council’s adoption of Ordinance 454 was arbitrary, capricious or illegal. To resolve both issues,
the court must first determine whether it is a legislative land use decision or an administrative
action. Under LUDMA, legislative decisions are reviewed under a reasonably debatable
standard of review while administrative decisions must be supported by substantial evidence in
the record. Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3) (2007).

Summary judgment is appropriate when there is no genuine issue of material fact and the
moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. Utah R. Civ. P. 56 (2007); Wilcox v.
Anchor Wate, Co., 2007 UT 39, J 10, 164 P.3d 353, 357. When a motion for summary judgment

is made, an adverse party “must set forth specific facts showing that there is a genuine issue for
trial. Summary judgment, if appropriate, shall be entered against a party failing to file such a
response.” Utah R. Civ. P. 56(e). LUDMA adds to this standard by imposing upon a party
challenging a land use ordinance the obligation to overcome the presumption that a land use
ordinance is valid. See Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)(a) and (b); Utah R. Evid. R. 301(a) (legal
presumption imposes on party against whom it is directed burden of proving that nonexistence of
presumed fact is more probable than not).

Here, Cloudrock is entitled to summary judgment as a matter of law because: (A) only
this court has jurisdiction to review Ordinance 454; (B) Ordinance 454 is a legislative act of the
Council; and (C) the Council did not act arbitrarily, capriciously or illegally when it enacted
Ordinance 454. The arguments below are not repetitive but each relates to separate, but related,

parts of LUDMA and applicable precedent. Section (A) answers the question of jurisdiction,

8559694 12

Section (B) establishes the appropriate standard of review, and Section (C) confirms that
Cloudrock is entitled to the affirmative relief it seeks in its motion for summary judgment
namely a declaration that the enactment of Ordinance 454 was legal and not arbitrary and

capricious.

A. Only this Court has Jurisdiction to Hear an Appeal of Ordinance 454.

Plaintiffs seek a declaratory judgment that this court lacks jurisdiction to hear the appeal
of Ordinance 454 because they claim that Ordinance 454 was an administrative land use decision
which must first be appealed to the BOA. (Pls.’ Cross Mot. S.J. at 3, 33-46.) Their argument is
without merit.

1. Ordinance 454 is a Land Use Ordinance.

LUDMA defines a “land use ordinance” as a “planning, zoning, development, or
subdivision ordinance of the county, but does not include the general plan.” Utah Code § 17-
27a-103(19). As a land use ordinance, any challenge must be filed with the district court within
thirty days of enactment. Id. § 17-27a-801(5). Ordinance 454 falls squarely within the definition
of a land use ordinance because it approved an Amended Master Plan and an Amended
Development Agreement, which together operated to amend the Johnson’s Up-on-Top PUD, and
both of which relate to zoning, development and subdivisions. Plaintiffs’ initiation of this
lawsuit within thirty days® of Ordinance 454’s enactment confirms that they understood that
Ordinance 454 is a land use ordinance.

Plaintiffs now argue that Ordinance 454 is not a land use ordinance, but rather a series of
applications of the 2000 LUC. (Pls.’ Cross Mot. S.J. at 36, 38-40.) Their argument continues

that if an ordinance references other ordinances or policy statements it cannot be considered

legislative but becomes administrative. Plaintiffs’ arguments must fail because they ignore
LUDMA’s definition of what constitutes a land use ordinance. LUDMA does not contemplate
that some land use ordinances (those that reference other ordinances or matters which if isolated

ordinances would be appealed directly to court. Rather, it says that appeals of a land use

2. The BOA Lacks Authority to Review the Enactment of a Land Use

Ordinance.

Under LUDMA, the BOA is without power to review the enactment of a land use
ordinance. LUDMA defines the jurisdiction of appeal authorities, such as the BOA here, and
limits them to deciding purely administrative issues grounded in legislative ordinances.
Specifically, these agencies can address: “{a) requests for variances from the terms of the land
use ordinances; and (b) appeals from decisions applying the land uses ordinances.” Utah Code
§ 17-27a-701. The enactment by the Council of Ordinance 454 cannot be said to fall within
either of these statutory classifications: it is certainly not a “variance” and it is also not
“applying the land use ordinances.”

Many Utah cases describe what constitutes “a decision applying the land use ordinances.”
For example, in Xanthos v. Board of Adjustment of Salt Lake City, a property owner appealed a
board of adjustment’s refusal to grant a variance from the zoning requirements setting the
dimensions for side and back yards, and requiring off-street parking and frontage on a public
street. 685 P.2d 1032, 1033 (Utah 1984). In that case the Utah Supreme Court explained that a
board of adjustment’s consideration of whether a variance request met the legal requirements for
a variance was an administrative action. See id. at 1034-35. In Patterson v. Utah County Board
of Adjustment, a neighboring property owner appealed a board of adjustment’s decision granting
a special exception permit to build a private airstrip. 893 P.2d 602, 603 (Utah Ct. App. 1995).
The court observed that the board there had the authority to “hear and decide special exceptions
based on the standards contained in the zoning ordinance.” Id. at 604. In Caster v. West Valley
City, a property owner challenged a zoning administrator’s interpretation of a zoning ordinance.
2001 UT App. 212, 43, 29 P.3d 22, 23. The court explained that an interpretation of a zoning

ordinance is an administrative decision applying zoning ordinances. See id. J 4-5. These cases

8559694 15

illustrate that a variance, a special exception and a zoning interpretation are types of matters
within a board of adjustment’s jurisdiction and all constitute decisions applying or interpreting
an ordinance.

In contrast, the Utah Supreme Court has said that the “enactment of zoning policies .. .
cannot be delegated to other governmental bodies.” Bradley v. Payson City Corp., 2003 UT 16,
413, 70 P.3d 47, 51. This principle was best explained in Sandy City v. Salt Lake County, 827
P.2d 212, 219, 220 (Utah 1992). There, the court held that boards of adjustment are “powerless
to act on questions of zoning or rezoning. . . . Boards of adjustment can tailor a zoning or
rezoning ordinance to specific, unforeseen circumstances, but they lack the authority to
determine zoning classifications of their own accord.” Id. Consistent with the limits expressed
in Bradley and Sandy City, there is no Utah case which holds that an appeal from an enactment
of a zoning ordinance can be, much less must first be, heard by a board of adjustment. In sum,
because Ordinance 454 is a land use ordinance, and because the BOA is without authority to
review the enactment of such an ordinance, this court should deny Plaintiffs’ cross motion for

summary judgment seeking to force review before the BOA.

B. The County Council Exercised Legislative Discretion in Enacting Ordinance
454.

Plaintiffs argue that enactment of Ordinance 454 must be reviewed under the less
deferential “substantial evidence” test. Cloudrock contends that the enactment of Ordinance 454
was a legislative act which under LUDMA requires this court to apply the highly deferential
reasonably debatable standard of review. Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)(b). Cloudrock’s position
is correct because: (1) rezoning is fundamentally a legislative act; and (2) persuasive authority

from other jurisdictions confirms that creation of a PUD is a legislative act.

8559694 16

d. Rezoning is Fundamentally a Legislative Act.

Ordinance 454 is a legislative action because it amended the zoning which applies to a
large piece of land from range and grazing to a specific PUD zoning district. Enactment of a
zoning amendment is fundamentally a legislative act. Bradley, 2003 UT 16, 7 13, 70 P.3d at 51.
In Bradley, the Utah Supreme Court explained that “the exercise of zoning power is a legislative
function.” Id. 12. “The wisdom of the zoning plan, its necessity, the nature and boundaries of
the district to be zoned are matters which lie solely within that discretion.” Id. A Utah court will
“avoid substituting its judgment” for that of the elected body which enacts a zoning ordinance.
Id.

The County amended the zoning district that applied to Cloudrock’s property by enacting
Ordinance 454. The County’s 2000 LUC identifies nine residential zoning districts, of which a
PUD zoning district is one. See 2000 LUC Art.III.A. The County Council creates a PUD zoning
district by amending the then in effect zoning districts. See 2000 LUC Art.III.R.1 and 3. When
the County Council enacted Ordinance 454, it expressly amended the zoning district that applies
to Cloudrock’s property and specified which uses are allowed therein. Many recent Utah cases
confirm that the amendment of a zoning district constitutes a legislative act.

In Bradley, certain landowners applied to rezone their property from single family
residential to multiple family dwellings. 2003 UT 16, 3, 70 P.3d at 49. The city denied their
request and they appealed. See id. 41. The court upheld the city’s decision, stating that, “[t]here
is no dispute in this case that the enactment and amendment of zoning ordinances is
fundamentally a legislative act. The political nature of the decision making process underlying
municipal zoning demands that the power to make such decisions be vested in persons who are
publicly accountable for their choices.” Id. § 11, 70 P.3d at 51. Accordingly, the court refused

to disturb the legislative body’s action.

8559694 17

In Harmon City, Inc. v. Draper City, a landowner applied for a rezoning of its property
from residential to commercial to allow for a grocery store. 2000 UT App. 31, {J 2-3, 997 P.2d
321, 322-23. Draper City denied the application and the landowner sued. The landowner argued
on appeal that the rezoning decision must be supported by substantial evidence on the record,
and that the decision not to rezone lacked that supporting evidence. Id. 6, 997 P.2d at 323.
The court stated “establishing zoning classifications reflects a legislative policy decision with
which courts will not interfere except in the most extreme cases.” Id. J 18, 997 P.2d at 326. The
court then concluded that because the decision to rezone was a legislative action, it is to be
reviewed under the reasonably debatable standard, not the less deferential substantial evidence
standard. Id. § 25, 997 P.2d at 328; see Tolman v. Logan City, 2007 UT App. 260, {J 18-19, 167
P.3d 489, 499 (applying reasonably debatable standard of review to denial of rezoning); Smith
Investment Co. v. Sandy City, 958 P.2d 245, 251, n.6 (Utah Ct. App. 1998) (“rezonings in Utah
are characterized as legislative”).

Ordinance 454 effected a rezoning of the property and that decision, under the above
authorities, was legislative.

Plaintiffs argue that rezoning is not a legislative act and rely upon four referendum cases
for that proposition. (Pls.’ Cross Mot. S.J. at 41 and 45.) See Bird v. Sorenson, 394 P.2d 808
(Utah 1964); Wilson _v. Manning, 657 P.2d 251 (Utah 1982); Citizen’s Awareness Now_v.
Marakis, 873 P.2d 1117 (Utah 1994); Low v. City of Monticello, 2004 UT 90, 103 P.3d 130.
However, these four cases are inapplicable here because they are referendum cases that were all
decided before the enactment of LUDMA and none of them address the standard of review for
legislative enactments.

A relatively recent case confirms that Bird, at least, is not applicable to determining

standards of review. In Smith Investment Co., certain city residents facially challenged the

Plaintiffs also incorrectly assert that a case known as Springville Citizens is controlling

administrative decisions in Springville Citizens stemmed solely from the fact that the standard of

Cc, The County’s Enactment of Ordinance 454 Was Not Arbitrary, Capricious
or Illegal.

Plaintiffs assert the Council acted arbitrarily, capriciously and illegally when it enacted
Ordinance 454. (Pls.’ Mot. Cross S.J. at 46-48.) Under LUDMA, this court must presume a land
use ordinance is valid and determine only whether it is arbitrary, capricious or illegal. Utah
Code § 17-27a-801(3). A legislative land use ordinance is not “arbitrary” or “capricious” if it is
reasonably debatable that the decision could promote the general welfare. Utah Code § 17-27a-
801(3)(b); Bradley, 2003 UT 16, 414, 70 P.3d at 52. A land use decision is not “illegal” if it
complies with the applicable statutes and/or ordinances. See Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)(d);
Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25, | 31, 979 P.2d at 338. Even if a decision or ordinance did not
comply with every legal requirement, Plaintiffs must show that “that they were prejudiced by the
[county’s] noncompliance with its ordinances or, in other words, how, if at all, the [county’s]
decision would have been different and what relief, if any, they are entitled to as a result.”
Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25, 4 31, 979 P.2d at 338.

The Council’s enactment of Ordinance 454 was not arbitrary, capricious or illegal.

1, It is “Reasonably Debatable” that Ordinance 454 Promotes the General
Welfare.

Plaintiffs do not assert that there can be no reasonable debate as to whether Ordinance
454 promotes the general welfare. Instead, Plaintiffs assert that Ordinance 454 is arbitrary and
capricious because it is not supported by substantial evidence in the record. (Pls.’ Cross Mot.

S.J. at 46-47.) However, this is the wrong standard of review. Under LUDMA, a legislative

8559694 22

land use decision is not arbitrary and capricious if it is reasonably debatable that the decision
could promote the general welfare. Only an administrative land use decision, which Ordinance
454 is not, must be supported by substantial evidence in the record. Utah Code § 17-27a-
801(3)(b), (c). Plaintiffs have not even attempted to meet LUDMA’s requirement that a person
challenging a legislative decision show that the decision is not “reasonably debatable.” Id. § 17-
27a-801(3)(b). Therefore, this court should find that Ordinance 454 is not arbitrary and

capricious under LUDMA and Bradley.

2. Ordinance 454 is not Illegal.

Under LUDMA, a land use decision is illegal if it does not comply with applicable
ordinances, statutes and laws. Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3). However, a showing of illegality is
not enough to overturn Ordinance 454. Rather, Plaintiffs must also show that the illegality
prejudiced them; “in other words, how, if at all, the [county’s] decision would have been
different and what relief, if any, they are entitled to as a result.” Springville Citizens, 1999 UT
25, 7 31, 979 P.2d at 338.

In its Motion for Summary Judgment, pages 14-17, Cloudrock set forth the mandatory
requirements for PUD approval which Grand County established for itself. Those requirements
are that the Council make certain required findings of fact, the Council follow the process for
zoning map and text amendment, the Council require a site plan, and that the PUD not exceed

a. No Record is Required to Determine Validity of Legislative

Decision.

Plaintiffs argue that this court cannot determine whether Ordinance 454 is valid because
it lacks an administrative record. (Pls.’ Cross Mot. S.J. at 4.) LUDMA, however, does not
require the creation of a record for an appeal of a legislative act. See id. § 17-27a-801(7)(a).
Rather, an administrative record is created in those cases where a court is reviewing an appeal
from a decision of a “land use authority” or “appeal authority.” See id. § 17-27a-103(2), (17).
LUDMA further explains that the standard of review for reviewing the decision of a land use
authority or appeal authority is the substantial evidence standard, or rather whether the decision
is supported by “substantial evidence in the record.” See id. § 17-27a-801(3)(c).

The Council acted within its legislative authority when it passed Ordinance 454 and
LUDMA contains no requirement that a “legislative body” compile a record when its legislative
enactments are appealed. See id. § 17-27a-103(20). Moreover, if there is no record, LUDMA
expressly provides that “the court may call witnesses and take evidence.” See id. § 17-27a-
801(8)(b). There is no part of LUDMA that excuses Plaintiff for failing to respond to
Cloudrock’s motion for a declaration from this court that Ordinance 454 is legal. See Jefferson
County, 964 S.W.2d at 536 (“[W]hen reviewing legislative actions, the scope of review is not
limited to the record presented to the legislative body.”).

An appeal of a legislative act can be likened to a challenge to a statute. The legislature
does not submit a written record to support a statute if a party challenges that statute. Rather, the
party challenging the statute is obligated to identify the illegality and support that argument with
facts and law. In this case, it is Plaintiffs who bear the burden to prove illegality and under

LUDMA they cannot avoid their burden by claiming a record must first be submitted.

planning commission’s recommendation on a zoning ordinance. Utah Code § 17-27a-502 and -
503; see Hatch, 2001 UT App. 55, 21 P.3d 245. Under the 2000 LUC, Article VIB, a request
for a zoning map amendment must include a boundary survey of the land to be rezoned. If the
zoning ordinance is approved, “such changes or amendments shall be made promptly after
official adoption of the change or amendment as provided for herein. The County Planner shall
be responsible for the physical updating and the amendment of the official zoning map.” 2000
LUC Art. LB.2.

In submitting the request for PUD amendment, Cloudrock submitted a map showing the
land to be rezoned. (See Exhibit C to Cloudrock’s Supp. S.J. Memo.) By Plaintiffs’ own
admission, they obtained a copy of the maps included with Cloudrock’s submission and the first
page of those maps shows the boundaries of the PUD and is attached as Exhibit D to the
Declaration of Barbara A. Morra. (See Plaintiffs Statement of Undisputed Facts No. 46,
Plaintiffs’ Mem. Supp. S.J.) The boundaries shown on the Sheet 1 attached to the Amended
Development Agreement are the same as those shown the amendment to official zoning map
which the County Planner did eventually update and dated December 26, 2007. (A copy of the
amended official zoning map is attached to Cloudrock’s Supp. S.J. Memo. as Exhibit V.)

Moreover, Plaintiffs fail to demonstrate how any purported illegality regarding the
ultimate creation of the zoning map prejudiced them. Plaintiff obtained a copy of the boundary
map of the PUD and are well aware of its location within Grand County. Plaintiffs cannot

demonstrate that any delay in the update of the official zoning map prejudiced them in any way.

IV. CONCLUSION

For all of the foregoing reasons, this court should conclude that the enactment of
Ordinance 454 was legal and not arbitrary or capricious. This court should further conclude that
the BOA lacks jurisdiction to review the enactment of Ordinance 454. Accordingly, this court

17-27-1001 Appeals.

(1) No person may challenge in district court a county's land use decisions made under this chapter or under the
regulation made under authority of this chapter until that person has exhausted all administrative remedies.

(2) (a) Any person adversely affected by any decision made in the exercise of the provisions of this chapter may
file a petition for review of the decision with the district court within 30 days after the local decision is rendered.

(b) (i) The time under Subsection (2)(a) to file a petition is tolled from the date a property owner files a re-
quest for arbitration of a constitutional taking issue with the private property ombudsman under Section
63-34-13 until 30 days after:

(A) the arbitrator issues a final award; or

(B) the private property ombudsman issues a written statement under Subsection 63-34-13(4)(b) declining to
arbitrate or to appoint an arbitrator.

(ii) A tolling under Subsection (2)(b){i) operates only as to the specific constitutional taking issues that are
the subject of the request for arbitration filed with the private property ombudsman by a property owner.

(iii) A request for arbitration filed with the private property ombudsman after the time under Subsection
(2)(a) to file a petition has expired does not affect the time to file a petition.

(3) (a) The courts shall:
(i) presume that land use decisions and regulations are valid; and
(ii) determine only whether or not the decision is arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.

(b) A determination of illegality requires a determination that the decision violates a statute, ordinance, or ex-
isting law.

ANALYSIS
Applicability.
Exhaustion of administrative remedies.
Violation of zoning resolution.

Applicability.

Exhaustion requirement of Subsection (1) did not apply to action in which plaintiffs claimed that defendants had
violated zoning ordinances, building standards, and a conditional use permit, as this was not a challenge of a
county's land use decision. Culbertson v. Bd. of County Comm'rs, 2001 UT 108, 44 P.3d 642.

Exhaustion of administrative remedies.

Plaintiff seeking to enjoin construction of a trailer park was required to exhaust his administrative remedies be-
fore an action for injunctive relief could be maintained. Lund v. Cottonwood Meadows Co., 15 Utah 2d 305, 392
P.2d 40 (1964).

A party must exhaust administrative remedies before seeking judicial review of the denial of a building permit.
Hatch v. Utah County Planning Dep't, 685 P.2d 550 (Utah 1984).

Plaintiff aggrieved by a decision of the county commission applying the zoning ordinance was required to ap-
peal that decision to the board of adjustment; plaintiff could not initiate mandamus proceedings under § 17-27-
1002 against the commission for its alleged violation of the ordinance. Bennion v. Sundance Dev. Corp., 897
P.2d 1232 (Utah Ct. App. 1995).

Violation of zoning resolution.

Landowners under former § 17-27-23 had a separate cause of action in the courts when a violation of a zoning
resolution was charged; but where the alleged violation of the ordinance arose from the administration of the
zoning ordinance by an administrative agency, appeal from the administrative ruling should have been taken to
the proper administrative tribunal, or a suit should have been commenced in the courts within ninety days. Lund
v. Cottonwood Meadows Co., 15 Utah 2d 305, 392 P.2d 40 (1964) (decided under former § 17-27- 15).
