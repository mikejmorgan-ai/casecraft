I SUMMARY

Plaintiffs (“Citizens”) filed suit in this court against Grand County, Utah and the Grand
County Council seeking a declaratory judgment that the Council acted arbitrarily, capriciously,
and illegally in adopting Ordinance No. 454, Series 2007.' Ordinance 454 approved an
Amended Master Plan and Preliminary Phase 1 Plat (together defined as the “Amended Master
Plan”) and Amended Development Agreement which together operated to amend the Johnson’s
Up-on-Top Planned Unit Development (“Amended PUD”).

Citizens challenged Ordinance 454 in two forums simultaneously, by appealing its
adoption to the Grand County Board of Adjustment (“BOA”) and by challenging it in this Court.
Cloudrock moves for a summary judgment that first, only this court, not the BOA, has
jurisdiction to review the County’s legislative action enacting Ordinance 454; and second,
adoption of Ordinance 454 was not arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.

Review of county legislative land-use decisions must be made to district court, while
appeal of certain county administrative decisions are to be made to the BOA. Cloudrock
contends that only this court has jurisdiction over the Citizens’ challenge to Ordinance 454
because it is a land use ordinance under Utah law and is the result of legislative decision-making
on the part of the County.

In reviewing county land use actions, including the passage of a land use ordinance,

courts presume the validity of these actions and are to determine only if the challenged action

Il. ARGUMENT

This court should reject Citizens’ claims and grant Cloudrock’s motion for summary
judgment. Under Rule 56, summary judgment is appropriate when there is no genuine issue of
material fact and the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. Utah R. Civ. P.

56 (2007); Wilcox v. Anchor Wate, Co., 2007 UT 39, J 10, 164 P.3d 353, 357. When reviewing

legislative land use decisions, the Court must presume the decision, ordinance, or regulation is

462633.9

valid and can overturn it only if it finds the action challenged arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.
Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3) (2007); Tolman v. Logan City, 2007 UT App. 260, {] 14, 167 P.3d
489, 493.

Citizens have brought two claims in this case. The first is the assertion that the County’s
decision to approve Ordinance 454 was arbitrary, capricious, and illegal. The second claim is
that the Grand County Board of Adjustment (“BOA”) has initial jurisdiction over their challenge
to Ordinance 454, and this court has jurisdiction only to review the BOA’s decision. Cloudrock
is entitled to summary judgment as a matter of law on both claims because (i) the BOA does not
have jurisdiction to review the passage of Ordinance 454, a legislative act; and (ii) the Council

did not act arbitrarily, capriciously or illegally when it enacted Ordinance 454.
A. Only this Court has Jurisdiction to Hear an Appeal of Ordinance 454.

Citizens complain that this court lacks jurisdiction to pass on their challenge and that the
BOA should have heard their attack on Ordinance 454 first. This claim lacks any merit.

Utah law clearly provides that a challenge to Ordinance 454 must be brought before the
district court. See County Land Use Management and Development Act, Utah Code, Title 17,
Chapter 27a (“LUDMA”). Specifically, LUDMA states in relevant part that “a challenge to the
enactment of a land use ordinance or general plan may not be filed with the district court more
than 30 days after enactment.” Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(5) (2007). LUDMA defines the
term “land use ordinance” as “a planning, zoning, development, or subdivision ordinance of the
county, but does not include the general plan.” Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-103(18). Ordinance
454 is what LUDMA defines as a “land use ordinance.” Citizens filing of this action amounts to

a recognition that Ordinance 454 is an ordinance that concerns “planning, zoning, development

462633.9

10

or subdivision” of land within the County, and that any challenge to it lies before this court, not
the BOA. See Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(5).

Another provision of LUDMA makes clear that a BOA does not have power to review
legislative actions. In Utah Code § 17-27a-501, LUDMA states that only legislative bodies have
authority to enact “land use ordinances.” Such legislative authority cannot be delegated to quasi-
judicial or administrative bodies like the BOA. See Bradley v. Payson City Corp, 2003 UT 16, J
13, 70 P.2d at 51. LUDMA clarifies that the BOA has a role in reviewing certain administrative
actions of a county, but when it comes to legislative acts, the only avenue of review is to district
court. See Utah Code Ann. §17-27a-801(5)

Grand County’s own ordinances confirm this principle. Those ordinances state that the
BOA only has power to hear appeals of administrative actions and to consider variance requests.
See 2000 LUC Art. VI.A 2.c. Ordinance 454 is neither. Rather, it is the product of a legislative
deliberation by the County Council exercising its powers under LUDMA and other provisions of
the Utah Code to regulate the use of property within the County. See Utah Code Ann. §§17-27a-
501, -102(1)(b); 17-53-207. This court has exclusive jurisdiction over this appeal of Ordinance

454 because it involves a land use ordinance and is not an administrative action.

B. The County Council’s Approval of Ordinance 454 Was Not Arbitrary ,
Capricious or Illegal.

Cloudrock seeks a summary judgment that the Council’s adoption of Ordinance 454 was
not arbitrary, capricious or illegal because (i) there is reasonable debate that Ordinance 454 could
promote the public welfare; and (ii) the Council did not act illegally in adopting Ordinance 454.
Under LUDMA, this court is to presume a land use decision valid and is to determine only

whether it is arbitrary, capricious or illegal. Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3). A legislative land use

462633.9

11

decision is not “arbitrary” or “capricious” if it is reasonably debatable that it could promote the
public welfare. See id. And a land use decision is not “illegal” if it complies with the applicable
statutes and/or ordinances. See Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3); Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25, |
31, 979 P.2d at 338. “In fact, because a zoning classification reflects a legislative policy
decision, [courts] will not interfere with that decision except in the most extreme cases.”
Tolman, 2007 UT App. 260, 4 14, 167 P.3d at 493 (quotation omitted). The Council’s approval
of Ordinance 454 was not arbitrary, capricious or illegal.

1. It is “Reasonably Debatable” that Ordinance 454 Promotes the Public
General Welfare.

A county legislative decision is not arbitrary and capricious if it “could promote the
general welfare; or even if it is reasonably debatable that it is in the interest of the general

welfare.” Smith Inv., Co. v. Sandy City, 958 P.2d 245, 252 (Utah Ct. App. 1998); Tolman, 2007

UT App. 260, ¥ 18, 167 P.3d at 495; Patterson v. Utah County Bd. of Adjustment, 893 P.2d 602,
606-07 (Utah Ct. App. 1995); Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3). Here, Citizens do not even assert that
the enactment of Ordinance 454 meets the standard for invalidity set by Smith, Tolman, and
Patterson. Instead, they appear to argue that this law does not apply to the review of Ordinance
454.

The Utah Supreme Court has spoken very clearly to the issue of when deference to
legislative judgment is appropriate in considering challenges to the merits of legislation. The
rule it set forth in Bradley v. Payson City Corp is directly applicable here. See 2003 UT 16, 4 3,
6, 70 P.3d at 49, 50. In Bradley, plaintiffs sought rezoning of their property to allow a more
dense residential use. See id. The Payson City Planning Commission reviewed the applications
and recommended approval. Nevertheless, the City Council denied the rezoning. Id. J 4, 70

P.3d at 49. Plaintiffs sued and the district court overturned the Council's decision as arbitrary

462633.9

12

and capricious because it lacked evidentiary support. The plaintiffs appealed. Id. { 7, 70 P.3d at
50.

The Utah Supreme Court held that land use decisions should be upheld unless they are
arbitrary, capricious or illegal. “Indeed, municipal land use decisions as a whole are generally
entitled to a ‘great deal of deference.’” Id. § 10, 70 P.3d at 50 (citing Springville Citizens, 1999
UT 25, { 23, 979 P.2d 332). The court then distinguished administrative land use decisions that
are not arbitrary or capricious if supported by substantial evidence from legislative land use
decisions that are not arbitrary or capricious so long as the grounds for the decision are
reasonably debatable. Id. § 10, 70 P.3d at 50-51. Administrative land use decisions include
variance decisions, administrative interpretation of zoning ordinances, decisions on special
exception applications. Id. § 15, 70 P.3d at 52. Legislative land use decisions encompass
passing zoning ordinances and determining zoning policy. Id. 44 11-12, 70 P.3d at 51.

The Bradley court held that the Payson City Council's denial of the rezoning was
legislative in nature because “the enactment and amendment of zoning ordinances is
fundamentally a legislative act.” Id. § 11, 70 P.3d at 51. The court noted that the political nature
of zoning decisions “demands that the power to make such decisions be vested in persons who
are publicly accountable for their choices.” Id. Any party challenging such legislative decision
bears the burden of showing that the decision “was clearly beyond the city's power.” Id. J 12, 70
P.3d at 51.

The Bradley decision demonstrates that the Grand County Council’s decision to adopt
Ordinance 454 was legislative, not administrative in nature, and is entitled to the high standard of
presumed validity discussed in Smith, Tolman, and Patterson and embodied in Utah Code Ann.
§17-27a-801(3)(a) . Here, Citizens have not even attempted to show that adopting Ordinance

454 was beyond the County’s powers. Because Ordinance 454 constitutes a legislative action to

462633.9

13

rezone the subject property, and because Citizens do not allege that the grounds for the passage
of Ordinance 454—that it is in the interest of the general welfare—was not “reasonably
debatable,” Cloudrock is entitled to summary judgment. The passage of Ordinance 454 was not
arbitrary or capricious.

2. The County Complied with All Mandatory Requirements in Enacting
Ordinance 454.

The passage of Ordinance 454 also is attacked as “illegal.” Again, that claim lacks merit.
The County complied with all legal requirements in enacting Ordinance 454. A land use
ordinance is illegal only if it fails to comply with mandatory provisions of a statute, regulation or
ordinance. Springville Citizens, 1999 UT 25, 4 30, 979 P.2d at 338. In this case, Grand
County’s ordinances impose only four mandatory requirements on the process of creation of a
PUD: (i) that the County make three required findings; (ii) that the County amend the zoning
map in approving a PUD; (ili) that the County require a site plan; and (iv) that the approved
maximum density be no greater than permitted in the underlying zoning district prior to PUD
approval. See 2000 LUC, Art. III. R. All of the other‘ alleged illegalities identified in Citizens’
Amended Complaint either relate to discretionary choices made by the County or are not ripe for

review.

As is shown below, the County has met each of the four mandatory requirements for
amending the original PUD.

a.) Required Findings.

The 2000 LUC states that a PUD may be approved “provided the [o]rdinance includes
findings that the proposed development shall: (1) [bJe consistent with the Grand County General
Plan; (2) [bJe consistent with the character of existing land uses in the surrounding area; and (3)
[nJot adversely affect the future development of the surrounding area.” 2000 LUC, Art. III,
R.2.b. When the County approved Ordinance 454, it specifically made each of the above-
required findings. (See Ex. A.) Therefore, the County met this mandatory legal requirement.

b.) Amend Zoning Map

Another County requirement is that a PUD shall be “considered an amendment to the
zoning map.” 2000 LUC, Art. IIT, R. 3. In order to amend the zoning map, the County provides
notice of an action that will change the zoning map, holds public hearings and passes an
ordinance. See 2000 LUC, Art. VI.B.4.a.

The County complied with this requirement by providing public notice of its

consideration of Cloudrock’s application for an Amended PUD. (See Ex. O; Ex. P.; and Ex. T.)

Citizens responded to the County’s notices and repeatedly appeared before the County in public

hearings to argue against Ordinance 454. (See Ex. Q; and Ex. S.) After receiving input from the

Citizens and other members of the public, the County Council approved the Amended PUD, an
action that changed the County’s zoning map. A copy of the updated and amended zoning map
is attached as Exhibit V. The attached notice and map exhibits confirm that the County met this

legal requirement.

c.) Site Plan

The 2000 LUC states that “[a] comprehensive site plan shall be approved as part of the
ordinance approving a Planned Unit Development.” 2000 LUC, Art. III. R.3. a. The site plan is
to include “the requirements for ingress and egress to the property with adequate right-of-way,
special setbacks, sidewalks, trails, utilities, drainage, parking space, building height, open space,
screening. ..” Id. In this case, the County required Cloudrock to meet this requirement by
submitting the Amended Master Plan and the Cloudrock Code. The Amended Master Plan
depicts the Development’s configuration, utilities, drainage, roads and other important design
elements. (See Ex. C.) The Cloudrock Code contains standards and restrictions for the entire
development and individual lots throughout the Development. (See Exhibit E to the Amended
Development Agreement, attached as Exhibit B.) These documents, taken together, show that
the County clearly required Cloudrock to abide by the site plan requirement for approving a
PUD.

d.) Maximum Density

The County did not exceed the maximum density allowed for the Development’s lands
when it enacted Ordinance 454. As Citizens admit in their amended complaint, the underlying
base zoning for the Development’s lands allows the location of 1 unit per 5 acres for residential
units and | unit per 2.5 acres for wilderness lodge units. (See Am. Compl. J 111.) The Original
PUD amended this zoning and authorized a total of 260 residential units and 225 wilderness
lodge units. The original configuration required a total” of 1,862.5 acres. (See Am. Compl.
112.)

The Amended PUD reconfigured the density so that there will now be 336 residential

units and 73 wilderness lodge units. (See Ex. A.) As Citizens admit in their amended complaint,
> This number is the sum of 225 lodge units multiplied by 2.5 and 260 residential units multiplied
by 5.

these units only require a total of 1,862.5 acres, the exact same number of acres and total density
as was approved in 2002. (See Am Compl. | 112). There can be no argument that there has
been an increase in total density.

In an effort to claim the County still exceeded the maximum allowed density, Citizens
focus on employee housing. Although the number of approved employee housing units is the
same as was allowed under the Original PUD, Citizens claim that the 55 employee housing units
must be counted as residential units. Citizens’ claim ignores the fact that the 2000 LUC
expressly permits a wilderness lodge to include employee housing as an accessory use instead of
as an accommodation unit. See 2000 LUC Art. III C.2.h(2). As an accessory use, housing does
not count towards the Development’s density. Rather, the County’s ordinances state that an
accessory use is “subordinate to the permitted use by right of the land or lot area.” 2000 LUC
Art. II.B. The 2000 LUC provides other examples of this principle. For instance, in the Light
Industrial District a caretaker or guard residence is treated as an accessory use, not a use that
counts towards a particular development’s density. See 2000 LUC Art.P.5. The County has
made the legal decision in enacting the 2000 LUC to have the density for a wilderness lodge tied
to the accommodation units, not the rooms devoted to housing employees.

The County has structured its ordinances so that employee housing is an accessory use to
a wilderness lodge and there is nothing illegal about not including this accessory use as part of
the overall density for the Development. Accordingly, the County did not violate any part of its
ordinances, let alone any mandatory provisions related to approving or amending a PUD, in
approving 55 employee housing units in connection with the wilderness lodge.

In sum, the County met all mandatory requirements in adopting the Amended PUD and
enacting Ordinance 454. See 2000 LUC, Art.III.R. Under controlling Utah law, the decision is

therefore deemed legal and may not be overturned on review. Citizens’ claims that Ordinance

462633.9

17

454 is illegal are not supported or supportable. This Court should grant Cloudrock’s motion for

summary judgment.
IV. CONCLUSION

Grand County exercised its legislative discretion in passing Ordinance 454. The process
was political and Citizens participated at some level in every one of the many hearings the
Council held on Ordinance 454. Utah law requires reviewing courts to defer to such a legislative
decision. Citizens cannot carry their heavy burden to show that the decision is not reasonably
debatable. Indeed, they have not even attempted to do so. Moreover, Citizens cannot point to
any illegality related to Ordinance 454 because the County complied with all mandatory
requirements of its ordinances which govern PUDs. Accordingly, this Court should grant

Cloudrock’s motion for summary judgment.
