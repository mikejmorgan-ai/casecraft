I. The Enactment of Ordinance 454 was an Administrative Act.

On February 4, 2002, the Grand County Council (“Council”) approved development
applications for the Johnson’s Up-on-Top Development, including land use applications for (1)
PUD rezoning, (2) Preliminary Plat, Phase 1 and Preliminary Master Plan, (3) Use-on Review
for Wilderness Lodge, and (4) Special Exception for Design Standards. Prior to this approval,
the land subject to these applications had been zoned “RG, Range and Grazing.” [R. at
GC00295.] Over five years later, Cloudrock Land Development, LLC (“Cloudrock”), obtained
approval from the Council for “the Johnson’s Up-On-Top Preliminary PUD and Master Plan as
proposed, and the Amended and Restated Johnson’s Up-On-Top Mesa Development
Agreement” on May 8, 2007. [R. at GC00603.] The Council’s action to approve amendments to
land use approvals obtained five years previously constituted an administrative act, nothing
more.

Cloudrock’s argument that the 2007 action was legislative is the age old “form over

substance.” Ordinance 454, the form by which the Council selected to document its approval of
| Cloudrock’s application, did not seek a new zoning classification.' The land use application
submitted by the Cloudrock also did not seek a “PUD Amendment;” it only requested approval
of an “Amended Preliminary Plat.” [R. at GC00315.] At most, Ordinance 454 is an amendment
to an already existing PUD zoning classification. No evidence in the record even suggests that

Cloudrock applied for a new zoning classification. Any rezoning of the Johnson’s Up-on-Top

Cloudrock argues that Ordinance 454 is per se a legislative act given the Utah Supreme

served only to amend the preliminary plat and preliminary master plan for the already approved
Development. Therefore Ordinance 454 is distinguished from Friends of Maple Mountain and
cannot be considered per se legislative.

Cloudrock secondly relies on the Utah Supreme Court’s decision in BRAVE v. Beaver
County, 2009 UT 8, 203 P.3d 937. Once again its reliance is misplaced. BRAVE involved a

II. The County Acted Illegally When it Enacted Ordinance 454.

If, arguendo, this Court holds that Ordinance 454 was legislative, the action was void
because it was illegal. Unlike the arbitrary and capricious standard for legislative enactments
that yield substantial deference to the legislative body, illegality is reviewed by this Court under
a correctness standard.* The Council illegally approved Ordinance 454 and failed to strictly
comply with the Applicable LUC. Accordingly, Ordinance 454 is invalid. See Hatch v. Boulder
Town Council, 2001 UT App 55, 7, 21 P.3d 245. |

B. The County’s Violations of the Applicable LUC are Numerous and Substantial.

The approval of Ordinance 454 was illegal for a number of reasons. Each illegality is

independently sufficient to void the County’s action.

1. Cloudrock’s Subdivision Approval had Lapsed and Preliminary Plat Could not be
Amended.

It is undisputed that the only land use application before the Council in 2007 was
Cloudrock’s application for an Amended Preliminary Plat. [R at GC00315.] However, the
subdivision approved in 2002 had lapsed and the preliminary plat could not be amended.’

The Original Development Agreement required the parties (both Cloudrock and the
County) to “comply with the standards and procedures contemplated by the Code and the

General Plan . . . with respect to all required development approvals of the uses, densities, and

general configuration of the Project.” [R. at GC00286.] As Cloudrock must acknowledge, the
Original Development Agreement required continued compliance with the procedural
requirements of the Applicable LUC, including the time periods for approvals and requirements
to seek and obtain extensions.

Given the standards and procedures in the Applicable LUC, the preliminary plat
approved in 2002 had expired and could not simply be amended. According to the Applicable
LUC, the Grand County Planning Commission’s “preliminary approval of [a] subdivision shall
be deemed voided unless a final plat is submitted within the 12 month period or unless the 12
month period is extended by the Planning Commission at the request of the subdivider.”
Applicable LUC Art. VI.D.7.b (emphasis added). [R. at GC00110.] This same standard applies
for multi-phase subdivisions “unless at least one (1) phase of the subdivision is submitted whitn
12 months following approval of the previous final plat for the subdivision.” See id. (emphasis
added). It is undisputed that a final plat has never been submitted by Cloudrock and that that
neither the Planning Commission nor the Council extended approval of the preliminary plat
prior to Cloudrock’s application in 2006. Thus, the Council could not approve an amendment to
a void preliminary plat; the subdivision approval lapsed; and Cloudrock must start the
subdivision process anew.®

3. The County Failed to Strictly Comply with the Requirements for a Zone Change.

Essentially, Cloudrock argues that its 2006 land use application sought to do more than
merely amend the County’s 2002 approvals of the Johnson’s Up-on-Top Development; it sought
to completely replace the 2002 approvals with a new PUD and thereby changed the zone. If this
Court finds that Ordinance 454 was a legislative PUD rezoning as urged by Cloudrock, and not
an administrative amendment, then all of the requirements in the Applicable LUC governing
PUD applications should have been followed by the Council.

Assuming, arguendo, that Cloudrock’s Application constituted a PUD rezone, the public
notices for Ordinance 454 were fatally deficient as they all failed to identify a zone change had
been applied for. [R. at GC00325, GC00357.] The published agenda for the February 6 2007
public hearing only gave notice “for an Amended Preliminary Plat Phase 1 for Johnson’s Up-on-
Top Mesa Amended PUD.” [R. at GC00374.] The County did not provide the public notice of
any rezone of the Johnson’s Up-on-Top Mesa Development. It did not give notice even of an
amendment to the already approved PUD. Rather, it only gave notice of an Amended
Preliminary Plat Phase 1.

Further, Cloudrock’s Application was incomplete because it was unaccompanied by a

4851-6193-1783.MO006.001 9

title report, a treasurer’s report regarding the status of taxes, a certified boundary survey, a list of
adjacent owners, or a statement of the applicant, all required for a zone change application under
the Applicable LUC.'! See Applicable LUC, Art. VI.B.2.a-.h. [R. at GC00102-103.] In response
to Citizens pointing out that the 2006 land use application was incomplete, Cloudrock refers this
Court to a Sketch Plan submitted to the Planning Commission in November 2000. [R. at
GC00775 and GC00143.] However, Cloudrock cannot point to any evidence in the record that
the necessary documents were submitted with its application in 2006. The 2000 Sketch Plan was
submitted in support of the land use applications approved by the Council (by resolution) in
February 2002. [R. at GC00294-297.] Such documents were six years out of date when
Cloudrock submitted its application for an Amended Preliminary Plat in November 2006. [R. at
GC00315.] Thus, Cloudrock has not identified any evidence to show that its 2006 application
was complete.
CONCLUSION

In failing to adhere to its own land use code, the Council put the interests of a developer
ahead of the general welfare of the County and failed to address major issues related to the
development that could adversely affect the health, welfare, livelihood and/or property values of
Citizens and others. Therefore, Citizens respectfully request this Court to grant its Motion for
Summary Judgment and either remand the consideration of Ordinance 454 to the Grand County

Board of Adjustment or find the approval of Ordinance 454 to be illegal and void.
