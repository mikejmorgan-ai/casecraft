ARGUMENT

The Court should grant Plaintiffs’ cross-motion for summary judgment. Plaintiffs are entitled
to have their grievances aired before the BOA and remand to that administrative body should be
ordered. Even if remand is denied, however, the Court should not attempt to rule on the merits of
Plaintiffs’ appeal of an administrative decision without having the benefit of the record of
administrative proceedings below. Fundamental principles of law, embodied in the County Land
Use, Development, and Management Act (“LUDMA”), Utah Code Ann. § 17-27a-101 ef seq., dictate
that a district court’s review of an administrative action must be conducted upon the record. The
County’s failure to produce the record, despite its previous assurances that it would do so,' prevents
the Court from deciding the matter on the merits. Moreover, even assuming, for the sake of
argument, that the Court could rule on the merits of Plaintiffs’ appeal in the absence of a record, and
that remand to the BOA was denied, Cloudrock’s motion still should be denied because the County’s

actions were arbitrary, capricious, and illegal.

Cloudrock paints with too broad of brush when it argues that the approval of a PUD is a
rezone that necessarily equals a legislative act. The Utah Court of Appeals has recognized that in
“cases deciding whether PUD approval is legislative or administrative, the distinction is based upon
the extent to which a municipality’s PUD enabling ordinance limits the municipality’s discretion over
the PUD approval.” Harmon City, Inc. v. Draper City, 997 P.2d 321, 327 (Utah Ct. App. 2000).
Courts in other jurisdictions have held that PUD approvals are adjudicative or administrative
decisions. See Dillon Companies, Inc., v. City of Boulder, 515 P.2d 627 (Colorado 1973); Neuberger
v. City of Portland, 603 P.2d 771 (Ore. 1979); McCallen v. City of Memphis.°786 S.W.2d 633 (Tenn.
1990); Hirt v. Polk County Bd of County Commissioners, 578 So.2d 415 (Fla. App. 2™ Dist. 1991).
These cases, like the one presently before the Court, involved land use ordinances that
authorized the granting of a PUD if the applicant complied with all the applicable standards and
followed the required procedures. These courts have recognized that the local government’s
legislative discretion was used in forming and adopting the ordinances which allow for a PUD.
Thereafter, when the city or county determines whether the requirements of such ordinances have
been met in connection with a specific PUD application, the governmental bodies were acting
administratively. See Neuberger at 776-77. When the discretionary authority of the government
body must be exercised within existing standards and guidelines, the courts have held that the

governmental unit is acting administratively, not legislatively. See McCallen at 639.

These cases are consistent with the treatment of this issue by Utah’s appellate courts. See
Bradley v. Payson City Corporation, 70 P.3d 47 (Utah 2003), and Springville Citizens for a Better
Community v. City of Springville, 979 P.2d 332 (Utah 1999).

Here, the County’s processing of Cloudrock’s PUD application was a matter of applying pre-
existing standards and zones to the specific land use application before the County. This is the very
essence of “administering” the existing land use code, and end result thereof is administrative, not
legislative. If Grand County had intended that a PUD be a rezone or a new zone classification, the
land use code would have expressly so provided. Instead, the Applicable Land Use Code provides
only that “every PUD District approved [thereunder] . . . shall be considered an amendment to the
zoning map.” (Emphasis supplied.) See Applicable Land Use Code, Art. IIJ.R.3. Moreover, in Grand
County, the underlying zone district of an approved PUD does not change. Rather, the underlying
zone district remains in place and limits and controls not only density but also the uses that are
allowed within the PUD. See Applicable Land Use Code, Art. III.R.4.and 5. Thus, the approval of a
PUD in Grand County does not result in a rezone or the creation of a new zoning Classification, but
rather modifies the existing underlying zone district to “provide flexibility to accommodate mixed-
use, non-traditional and innovative developments . . . and the preservation of natural and scenic
qualities of open spaces.” See Applicable Land Use Code, Art. III-R.1.

Furthermore, the Applicable Land Use Code provides that the “PUD, Planned Unit
Development District may be applied in all zone districts” (emphasis supplied) and the requirement

that an approved PUD be referenced on the Official Zoning Map of the County simply gives notice

Hi. The County’s Failure to Produce the Record Precludes the Court From Ruling on the
Merits of Plaintiffs’ Petition for Review.

Cloudrock’s novel argument that production of the record is not required in this case because
the County’s decision was legislative is without merit and contrary to well-established legal
principles and LUDMA. As a general principle of administrative law, “a court may not reach a
decision without the administrative record.” Koch, Administrative Law and Practice, § 8.27 (an ed.).
Utah law follows this principle. Judicial review of formal administrative rulings requires submission
of the record. See Utah Code Ann. § 63-46b-16. This principle also is followed in LUDMA. Section
17-27a-801(7)(a) of LUDMA states that “the land use authority ... shall transmit to the reviewing

bed

court the record of its proceedings... .” Contrary to Cloudrock’s assertion, Section 17-27a-
801(7)(a) applies regardless of whether the land use authority acted administratively or legislatively.
The distinction between a legislative or administrative decision/ordinance is relevant only in
connection with determining the applicable standard of review. See LUDMA §17-27a-801(3)(b).
Nowhere else in LUDMA is this legislative/administrative dichotomy mentioned or discussed. If the

Utah legislature had intended to excuse land use authorities acting in their legislative capacities from

having to produce a record, then LUDMA would have expressly so provided.

4850-1000-4226MO006-001 9

The County Council is the land use authority in this case because, under the Applicable Land
Use Code, the Council has the authority “to act upon a land use application” such as the amended
PUD application at issue here. See LUDMA §17-27a-103(18) and Applicable Land Use Code, Art.
III.R.2. (the “County Council . . . may authorize the creation of the Planned Unit Development
Districts, subject to .. .”). The fact that only the Planning Commission is specifically identified in the
Grand County Land Use Code as a land use authority is not controlling because the Planning
Commission is not authorized to act on a PUD application other than to make a recommendation

thereon to the County Council which is then authorized to approve the application. Id.

A. The County’s Failure to Comply with the Land Use Code Invalidates the
Adoption of Ordinance 454.

Although Plaintiffs disagree with Cloudrock’s assertion that the PUD approval reflects a
legislative decision to rezone the relevant property, under the Applicable Land Use Code approval of
a PUD and approval of zoning amendments share the same procedural requirements. See Art. VI. B.
The County and Cloudrock failed to adhere to these requirements with regard to approval of the
Cloudrock PUD, making the approval contrary to law (i.e. illegal). First, Cloudrock’s Amended

Master Plan and Preliminary Phase 1 Plat dated October 2006 and submitted to the County did not

an amended preliminary plat and not for a rezoning. See Exh. 4 hereto. Second, while the Planning

In addition to the foregoing, a proposed amended zoning map was not presented at those

fall far short of the required accurate reflection of the text of the zoning amendment. This conclusion

In the Hatch decision, the failure of the Town Council to strictly comply with LUDMA
rendered its zoning ordinance invalid from its inception. In addition, there was no requirement in
Hatch that the appellants demonstrate any prejudice as a result of the failure of the governmental
body to comply with the enabling act in order to prevail. In accordance with the facts as presented
above and the Hatch decision, Ordinance 454 was not a valid rezone and Ordinance 454 is invalid as
a result thereof.

B. Plaintiffs Were Prejudiced by the County’s Failure to Follow the Law.

Plaintiffs acknowledge that the Springville Citizens decision includes a requirement that
plaintiffs generally must establish that they were prejudiced by a governmental entity’s
noncompliance with its ordinances in order to prevail in their challenge of the legality of a PUD. The
Plaintiffs point out, however, that the legal authority cited in the Springville Citizens decision does
not support such a requirement. First, in Board of Education v. Salt Lake County, the showing of
prejudice due to noncompliance with the law served as one of three bases for finding that compliance
with the statute in that instance was mandatory, not directory. A showing of prejudice in Salt Lake

County was not required in order for the plaintiffs therein to prevail. Similarly, Anderson’s American

4850-1000-4226MO006-001 12

Law of Zoning § 11.24, deals with the injury that must be shown to establish standing to challenge a
PUD approval and not the injury that must be demonstrated to be entitled to relief when a
governmental entity fails to strictly comply with the law. Moreover, in 2001, the Utah Court of
Appeals invalidated a zoning ordinance when the Boulder Town Council failed to strictly comply
with the statutory requirement to prepare a zoning map to accompany a zoning amendment. Hatch v.

Boulder Town Council, 21 P3d 245 (Utah App. 2001). In the Hatch decision, the Court did not

require that the appellants show prejudice in order to prevail.

Notwithstanding the foregoing, Plaintiffs are prejudiced by the County’s noncompliance with
the mandatory provisions of its own land use code for the following reasons. The County Council is a
public body that has a duty to the citizens of Grand County to adhere to the law for the benefit of the
general public. Accordingly, it must be assumed that if the County Council had fully understood and
appreciated the approval process and the findings it was required by law to make in connection with
Cloudrock’s amended PUD application, the County Council would not have approved Ordinance
454. Cloudrock’s amended PUD application clearly does not comply with the requirements of the
Applicable Land Use Code and the County Council failed to apply the applicable provisions thereof
in its review and approval of the application. In failing to adhere to its own land use code, the Council
put the interests of a developer ahead of the general welfare of the County and failed to address major
issues related to the Cloudrock development that could adversely affect the health, welfare, livelihood
and/or property values of the Plaintiffs who have standing to bring this action.

Despite the wording of Ordinance 454, a number of the findings contained therein are simply
contrary to the facts of this case. First, the amended PUD is not consistent with the Public Facilities

CONCLUSION

The County’s adoption of Ordinance 454 is best characterized as an administrative, rather
than legislative, act. Accordingly, Plaintiffs are entitled to have their appeal heard by the Grand
County Board of Adjustment prior to the Court’s consideration. Although review of the record could
aid in the Court’s decision on whether to remand to the BOA, because Plaintiffs are required to
exhaust their administrative remedies before obtaining Court review, this is a threshold jurisdictional
issue that must be decided even if the County continues to disregard its duty to produce the record. If
the Court were to decide to rule on the merits of Plaintiffs’ appeal notwithstanding the lack of a
record, Plaintiffs still should prevail because the County actions were arbitrary, capricious, and

illegal.

AN ORDINANCE AMENDING THE GRAND COUNTY LAND USE CODE,
ART. lil.C.H., DUDE RANCH, WILDERNESS LODGE AND SIMILAR REC. FACILITIES

WHEREAS, the Grand County General Plan ("General Plan”) was adopted by the Grand County Council on
August 5, 1996, with Resolution #2301;

WHEREAS, the Grand County Land Use Code (“Land Use Code”) was adopted by the Grand County Council on
January 4, 1999 with Ordinance No. 299, Series 1999, for the purpose of regulating land use, subdivision and
development in Grand County in accordance with Grand County General Plan;

WHEREAS, the amendments contained herein are designed to: replace “Wilderness Lodge” with the term
“Destination Resort’, and delete the vaque term of “similar recreational facilities”; increase the County’s discretion
with respect to such uses by requiring that the County make specific findings as a prerequisite to the approval of
bonus density and/or accommodations unit size; clarify that the list of accessory uses includes the plural of these
uses; standardize public trail and/or road dedication requirements; limit the height of accommodations units, but
not that of main lodge structures; limit total number of primary structures in the interest of promoting clustered
development; mandate varied roof lines and wall off-set requirements in order to limit the perceived mass and
scale of structures; and require provision of guest food service and a appropriate recreational amenity package in
all cases;

WHEREAS, the Planning Commission reviewed in public work sessions on April 4 and May 2, 2001, and
subsequently recommended adoption of this ordinance following a public hearing on June 6, 2001;

WHEREAS, due notice was given that the Grand County Council (“Council”) would meet to hear and consider this
ordinance; and
‘ _) WHEREAS, the Council has heard and considered all evidence and testimony presented with respect to the
amendments and has determined, subsequent to said public hearing that the adoption of this ordinance is in the
best interests of the citizens of the Grand County, Utah.

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT ORDAINED BY THE COUNTY COUNCIL OF GRAND COUNTY, UTAH, THAT the
Grand County Land Use Code is hereby modifies the Schedule of Use Regulations of Art. [Il.C.1. with respect to
“Dude ranch, wilderness lodge and similar recreational facilities” , to read as follows:

h. Dude ranch or destination resort. All dude ranches and destination resorts shall comply with the
standards of this section:

(1) Accommodation units may be provided for temporary overnight occupants or employees;

(2) Accessory facilities may include a restaurants, gift shops, employee housing, equestrian
facilities and health spas;

(3) All structures and parking shall be substantially hidden from view off-site from public rights-of-
way and high use areas through use of siting, screening, landscaping, or setbacks;

(4) Utilize earth-tone colors with low light reflective values that minimize contrast with the
surrounding landscape;

(5) The proposed project and accommodations density shall be compatible with the surrounding land
uses and suitable for the proposed site;

(6) Utilize indigenous, regional architecture styles and materials, or as otherwise approved by the
Planning Commission;

(7) Provide adequate public facilities and services based on the type and scope of the development
proposed; a private water and/or sewerage system may be required where the capacity of
available public water system and/or sewage treatment system is insufficient to serve current
zoned density;

(8) Provide public trai! and/or road right-of-way dedications as necessary to maintain historic
access to public lands;

(9) Limit the height of all accommodations units to 24 ft. and the main lodge structure to 35 ft.;

(10) Limit the number of primary structures on site to an average of one (1) per five (5) acres;

(11) Incorporate varied roof lines and wall off-sets as necessary to break-up the perceived mass
and scale of all structures larger than a typical single family home;

(12) Provide complete food service for all guests;

(13) Provide a recreational amenity package suitable for the scale of the project.

(14) The density of accommodations units shall be limited to one (1) per 2.5 acres, and the average
size of all accommodations units shall not exceed 600 sq. ft.; provided, however, that the County
may permit “bonus” density up to one (1) unit per acre and/or “bonus” average unit size, where
the County specifically finds that a proposed development will provide significant and substantial
public benefit, as compared to alternative uses for the subject property, by exceeding county
standards with respect to: ;

(a) protection of sensitive lands on the site;

(b) dedication of view shed easements across the property from high public use areas;
(c) dedication of 80% or more of the site as open space;

(d) protection and enhancement of wildlife habitat;

(e) diversification of the local economy; and

(f} preserve agricultural lands.
