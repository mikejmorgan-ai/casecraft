*7 Introduction
On December 14, 2005, the Preserve at Mapleton Development Company (“Preserve”), acquired approximately 170 acres
of property (“Property”) in Mapleton, Utah (“Mapleton” or “City”). The next day, December 15, 2005, the Preserve filed an
application with Mapleton to rezone the Property and reduce development density. On May 17, 2006, the Mapleton City Council
approved the zoning change requested by the Preserve and adopted the PRC-4 zone for the Property. The Preserve itself drafted
the zoning text for the PRC-4 zone. That text expressly restricts the maximum number of homes on the Property to 92 units.
It is undisputed that Mapleton only rezoned the Property with the owner's knowing consent and approval, capping density at
92 units. There are no contrary inferences.
Before the Preserve limited the density on its Property to 92 units, the zoning was A-2 (TDR-R). This meant that the Property
had an agricultural zoning allowing one home for every two acres, with the right to apply transferable development rights to
increase the density of development to allow up to 136 units. In Mapleton, a transferable development right is created when a
property owner restricts development on some property in exchange for a transferable development right or TDR that can be
applied to increase development density on approved properties elsewhere in Mapleton.
The A-2 (TDR-R) zoning was applied to the Property in 2003 by Mapleton in harmony with a development agreement
(“Original Agreement”) between Mapleton and the prior owner of the Property, Suburban Land Reserve (“SLR”). In the Original
Agreement, SLR agreed to restrict development on a portion of its Property in return for *8 77 TDRs and the rezoning of
another portion of the Property as a TDR receiving site that would allow up to 136 units on the Property. This was the zoning
density until the Preserve purchased and reduced it, in favor of large, estate-type lots.

In 2006, when Mapleton City rezoned the property to PRC-4, as requested by the Property owner, it expressly restricted density
to 92 units. The PRC-4 zone did not add the TDR-R designation because the Preserve could develop all 92 units on the Property
without expending TDRs. This left the Preserve free to sell its TDRs to others or gain value from them by applying them in
another zone with a TDR-R designation. The Preserve intentionally waived any contractual or other development right it had
to more than 92 units and even agreed to that change in a written memorandum of understanding with Mapleton. The Preserve
would be estopped from claiming breach of contract and so should LDIII.
LDIII entered the picture when it acquired the Property through foreclosure in 2008. Almost a decade later, in 2017, LDIII
sought to increase the development density on the Property back to what SLR had. The Mapleton City Council agreed to
rezone the Property with a TDR-R designation in the summer of 2017. In addition, due to the express language in the PRC-4
zone restricting development to 92 units, the Council would also need to modify the PRC-4 zoning text to allow increased
development density on the Property. However, before the PRC-4 zoning text could be modified, citizens in Mapleton sought
a referendum to overturn the Council's legislative action to rezone the property with a TDR-R designation. When sufficient
signatures were obtained, Mapleton *9 called a referendum election, which was held in November 2017. That election resulted
in overturning the Council's action to apply the TDR-R designation to the Property.
Only after the November 2017 election, and more than a decade after the Property had been rezoned to PRC-4 without a TDRR designation, LDIII belatedly sued Mapleton for breach of the Original Agreement and for an order declaring that at all times
since 2003, the Property was zoned to be a TDR receiving site and that the density rights granted to SLR were left unchanged
by the Preserve's seeking and obtaining Mapleton's approval for a new zoning and new development approval. This lawsuit was
untimely. Any defect in the rezoning and any breach of contract occurred a decade earlier and must have been raised before
2017. The District Court correctly entered summary judgment that LDIII's lawsuit was untimely and that any right to greater
density was waived or amended by the Preserve.

*14 Statement of the Case
I. Facts
1. In 2003, Suburban Land Reserve (“SLR”) owned approximately 245 acres of real property in Mapleton (“Property”). R249.
2. On May 7, 2003, SLR executed an agreement with Mapleton regarding the development of the Property (“Original
Agreement”). This agreement contemplated a specific development plan for the Property, with terms for density, lot layouts,
and design elements. R251-253.
3. To carry out this development plan, the parties agreed that SLR would transfer 76 acres of the Property to Mapleton to be
re-zoned a Transferable Development Right sending site. SLR and Mapleton agreed that 15 units could have been built on the
76 acres, and that SLR would receive density bonuses for the transfer “on a 5:1 ratio.” Accordingly, SLR was “entitled to have
seventy-five (75) density units transferred to the remainder” of the Property. SLR ultimately received 77 TDRs that it could use
on any property zoned to receive increased density from TDRs, including its own Property or other approved locations within
Mapleton. R250-251, 252-253, 269.
4. Mapleton zoned the remaining approximately 170 acres of the Property as A-2 (TDR-R), meaning it was a Transferable
Development Right receiving site that could be developed with TDRs. R250-251.
5. Under the A-2 zone, the maximum density allowed is one lot per two acres, which would have permitted approximately 85
lots on the 170 acres. With a TDR-R overlay and the use of TDRs, the density could be doubled to one lot per one acre. MCC
*15 18.28.050(A), 18.76.070(B); R352.
6. The Original Agreement provides that, after transferring the 76 acres to Mapleton and “[w]ith the transfer of density completed
by the zoning of the Property to the A-2 (TDR-R) Zone, [SLR] has a vested right to develop a maximum of one hundred thirtysix (136) single family residential units on individual lots.” R252.
7. Paragraph 10 provides that SLR's rights under the Original Agreement were “personal” and “shall only run with the land so
long as [SLR or a related entity] shall own and be the Owner of the Property.” It also gave Mapleton the right to approve any
transfer of the rights granted by the Original Agreement: “Only upon the express prior written approval by the City, shall any
rights of Owner with respect to the potion of the Property being sold be deemed transferred to the new owner thereof.” R254.
8. On December 14, 2005, SLR transferred the Property to Craig Thueson, on the condition that Mapleton grant “approval to
the assignment as required under the [Original] Agreement.” R298, 301-302, 473-479.
9. The same day, Thueson transferred the Property to The Preserve at Mapleton Development Company (“Preserve”). R481-488.
10. SLR also transferred 67 of its 77 TDRs to the Preserve. R269-283.
11. The next day, December 15, 2005, Nathan Simpson on behalf of the Preserve applied to rezone the Property from A-2
to PRC-4 in order to develop 55 units. There is no reference in the application regarding the retention of the TDR-R overlay
zoning. R304.
12. The PRC-4 zone is properly titled, “Preserve at Mapleton Subdivision, *16 Planned Residential Community (PRC-4)
Zone.” A “PRC” zone is a master-planned development that has specific terms and conditions that are contained in a zoning
ordinance tailored to the specific development. The only land in Mapleton subject to the PRC-4 zone is the Property. R285-290,
352, 368.

This Court should affirm the District Court's grant of summary judgment to Mapleton because there are no disputes of fact in this
case. The record in this case is made up almost entirely of contracts, public meeting minutes, deeds, and city ordinances. There is
no dispute over what the public record shows, and there can be no dispute over the reasonable inferences to be drawn therefrom.
The Preserve was the authorized assignee and transferee of property and development rights granted by Mapleton to SLR in
2003. But the Preserve did not want SLR's development. It had its own plan, and it worked with Mapleton for twenty months
to implement that plan. In so doing, the Preserve permanently modified and relinquished any development or contract right
it inherited from SLR that did not align with its plan. Most critically, the Preserve applied for, drafted, and obtained approval
of a new PRC-4 zoning ordinance for the Property that expressly restricts development density to 92 units. The District Court
correctly determined that the Preserve's actions waived or amended the rights it obtained from SLR, and LDIII cannot reclaim
those rights.

A. The Preserve Waived Its Right to Develop More than 92 Units on the Property
The District Court correctly concluded that the Preserve had waived any right to develop 136 units on the Property and LDIII was
bound by the Preserve's waiver. “[W]aiver is the intentional relinquishment of a known right. To constitute waiver, there must
be [1] an existing right, benefit or advantage, [2] a knowledge of its existence, and [3] an intention to relinquish it.” McCleve
Props., LLC v. D. Ray Hult Family Ltd., 2013 UT App 185, ¶ 10, 307 P.3d 650 (citation omitted). Rights that have been waived
may not thereafter be reclaimed or enforced by the waiving party or others that claim through the waiving party. Sunridge Dev.
Corp. v. RB&G Eng. Inc., 2010 UT 6, ¶ 13, 230 P.3d 1000 (“[An] assignee is subject to any defenses that would have been good
against the [assignor].”) Cheney v. Rucker, 381 P.2d 86, 90-91 (Utah 1963) (“[An assignee] could have nothing more than his
assignor and is bound by any waiver, relinquishment or change of its rights which had occurred.”); Drazich v. Lasson, 964 P.2d
324, 327 (Utah Ct. App. 1998). While “waiver is intensely fact-dependent, … a district court may grant summary judgment in
favor of a party on the issue of waiver” so long as there are no genuine issues of fact “whether the totality of the circumstances
warrants the inference of relinquishment.” IHC Health Servs. v. D&K Mgmt., Inc., 2008 UT 73, ¶¶ 15, 18-19, 196 P.3d 588.
Further, “unlike most cases, the legal conclusions underlying a trial court's *28 grant of summary judgment on a waiver issue
are reviewed with some measure of deference.” IHC Health Servs. v. D&K Mgmt., Inc., 2003 UT 5, ¶ 6, 73 P.3d 320.
As the District Court correctly concluded, there are no disputes over the first two elements of waiver. First, there was “an
existing right, benefit or advantage”: the Original Agreement granted to SLR, and then to its assignee the Preserve, the right
“to develop a maximum of one hundred thirty-six (136) single family residential units on individual lots.” R252. Second, the
Preserve had “knowledge of its existence”: The Preserve's acquisition of the Property was conditioned on Mapleton approving
the assignment of the Original Agreement to the Preserve, and later the Preserve executed a MOU with Mapleton acknowledging
the Preserve's status as SLR's assignee.

“It is well-settled law that the parties to a contract may, by mutual consent, alter all or any portion of that contract by agreeing

is there for the plaintiff, assignee of [a contract], to rescind [the amendment] and go back to the original … agreement? We
think the trial court correctly concluded that there was none.” Cheney, 381 P.2d at 90. An assignee can “have nothing more than
his assignor and is bound by any waiver, relinquishment or change of its rights which had occurred by virtue of its execution
of the new agreement.” Id. at 91. All that is required to show that a binding, permanent amendment has occurred is proof of
“a meeting of the minds of the parties, which must be spelled out, either expressly or impliedly, with sufficient definiteness.”

a separate compromise contract when they encountered problems with drilling the well. This compromise contract recognized
and incorporated the original contract, though it provided new and different payment terms. Id. at 197. The parties disputed
the amount owed to the driller, with the driller asserting entitlement to the payment terms defined by the compromise contract
rather than the terms set forth in the original contract. Id. at 197-98. The Utah Supreme Court agreed with the driller, holding
that the compromise agreement was an amendment to the first contract. As such, the compromise contract, “in conjunction with
those terms of the original contract it [did] not contradict, create[d] a substitute contract and thereby extinguish[ed] any rights
under the original contract now defined by the amendment.” Id. at 198.

The District Court correctly ruled that, regardless of the substance of LDIII's claims, all such claims are barred by the fact that
LDIII failed to raise any claim in a timely manner. The crucial events regarding the zoning and density rights of the Property
all occurred between December 2005 and August 2007. LDIII had a security interest in the Property as early as December 6,

A. LDIII's Claims Are Barred by the 30-Day Limitation for Land Use Claims

use decisions and regulations be brought within 30 days. Both the Mapleton City Code and the Utah Land Use Act impose a
30-day window for any person adversely affected by the City Council's land use decision or regulation to file a challenge. MCC
18.84.460(D) (“Any person aggrieved by any decision of the city council may have and maintain a plenary action for relief
therefrom in any court of competent jurisdiction; provided petition for such relief is presented to the court within thirty (30) days

made in the exercise of or in violation of the provisions of this chapter may file … with the district court within 30 days after
the decision is final.”); id. § 10-9a-801(5) *39 (“[A] challenge to the enactment of a land use regulation or general plan may
not be filed with the district court more than 30 days after the enactment.”). LDIII's claims regarding the Property's density and
zoning are in fact challenges to the City Council's actions from 2005-2007, when Mapleton granted the Preserve's requests to
modify the development rights of the Property. LDIII's claims also challenge Mapleton's actions in May and June of 2017, when
the City Council affirmed that the TDR-R zoning overlay had been removed, the maximum density allowed on the Property

2017, City Council meeting when the City Council discussed the history of the Property, the MOU, the density restrictions,
and whether to rezone the Property again. R459. Both the Preserve and LDIII had actual and constructive notice of Mapleton's
actions, and that is sufficient to trigger the limitations period. See Fox v. Park City, 2008 UT 85, ¶¶ 34, 39-42, 200 P.3d 182.
Under City Code and Utah law, LDIII's was required to raise its claims within 30 days of Mapleton's actions. Because it did
not do so in 2006, 2007, or 2017, its claims are time-barred.
B. LDIII's Claims Are Barred by the 6-Year Limitation for Contract Claims
As Mapleton argued below, LDIII's claims are barred by the 6-year limitation period for contract claims provided by Utah Code
section 78B-2-309(1)(b). R230-231, 1043. LDIII's principal claim is that it is entitled to develop up to 136 units on the *41
Property, as set forth in the Original Agreement. Assuming, arguendo, LDIII is correct, Mapleton would have breached the
Original Agreement when it imposed a density cap of 92 units on the Property (at the Preserve's express request). That limit
was initially put in place on May 17, 2006, when the City Council approved rezoning the Property to PRC-4 for up to 92 units,
and confirmed on August 7, 2007, when the City Council adopted the Preserve's zoning text for the PRC-4 zone that included
the express density restriction. Accordingly, any claim that Mapleton improperly restricted density on the Property in violation
of the Original Agreement must have been brought by August 7, 2013.
C. LDIII Should Be Equitably Estopped from Raising Its Claims Due to
the Preserve's Relinquishment of Rights and LDIII's Acquiescence Thereto
As Mapleton argued to the District Court below, LDIII should be equitably estopped from claiming development rights that
were relinquished by the Preserve. R231- 234. Ultimately, at some point LDIII must be prohibited from challenging meetings,
actions, and decisions that occurred over a decade ago. Even without a specific statute of limitation, LDIII's acquiescence to the
development and density restrictions of the PRC-4 zone should estop LDIII from raising challenges to those restrictions now.
Estoppel is an equitable doctrine which precludes parties from asserting their rights where their actions render it inequitable to
allow them to assert those rights. Estoppel requires proof of three elements: (1) a statement, admission, act, or failure to act by
one party inconsistent with a later-asserted claim; (2) the other party's reasonable action or inaction based upon the first party's
statement, admission, act, or failure to act; and (3) injury to the second party that would result from allowing the first party to
contradict or repudiate its statement, admission, act, or failure to act.

Burrow v. Vrontikis, 788 P.2d 1046, 1048 (Utah Ct. App. 1990) (citation omitted). A party's failure to “make any request or to
take any action” regarding a particular right for *42 many years, coupled with affirmative statements that the party would not
seek to enforce that right warrants estopping the party from enforcing that right later. Id.

IV. The District Court Correctly Determined that the TDR-R Zoning
Overlay Was Removed When Mapleton Rezoned the Property
The District Court correctly concluded that the TDR-R overlay on the Property was removed as part of the PRC-4 rezone.
The District Court's discussion of this removal focused on whether Mapleton could modify zoning issues through the general
ordinance procedure. That discussion is, in Mapleton's view, beside the point. Nothing in LDIII's *43 complaint or its briefing
on the motions for summary judgment, motion to amend judgment, or appeal suggests Mapleton failed to follow the appropriate
procedures for adopting the PRC-4 zone, whether through the general ordinance procedure or the more specific procedures
for land use ordinances, and LDIII may not challenge Mapleton's procedures in adopting the PRC-4 zone for the first time on
appeal.5 LDIII's sole challenge regarding the rezone is that Mapleton did not provide special notice that the TDR-R overlay
would be removed in connection with the otherwise admittedly proper rezoning of the Property, and that such failure means
Mapleton did not and could not have removed the TDR-R overlay. The District Court properly recognized that Mapleton could
and did remove the TDR-R overlay when it rezoned the Property to PRC-4 without any such special notice.
It is undisputed that Mapleton gave notice of the rezoning of the Property. The staff report to the Planning Commission
specifically states that the rezoning was “re-public noticed” and that “[a] letter ha[d] been sent to all property owners within
500 feet of the proposed rezone as required by law.” R490. Mapleton does not need to use specific magic words in providing
notice of a rezoning. The Utah Supreme Court addressed this issue in Suarez v. Grand County, 2012 UT 72, 296 P.3d 688.
There, citizens complained that Grand County had failed to provide sufficient notice of a rezoning because the notice stated that
a “hearing would be about ‘Amended Master Plan and Amended Preliminary Phase 1 Plat’ for ‘a Planned Unit Development,”’
but “did not specifically refer to a *44 rezoning, zoning amendment, zone boundary change, a PUD amendment, a zoning
ordinance, or a zoning code.” Id. ¶ 69. The Court held that the notice was sufficient to “inform the public that the Council would
be making major decisions concerning the … [d]evelopment,” and that is all that is required. Id. ¶ 76.
The record shows, and LDIII has not challenged, that Mapleton gave notice that it was considering rezoning the Property to
the PRC-4 zone. That is sufficient. There is no requirement that Mapleton give additional, specific notice regarding the TDRR zone or use special language specifically identifying every aspect of the Property's zoning that could have been affected by
Mapleton's action. Indeed, Utah Code section 10-9a-201(1) states that, “[a]t a minimum, each municipality shall provide actual
notice or the notice required by this part.” The Preserve had actual notice as its representative, Nate Simpson, was present at
the May 17, 2006, City Council meeting.6 R307. Any defect in the notice given by Mapleton could be and was waived by the
Preserve's proceeding with the rezoning, and, at this point, all notices are deemed to be adequate and proper. UTAH CODE §
109-9a-202(2), -209. Accordingly, Mapleton gave sufficient and proper notice of the potential removal of the TDR-R overlay,
and the undisputed facts show that Mapleton did remove the overlay.
Mapleton City Code requires rezoning applications to include “[d]esignation of the specific zone change or title amendment
desired,” and the rezoning application submitted by the Preserve sought only “PRC-4.” R304; MCC 18.12.010(A)(1). The staff
report to *45 the Planning Commission noted that the Property was zoned “A2 and A2 (TDR-R),” as it “was previously given
a zone designation of A2 TDR-R,” but that the zone sought by the Preserve was just the “PRC-4 Zone.” R490-491. The rezoning
was recommended by the Planning Commission and approved by the City Council. R306-307, 311, 490-492. Mapleton's official

zoning maps, which are just “as much a part of [the City's code] as if the boundary lines and other references and information
set forth thereon were fully described [therein],” show the Property zoned as A-2 (TDR-R) in 2006, but just PRC-4, with no

Further, the removal of the TDR-R overlay as part of the rezoning was reasonable given that the PRC-4 zone, as drafted and
applied for by the Preserve, is incompatible with the use of TDRs. Every PRC zone is designed for an individual, master-planned
development, with an individualized zoning text that specifically identifies the maximum allowable density for the development
agreed upon by Mapleton and the developer. Once that density is set, “no new subdivision lots shall be permitted beyond that
which was originally approved, for the purpose of increasing the density.” MCC 18.82.030(K). It is the specific zoning text,
with its specific density restriction, that ultimately governs development, rather than the general standards for PRC zones. MCC
18.82.050 (“The applicant or developer of each PRC zone may create a zone text for the development that is more restrictive
than this code. The governing text of each development that is more restrictive shall apply.”). So while PRC zones are, as a
general matter, eligible for TDR density bonuses, those bonuses must be included in the maximum allowable density set out
in the individualized PRC zoning text because no additional lots can be approved that *46 would increase the density of the
development beyond the density cap, with or without TDRs.7 If, after the fact, a developer wants to use TDRs, the zoning text
would need to be amended through the usual legislative process to increase the density.
As LDIII acknowledges, the PRC-4 zone does not require the use of TDRs to obtain its maximum allowable density of 92 units
across 200+ acres. R352, 523-524. Because the agreed-upon maximum density of the PRC-4 zone did not include a potential
bonus from the use of TDRs, the only way TDRs can be used on the Property is if the PRC-4 zone is amended to allow more
density. R352, 435. This was the City Council's plan when it approved the re-application of the TDR-R overlay to the Property
in 2017. R352, 435, 512-513. Accordingly, a TDR-R overlay on the Property is incompatible with the PRC-4 zone. R579. The
only reasonable inferences to be drawn from these undisputed facts is that the 2006 rezoning removed the A-2 and the TDRR zoning designations and replaced them with the specific rights and restrictions of the PRC-4 zone. Accordingly, the District
Court correctly determined that the TDR-R overlay was removed from the Property.
V. The District Court Correctly Determined that LDIII Did Not Succeed to the Original Agreement
As an alternative basis for granting summary judgment in favor of Mapleton, the *47 District Court concluded that LDIII did
not succeed to the Preserve's rights under the Original Agreement.8 As the District Court recognized, LDIII's acquisition of
the Property through foreclosure does not entitle it to the Preserve's development and contract rights. The general rule is that a
particular zoning or development right “is not a static thing which once established becomes set in concrete forever.” Smith Inv.
Co. v. Sandy City, 958 P.2d 245, 258 n.19 (Utah Ct. App. 1998). Mapleton's approval of SLR's development plan did “not create
independent free-floating vested property rights” that could be exercised by any other owner of the Property without Mapleton's
approval. Keith v. Mt. Resorts Dev., LLC, 2014 UT 32, ¶¶ 31-35, 337 P.3d 213.
The only way Mapleton's original authorization for SLR to develop 136 units on the Property may be claimed by LDIII now is if
it somehow succeeded to and is entitled to exercise the rights granted by the Original Agreement unaffected by any intervening
amendment or waiver of those rights. As discussed above, LDIII is bound by the Preserve's waiver and amendment of the rights
granted by the Original Agreement, which limits any rights it has to the Original Agreement. However, even assuming arguendo
that there was no relinquishment of rights, LDIII still must show that it succeeded to the Preserve's contractual rights under the
Original Agreement. The undisputed facts and the interpretation of the Original Agreement confirm that LDIII did *48 not
succeed to the Preserve's contractual rights, LDIII is not an assignee of the Original Agreement, and the Original Agreement
does not run with the land.
A. LDIII's Acquisition of the Property through Foreclosure Did Not Transfer the Preserve's Contract Rights

LDIII claims that it succeeded to the Preserve's rights under the Original Agreement when it foreclosed on and purchased
the Property at the trustee's sale. However, a party's acquisition of property that is the subject of a contract does not, by
itself, make that party an assignee of agreements related to the property. There must be a specific assignment of any such
agreements in addition to the acquisition of property rights. Hillcrest Inv. Co. v. Utah DOT, 2012 UT App 256, ¶¶ 15-18, 287
P.3d 427 (holding that purchase of property did not assign a related contract because the purchase agreement did not describe
the contract or have any assignment-like language); West v. Case, 2006 UT App 325, ¶¶ 18-21, 142 P.3d 576 (holding that
“a quit-claim grantee merely becomes a successor to the grantor's property interest” when “there [is] no formal assignment of
the Agreement”); Oquirrh Assocs. v. First Nat'l Leasing, 888 P.2d 659, 663-64 (Utah Ct. App. 1994) (holding that a contract's
language binding “‘successors and assigns' [only refers] to those who succeed to one party's interest in the contract through
inheritance, assignment, or the like,” and does not assign the contract to someone who “merely became a successor to the …
property interest”). Hansen v. Green River Grp., 748 P.2d 1102, 1104 & n.2 (Utah Ct. App. 1988) (holding that a sub-purchase
contract did not assign certain rights because it lacked “the usual words of either an assumption or an assignment, such as
‘assumes,’ ‘agrees to pay,’ ‘assigns,’ ‘transfers' or ‘conveys.”’).
*49 LDIII's acquisition of the Preserve's property interests did not grant it the Preserve's contract rights because nothing in
the contract between LDIII and the Preserve specifically assigned those rights. There are none of “the usual words of either
an assumption or an assignment” of development or density rights. Hansen, 748 P.2d at 1104. Neither the Original Agreement
nor the MOU is described in the property that secured the Preserve's debt, and LDIII acknowledged that the trust deed does not
reference the Original Agreement. R339-347, 525. LDIII points to the trust deed's boilerplate language describing the property
it conveyed,9 though LDIII does not provide any meaningful analysis of this language. LDIII simply asserts that this language
did not “carve out or withhold” the assignment of the contract rights. This flips the standard on its head. LDIII must show that
its agreement with the Preserve included an assignment of the Original Agreement, not just that it did not preclude such an
assignment. If the Preserve intended to assign its contract rights, “such a provision should have and could have been stated in
the contract with specificity.” Hansen, 748 P.2d at 1104.
Regardless, even construing LDIII's foreclosure of the Property as an assignment of the Preserve's contract rights, such an
assignment is invalid because it was not approved by the City. Unlike the transfer of the Property between SLR and the Preserve,
LDIII was never approved as an assignee of the Original Agreement and there is no agreement with Mapleton regarding the
rights LDIII would have as the owner of the *50 Property. R268, 527. Accordingly, the District Court correctly concluded
that LDIII is not a successor to the Original Agreement.
B. The Original Agreement Did Not Run With the Land Without Mapleton's Prior, Written Approval
LDIII also argues that it may claim the density benefits granted in the Original Agreement because the Original Agreement
runs with the land to all future owners of the Property. Again, this is an issue that requires the Court to first determine that the
Preserve did not amend or waive its development rights under the Original Agreement. But even assuming that the development
rights granted by Original Agreement in 2003 inexplicably passed through the Preserve's ownership of the Property unaltered
by the Preserve's actions, the plain language of the Original Agreement provides that it runs with the Property only to those
who have been previously approved by Mapleton. Because LDIII concedes it never sought or obtained Mapleton's approval to
its alleged assumption of the Original Agreement, the District Court correctly granted summary judgment to the City.
In order for a contractual covenant to run with the land such that it binds or benefits future land owners even without a specific
assignment of the covenant, “(1) the covenant must touch and concern the land affected by the covenant, (2) the original parties
to the covenant must have expressly or impliedly intended the covenant to run with the land, (3) there must be privity of estate,
and (4) the covenant ‘must be in writing.”’ Stern v. Metro. Water Dist., 2012 UT 16, ¶ 40, 274 P.3d 935 (citation omitted). In
this case, there is no dispute over the first, third, and fourth elements. The benefits *51 granted by the Original Agreement
touch and concern the Property, there is privity of estate between SLR and LDIII, and the Original Agreement is in writing. The
issue lies in whether Mapleton and SLR intended for the Original Agreement to run with the land. “[A]n express statement in
a deed or testimony from the original covenantors that a covenant is intended to run (or not to run) with the land is normally

sufficient” to resolve the issue. Id. ¶ 43. As discussed below, the Original Agreement's statement that “Owner's rights under this
agreement shall be personal to Owner and shall only run with the land” in limited circumstances is such an express statement.
The interpretation of the Original Agreement is in this case a purely legal question. Gillmor v. Macey, 2005 UT App 351, ¶
9, 121 P.3d 351. In interpreting the contract, “[i]t is axiomatic that a contract should be interpreted so as to harmonize all of
its provisions and all of its terms, which terms should be given effect if it is possible to do so.” Id. ¶ 19 (citation omitted).
While extrinsic evidence may be offered to show ambiguity, “the judge must ensure that ‘the interpretations contended for are
reasonably supported by the language of the contract.” Dairies v. Vincent, 2008 UT 51, ¶ 26, 190 P.3d 1269 (citation omitted).
“[W]ords and phrases do not qualify as ambiguous simply because one party seeks to endow them with a different interpretation
according to his or her own interests.” Saleh v. Farmers Ins. Exch, 2006 UT 20, ¶ 17, 133 P.3d 428. Accordingly, “[i]f any
apparent ambiguity or conflict can be resolved by harmonizing the ambiguous or conflicting terms, a court may not conclude
as a matter of law that the contract is ambiguous and take extrinsic evidence to clarify the problem.” Gillmor, 2005 UT App
351, ¶ 19 n.8.
*52 In Gillmor v. Macey, a contract granted a right “for access by Gillmor and his immediate family to the first degree
of consanguinity, and their spouses and children, to the Gillmor property.” Id. ¶ 15. The contract described these rights as
“‘covenants and restrictions which run with [the properties],’ which “shall be binding upon and shall inure to the benefit of all
present and future owners and/or purchasers, occupants and lessees of said Properties.” Id. ¶ 17 n.6. The contract also stated
that it “shall be binding upon and shall inure to the benefit of the parties …, their officers, directors, invitees, agents, employees,
heirs, personal representatives, successors, assigns, owners, occupants and lessees of the subject properties.” Id. Nevertheless,
the Court of Appeals held, “[w]hile the Agreement states, in several places, that the grants and limitations in the Agreement are
appurtenant to the land and run with the land to future successors in interest, the Agreement also details a specific and limited
class of individuals who are to be benefitted.” Id. ¶ 17. By harmonizing all of the relevant provisions of the contract, the Court
held as a matter of law that the contract's specification of a class of persons to be benefited by the contract “undermines any
notion that this benefit is somehow intended to run with the land to the benefit of all future owners of the … property.” Id. ¶ 17.
The same is true here. The parties to the Original Agreement did not intend to create free-floating rights that would continue in
perpetuity to anyone and everyone that acquired the Property. While certain parts of the Original Agreement suggest in general
manner that the Agreement could benefit future owners, “the Agreement also details a specific and limited class of individuals
who are to be benefitted.” Id. ¶ 17.
Paragraph 10 of the Original Agreement provides:

VI. The District Court Correctly Determined the Re-Application of the TDR-R Overlay Was Legislative Referable
Finally, LDIII challenges the propriety of calling the referendum election, but only after the election was held and the voters
sided against LDIII's wishes. Again, LDIII's claims are both untimely and substantively baseless. LDIII asserts that Mapleton's
action in June 2017 to rezone the Property with a TDR-R overlay was an administrative act, not *55 legislative. However,
LDIII was well aware that citizens had obtained the necessary signatures for a referendum and Mapleton called an election on
the issue for November 7, 2017. If LDIII disagreed with Mapleton's conclusion that the citizens were entitled to vote, then it
was obligated to file suit and challenge it before the election was held. Utah Code section 20A-7-607(4)(c) provides a remedy
to anyone who believes that “any petition filed is not legally sufficient.” LDIII gambled that it would prevail at the ballot box.
It did not. Only after the election results came back against it did LDIII file its lawsuit claiming that the election never should
have been held. LDIII cites Baker v. Carlson, 2018 UT 59, 437 P.3d 333 for support, but it cuts against it because in Baker, the
challenge to the election came before the vote, not afterward. Id. ¶¶ 6-7. There is no basis for LDIII to challenge a lawfully and
democratically held referendum election after the fact simply because it did not like the way the votes came out.
LDIII's claims regarding the referendum are also substantively defective. “[W]hen a city council exercises its legislative
authority, voters retain the constitutional prerogative of challenging its decisions by referendum.” Krejci v. City of Saratoga
Springs, 2013 UT 74, ¶ 21, 322 P.3d 662. The Utah Supreme Court has held that applying a zoning designation to a parcel of
land - site-specific zoning - is “a legislative act - and thus subject to referendum.” Id. ¶ 38. Under Mapleton City Code, a TDR-R
overlay is a type of zoning designation. MCC 18.24.010. As such, it is a rule of general applicability. Had the voters supported
it, the TDR-R zoning designation would have applied to all present and future owners of the Property. Krejci, 2013 UT 74, ¶ 32.
In rezoning the Property in 2017, the City Council weighed numerous, broad policy *56 considerations in the detailed public
hearing held immediately before its decision, including factors beyond the Property boundaries, such as development density
on adjoining property and the impact the development would have on the general public. R 138-175, 457-462, 511-513. The
general applicability of the zoning, including the TDR-R overlay, and the broad policy factors considered by the City Council
are hallmarks of legislative action rather administrative tasks. Carter v. Lehi City, 2012 UT 2, ¶ 34, 269 P.3d 141. Mapleton's
decision to rezone the Property with a TDR-R overlay was site-specific zoning, which is a legislative act subject to referendum.

This Court should not indulge LDIII's attempt to undo an election that has been on the books for almost two years. The time for
LDIII to challenge Mapleton's decision to reapply a TDR-R overlay to the Property or to challenge Mapleton's determination
that such action was referable expired before this lawsuit was filed. LDIII's assertion that the rezoning of the Property with a
TDR-R overlay was administrative directly contradicts the Supreme Court's holding in Krejci. Accordingly, the District Court
properly granted summary judgment in favor of Mapleton.

for numerous, independent reasons.
