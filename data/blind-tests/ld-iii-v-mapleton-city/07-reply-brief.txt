*1 ARGUMENT
I. Summary Judgment Was Inappropriate.
Mapleton City (“Mapleton”) argues the trial court was correct in granting it summary judgment. Mapleton relies on Heslop v.
Bear River Mutual Insurance Co., 2017 UT 5, 390 P.3d 314 for the proposition that a court does not have to draw every possible
inference in favor of the nonmoving party. Heslop states:
In determining whether a factual dispute exists, we apply an objective standard. The objective standard asks whether reasonable
jurors properly instructed, would be able to come to only one conclusion, or if they might come to different conclusions, thereby
making summary judgment inappropriate. But where there could be no reasonable difference of opinion on a question of fact
in light of the available evidence, the decision is one of law for the trial judge or for an appellate court.

Id. at ¶ 20. “[A] reasonable inference exists when there is a least a foundation in the evidence upon which the ultimate conclusion
is based while in the case of speculation, there is no underlying evidence to support the conclusion.” Id. at ¶ 22. Here, LD III,
LLC (“LDIII”) has shown the evidence supports inferences in its favor, which the trial court altogether failed to draw.
Mapleton also relies on Surety Underwriters v. E&C Trucking, Inc., 2000 UT 71, 10 P.3d 338. The Surety Underwriters court
stated, “[t]he question before us, however, is whether the court may draw a reasonable inference from the uncontested facts,
even though adverse to the nonmoving party.” Id. at ¶ 35. The court added, “[t]he law requires that the trial court view the facts
and all reasonable inferences drawn therefrom in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party.” Id. at ¶ 36.
*2 Accordingly, while the trial court may make inferences against the nonmoving party, if there is a reasonable inference
favoring the nonmoving party, the trial court is obligated to make that reasonable inference. Mapleton fails to explain why
inferences LDIII argues the trial court should have made in its favor are not reasonable. Instead, it argues inferences in its favor,
and against LDIII, are the only reasonable inferences. That is not the standard.
II. Mapleton City Waived Any Right to Contest a Density of Less Than 176 Units.
Mapleton argues any density rights in excess of 92 units were waived. The requirements for waiver are “[1] an existing right,
benefit or advantage, [2] a knowledge of its existence, and [3] an intention to relinquish it.” McCleve Properties, LLC v. D. Ray
Hult Family LTD. Partnership, 2013 UT App 185, 307 P.3d 650. Mapleton points this argument in the wrong direction. Under
the doctrine of waiver, Mapleton was the party that waived any rights it had to cap the Property's density at 92 units, and also
waived any claim the Property is not entitled to TDR receiving site (“TDR-R”) status.
Mapleton argues the zoning was set at 92 units. It admits it knew about the zoning cap. Mapleton admits in 2017 it changed
the Property's base zoning from 92 units to 176 units and it applied a TDR-R status to the Property. Mapleton clearly approved
the density of 176 units. Therefore, Mapleton intended to relinquish and waive the 92 unit density limit and set it at 176 units.

*3 III. There Was No Waiver of the Right to Develop More than 92 Units on the Property.
Mapleton argues The Preserve, LDIII's predecessor in interest, earlier waived the right to build more than 92 units when it
applied to change the Property's base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4. Trial courts are cautioned to be especially careful in considering
evidence in questions of waiver, especially where waiver is merely implied. Id. at ¶ 10. Because waiver is intensely factdependent, district courts should exercise care when granting summary judgment on this issue.” IHC Health Services, Inc. v.
D&K Management, Inc., 2008 UT 73, ¶ 15, 196 P.3d 588.
[A] district court is precluded from granting summary judgment if the facts shown by the evidence on a summary judgment
motion support more than one plausible but conflicting inference on a pivotal issue in the case particularly if the issue turns on
credibility or if the inferences depend upon subjective feelings or intent.

Id. at ¶ 18 (emphasis in original).
The intent of the parties is pivotal when considering the issue of waiver. Mapleton clearly did intend to waive the lower density.
But there is nothing other than an argument based on an unproven inference that The Preserve intended to waive the Property's
TDR-R status. A change of the Property's base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4 has nothing to do with TDR-R status, because both
zones allow TDR-R rights. Mapleton ignores that there is no evidence the application to change the Property's base zoning
mentioned removing or was accompanied with the intention to remove the Property's TDR-R status. Removing the Property's
TDR-R status was never discussed or mentioned by either of the parties.
*4 Mapleton makes the unsupported argument that the only “reasonable” inference is that The Preserve intended to remove
the TDR-R status because it was not mentioned in the application to change the Property's base zoning.1 However, it is equally
reasonable to infer omitting mention of TDRs implies The Preserve intended to keep the Property's TDR-R status. The Property's
TDR-R status was not addressed in the Mapleton City Council Meeting and there is no evidence of the application to change
the Property's base zoning. Because either inference is reasonable, the trial court was required to make the reasonable inference
in favor of LDIII, which it failed to do. Contrary to Mapleton's argument, there are other reasonable conclusions to be drawn
from the facts in the record.
As set forth above, an element of waiver is an intention to relinquish the right. There is no evidence in the record regarding the
intent of The Preserve or any other party. The only way the trial court could determine The Preserve's intent was by making
improper inferences in favor of Mapleton when there are equally reasonable inferences to be made in favor of LDIII. Fact finding
through discovery would allow the trial court to establish The Preserve's intent by direct evidence, not by making inferences
about the parties' intent. Mapleton makes the unwarranted leap that because it unilaterally, without making the required notices
and without explicitly addressing the issue, removed the Property's TDR-R status, the only reasonable inference is The Preserve
intended to relinquish the Property's TDR-R status. It is just as reasonable to infer The Preserve intended to keep the Property's
TDR-R status.
*5 IV. The Original Agreement Was Never Modified to Limit Density to 92 Units.
Mapleton argues the original Development Agreement (“Development Agreement”) was amended when the Property's base
zoning was changed from A-2 to PRC-4. However, Mapleton ignores the Development Agreement's plain language. It states it
“shall not be modified or amended except in written form mutually agreed to and signed by each of the parties.” R.48 (emphasis
added). As Mapleton admits, the trial court did not address whether the agreement was modified. There is no evidence of a
written instrument, signed by both parties, modifying the Development Agreement.

Rather, Mapleton argues the Development Agreement was amended when the Property's base zoning was changed. The only
way to arrive at Mapleton's conclusion is to make the inference the parties intended to modify the Development Agreement
when the Property's base zoning was changed. This requires a double inference against LDIII: First, the trial court had to infer

*6 V. LDIII Is Not Time Barred From Asserting the Property's Vested Development Rights.
The crux of this issue is whether the failure of Mapleton to give any notice of its alleged intent to remove the Property's TDRR status somehow constitutes notice. It is uncontested Mapleton did not give notice of any intent to remove the Property's TDRR status. It is uncontested when the Property's base zoning was changed from A-2 to PRC-4 there was no mention of removing
its TDR-R status. It is uncontested the Property's TDR-R status was only changed when Mapleton city staff, in an unnoticed
administrative act on an unknown date, changed the Property's status on city maps.
Mapleton argues notice is deemed adequate and proper if not challenged within 30 days after the action or the meeting. Mapleton
fails to address that it failed to give any notice to anyone of its action amending and deleting the TDR status. Mapleton asks
this Court to hold that if a municipality fails to give notice of an action it intends to take, and if no one complains about it
within 30 days from an unpublished date, the municipality can ignore Utah's notice requirements. Such an argument contradicts
Utah's notice requirements and contradicts the clear language of Utah law. This Court emphasized the time period to challenge
an enactment of a land use ordinance only applies “[if] the municipality has complied with the notice provisions of Section
10-9a-295.” Powder Run at Deer Valley Owner Association v. Black Diamond Lodge at Deer Valley Association of Unit Owners,
2014 UT App 43, n. 6, 320 P.3d 1076. There is no evidence in the record to prove Mapleton *7 complied with Utah's notice
requirements in a way that would cause a statute of limitations to begin to run.
Mapleton further argues all of its actions were made in public meetings and became part of the public record. However, it is
uncontested there was never any notice of Mapleton's alleged intent to remove the Property's TDR-R status. It is uncontested
the minutes from the Mapleton City Council meeting in which the Property's base zoning was changed from A-2 to PRC-4 do
not mention removing the Property's TDR-R status. It is uncontested the Property's TDR-R status was sometime later removed
on an unknown date when Mapleton City staff changed the city maps. Contrary to Mapleton's argument, there is no record
showing the removal of the Property's TDR-R status was noticed or ever explicitly considered in the public record. The only
place it can be found is in the map changed by city staff on an unknown date without any public notice or public discussion.
VI. The Property's TDR Receiving Site Status Was Never Properly Removed.
Mapleton argues the Property's TDR-R status was removed when the Property's base zoning was changed from A-2 to PRC-4,
even though there is no mention of its removal in the notice or in the discussion by the Mapleton City Council. Mapleton
argues it is undisputed it gave notice of the removal of the TDR receiving site status. Mapleton refers to a staff report stating
the rezoning was “re-public noticed” and that a letter was sent to all property owners within 500 feet of the proposed rezone.
However, the record cited by Mapleton makes no mention of the removal of the Property's TDR-R status. It only refers to
changing the Property's base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4. R.490. Mapleton argues, *8 without support, that giving notice of
a proposed change of the Property's base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4 is sufficient and there is no requirement to give notice of
a change in the Property's TDR-R status.

Mapleton relies on Suarez v. Grand County, 2012 UT 72, 296 P.3d 688 for the proposition its notice of changing the zoning
from A-2 to PRC-4, without mentioning removal of the property's TDR receiving site status, was sufficient. However, Suarez
is not applicable here.
In Suarez, the court considered the adequacy of a notice given by Grand County regarding amending the master plan and
preliminary plats for a development. See Id. at ¶ 76. The Utah Supreme Court adopted the “reasonable relation” standard for
the adequacy of notice, which followed the Colorado Supreme Court's reasoning.
The reasonable relation standard, as explained by the Colorado Supreme Court, states the “notice is sufficient as long as the
items actually considered at the meeting are reasonably related to the subject matter indicated by the notice.” Id. at 74 (citing
Town of Marble v. Darien, 181 P.3d 1148, 1152 (Colo. 2008). Here, there is no evidence regarding what was in the notice of any
meeting. Mapleton cannot cite any part of the record containing the actual notice given. Rather, Mapleton refers to a Mapleton
City Planning Commission Staff Report. R.490. There is nothing showing the content of the City's notice.
Further, the document offered by Mapleton refers to the Planning Commission which cannot change zoning. It does not address
anything related to the consideration of the rezoning by the Mapleton City Council and there is no mention of removing the
Property's *9 TDR-R status in the Mapleton City Council minutes. Therefore, it was error for the trial court to conclude the
notice given was adequate when there is nothing in the record showing the contents of the notice.
Mapleton argues its city code requires rezoning applications include the designation of the specific zone change or title
amendment desired. Mapleton argues the Planning Commission Staff Report shows the Property was zoned A-2 and A-2 (TDRR) but the zone sought was PRC-4. While the record reflects such statements in the staff report, Mapleton takes them out of
context to support its argument. Further, there is no mention of the removal of the Property's TDR-R status in the Mapleton
City Council Minutes.
It is a logical fallacy for Mapleton to rely on an ordinance setting forth what is required in a zoning application and then rely
on the staff report rather than the actual zoning application, which would presumably be available to Mapleton. Mapleton's
argument should fail because they rely on a staff report and conceal the actual application.
Further, the staff report refers to two different properties, one owned by Jack Perry and the Property at issue here. At the time,
Perry's property was zoned A-2 and the Property at issue here was zoned A-2 (TDR-R), which explains the dual reference. R.490.
Although the report refers to the Property's A-2 (TDR-R) status, it never mentions the removal of the Property's TDR-R status.
Mapleton's argument supports LDIII's position. Mapleton's ordinance requires a rezoning application to include the designation
of the specific zone change or title amendment desired. Here, the record lacks any specific mention of a change of the *10
Property's TDR-R status. The only specific mention is the change of the base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4. Because this matter
was decided on summary judgment, the trial court should have made the reasonable inference that because the removal of the
Property's TDR-R status was not specifically designated, it was not part of the rezoning application. Such an inference is more
in line with the requirements of Mapleton's ordinance.

VIII. The Development Agreement Ran With the Land.
*11 Mapleton argues the Development Agreement runs with the property “only to those who have been previously approved
by Mapleton.” However, there is no dispute the Development Agreement states it runs with the land. R.47. The Development
Agreement also states it is binding on the successors and assigns. R.45. In the referenced sections of the Development
Agreement, there is no condition on when it runs with the land, nor when it is binding on successors.
Mapleton correctly explains an element of whether the Development Agreement runs with the land is the intent of the parties.
But once again, Mapleton requests the Court to ignore inferences in favor of LDIII and only make inferences in its favor.
Mapleton ignores the plain language of the Development Agreement stating it runs with the land. Without any direct evidence
of the parties' intent, the trial court must make all reasonable inferences in favor of LDIII. It is certainly reasonable to infer
that because the Development Agreement itself states it runs with the land and is binding on successors, the parties intended
just that. Therefore, it was error for the trial court to determine the parties did not intend for the Development Agreement to
run with the land.
IX. The Agreement is Ambiguous.
To support its argument the Property's vested rights did not run with the land, Mapleton argues the Development Agreement is
not ambiguous and its parts can be harmonized. However, Mapleton only harmonizes the Development Agreement's terms by
ignoring the Development Agreement language stating it runs with the land, it is to be recorded (which Mapleton failed to do)
and it is binding on all successors. As set forth in *12 LDIII's opening brief, because the Development Agreement contains
conflicting terms, it is ambiguous and the trial court erred in not taking extrinsic evidence regarding the parties' intent.
This Court stated:
Harmonizing conflicting or apparently ambiguous contract language before concluding that provisions are actually ambiguous
is an important step in the hierarchy of rules for contract interpretation. A trial court must first attempt to harmonize all of the
contract's provisions and all of its terms when determining whether the plain language of the contract is ambiguous.

Gillmor v. Macey, 2005 UT App 351, ¶ 19, 121 P.3d 57. Here, the trial court entirely ignored the Development Agreement's
language stating it runs with the land and is to be recorded. It also ignored language stating it is binding on all successors and
assigns. The trial court neither attempted to harmonize, nor consider in any way, that conflicting and ambiguous language of
the Development Agreement existed here. Such is error.
Mapleton relies on Gillmor for the idea that the Development Agreement did not “create free-floating rights” However, Gillmor
dealt with an easement which was granted and which placed specific limitations on the purposes for which the easement could
be used. See Id. at ¶ 3. The easement specifically prohibited the use of “three-wheeled motorized All Terrain Vehicles” on the
easement. See Id. at 4. The original party to the easement died and the easement was used by that person's children. Id. The
easement specifically stated it was for the benefit of “Gillmor and his immediate family to the first degree of consanguinity, and
their spouses and children.” Id. at ¶ 15. The Court found the easement “details a specific *13 and limited class of individuals
who are benefitted by this specific right of vehicular access.” Id. at ¶ 17.
Here, the Development Agreement does not state it is just for the benefit of Suburban Land Reserve or The Preserve. More
importantly, the trial court never considered the language in the Development Agreement which states it runs with the land and

X. The City Council's 2017 Decision was Not Referable.
Mapleton failed to respond to the fact that on June 7, 2017 the Property retained its TDR status and the City Council again
affirmed the Property as a TDR receiving site. Subsequently, a referendum on the November 2017 ballot passed intending to
reverse the City Council's decision. It is Mapleton's position now that the action the City Council took was reversed by that
referendum.
The referendum was illegal and can have no effect. Utah Code § 20A-7-601 deals with local referenda procedures. Utah Code
§ 20A-7-601(1) provides that “a person seeking to have a local law passed by the local legislative body submitted to a vote of
the people ....” (Emphasis added.) It is important this refers to a “local law.” Utah Code § 20A-7- 101(13) defines a “local law”:
“(a)'Local law' includes: (i) an ordinance; (ii) a resolution; (iii) a master plan; (iv) a comprehensive zoning regulation adopted
by ordinance or resolution; *14 or (v) other legislative action of a local legislative body, (b) 'Local law' does not include an
individual property zoning decision.” Utah Code § 20A-7-101(13) (emphasis added).
Mapleton attempts to distinguish Baker v. Carlson, 2018 UT 59, 437 P.3d 333 (Sup.Ct.) by arguing about timing. The question
of timing is not relevant to whether a referendum can be held. The Baker decision clarifies the earlier decision of Krejci v. City
of Saratoga Springs, 2013 UT 74, ¶ 34, 322 P.3d 662. Baker and Krejeci both support the proposition that whether a referendum
is appropriate turns on “[i]f the municipality's decision is ‘open-ended’ and made without reference to ‘fixed criteria,’ then the
decision may be legislative.” Baker at 339 (citing Krejci ¶ 34). “But if the municipality's decision involves the ‘application
of existing law to the facts presented by an individual applicant’ or is ‘limited to the evaluation of specific criteria fixed by
law,’ then the decision is administrative.” Id. Unlike this case, the issue in Baker concerned whether the city should adopt a site
master development plan for that location, without regard to any specific entity. Id. at 334. Resolution 2018-17, like this case,
concerned the amendment of an existing Agreement for the Development of Land, which was an agreement between specific
parties. Id. Because “[t]he Amended ADL applie [d] only to the contracting parties and its approval involved the application of
law to specific facts Resolution 2018-17 [was] therefore not referable.” Id. at 342.
Here, the referendum concerned only whether the increased density allowed by the use of TDRs would be specifically allowed
on the Property. This decision was necessarily made *15 in accordance with the parties' Development Agreement. Therefore,
it was administrative and not legislative. The trial court was in error in determining that it was referable.
In 2017 when approving development of the Property, Mapleton complied with all Utah notice requirements for a zoning change.
But their only act was to confirm the Property retained its TDR-R status! That issue was specifically mentioned in notices and
was specifically discussed in the Mapleton City Council Meeting. Mapleton allowed a referendum vote on the issue. Yet its
position in this appeal is based entirely on the opposite position: Notice was not required when it removed the Property's TDRR status, and the issue did not need to be specifically discussed in a city council meeting, and it could be removed by city staff
changing the city maps. If Mapleton's appeal position is correct, the TDR-R status could have been added through the same
informal procedure. It is clear Mapleton wants to treat the removal of the Property's TDR-R status as a permitted administrative
act, but the approval of TDR-R status for the Property must be a legislative action. Either the Property's TDR-R status was
legislative and improperly removed in 2007, or addressing the TDR-R status is an administrative act which is not appropriate for
a referendum. It must be one or the other. It cannot be both. Either way, summary judgment for LDIII should have been granted.
