INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND
The one constant throughout the fifteen-year development of the Preserve is that the City
has always supported the property rights of whatever entity owned the Preserve Property at that
time. In 2003, the City authorized up to 136 units on the Property and granted transferable
development rights (“TDRs”) that can be used to increase the allowable density in a development
for the benefit of Suburban Land Reserve; in 2005–2007 the Property exchanged hands, and as
requested by the new owner—the Preserve at Mapleton Development Company (“Preserve at
Mapleton”—the City rezoned the property to PRC-4 and limited its density to 92 units; and
finally in 2017, the City once again attempted to rezone the property for the benefit of the new
owner and developer of the Preserve—LD III and Balton Development. However the 2017
rezone was overturned by a referendum vote. The City has consistently worked with the owner
of the Preserve Property to achieve a development that satisfies the City’s requirements and
standards while also allowing the owner the freest use of its property possible. To do so, the City
has executed two development agreements, rezoned the Property three times, and approved
several plans for development. Yet the Preserve remains largely undeveloped.
A new dispute has arisen regarding the continued application of a Development
Agreement entered into between Suburban Land Reserve—not a party to these proceedings—
and the City in 2003, and whether the current owner of the Preserve Property, LD III, is entitled
to the benefits of that Development Agreement. Further, there is also a dispute regarding the
status of the City’s previous rezoning of the Preserve Property to allow for the use of TDRs to
increase the density allowed on the Preserve.
Regarding the Development Agreement, LD III’s claims are untimely and are barred due

The Utah Code permits municipalities to adopt ordinances regarding the creation

and use of “transferable development rights” or “TDRs” UTAH CODE § 10-9a-509.7. TDRs allow
“a land owner in a designated sending zone to transfer land use rights from a designated sending
zone to a designated receiving zone.” Id. § 10-9a-103(60).
2.

Mapleton City adopted a TDR ordinance in 1998, which provided that certain

properties could be designated as “TDR Sending Sites” and receive a “TDR-S” overlay zone in
addition to their base zoning status This requires the owner to grant a conservation easement or
transfer the property to the City in order to prevent all future development on that property.
Exhibit 17, Ordinance 98-36, §§ 18.90.040, 060; MCC § 18.76.040, 060.
3.

In exchange, the owner would receive TDR certificates, commonly referred to

simply as “TDRs”, based on the size and zoning of the TDR Sending Site property. TDRs are a
property right that can be bought and sold as any other property independently of the property for
which they were granted. Exhibit 17, § 18.90.060; MCC § 18.76.060; Exhibit 3 ¶¶ 12–13.
4.

TDRs grant the owner the right to increase the permitted density of a

development. In effect, TDRs allow for a transfer of density rights from one property to another.
And the TDRs used for a particular development do not need to come from the property sought
to be developed. Exhibit 17 § 18.90.070; MCC § 18.76.070; Exhibit 3 ¶ 12–14.

A. LD III’s Claims are Procedurally Barred Because It Failed to Timely Assert Them
The Municipal Land Use, Development, and Management Act (“MLUDMA”) authorizes
municipalities to create and enforce land use regulations regarding “the erection, construction,
reconstruction, alteration, repair, or use of buildings and structures, and the use of land.” UTAH
CODE § 10-9a-505(1). These regulations may take the form of ordinances, rules, and maps—
including zoning maps. Id. § 10-9a-103(29), (63). The Mapleton City Code specifies that the
City’s zoning ordinances are to be memorialized in an official zone map. MCC §§ 18.08.290,
18.24.020. “The location and boundaries of each of the zones shall be as set forth on the official
zone map, … [and] [t]he official zone map and all notations, references and other information
shown thereon shall be as much a part of [the City Code] as if the boundary lines and other
references and information set forth thereon were fully described in this chapter.” MCC §
18.24.020(A). The official zone map may be amended only by following the procedures set forth
in the City Code and MLUDMA. MCC §§ 18.12.010, 18.12.020; UTAH CODE § 10-9a-205.

B. The City’s Actions Constitute an Implied Repeal of the TDR-R Overlay Zone

present case, all of the evidence shows that the City intended to remove the TDR-R overlay zone
from the Preserve Property and in fact adopted zoning restrictions on the Preserve Property that
specifically prevent the use of TDRs on the property.

C. The Statute of Frauds Does Not Bar the City’s Actions
LD III’s final claim regarding zoning is that any removal of the TDR-R zoning did not
comply with the statute of frauds. LD III asserts that the TDR-R overlay zone was a property
interest that could only be transferred or removed by a written document signed by the
appropriate party. In the first place, the zoning of a property is generally not considered a
property interest: “[A] property owner has no vested right to ‘continuity of zoning.’ … A person
who purchases land in reliance upon its current zoning restrictions acquires no right that the
restrictions remain the same.” Smith Inv. Co. v. Sandy City, 958 P.2d 245, 258 n. 19 (Utah Ct.
App. 1998); see also Keith v. Mt. Resorts Dev., LLC, 2014 UT 32, ¶ 31 337 P.3d 213 (“A
development approval does not create independent free-floating vested property rights.”). As
such, the statute of frauds is inapplicable.
Regardless, LD III has overlooked the fact that the City’s actions in changing the zoning
of the Preserve Property were based on a signed, written document: the Preserve at Mapleton’s

signed by the agent of the owner of the property, and it specifically requests the actions that the
City took. Exhibit 7. As such, the City was authorized to make changes in conformity with that
application, and the statute of frauds does not apply in the present case.

admission, act, or failure to act.

B. LD III Has No Rights Under the Development Agreement
LD III’s central claim is that by acquiring the Preserve Property through a foreclosure
sale, it became a successor to or assignee of all of the rights and benefits of the 2003
Development Agreement between Suburban Land Reserve and the City. This claim fails because
Utah law is clear that the acquisition of property does not, in and of itself, make that party a
successor to or assignee of all agreements related to that property. The mere fact that Suburban
Land Reserve and the Preserve at Mapleton may have had a vested contractual right to a
particular density does not mean that those rights passed to LD III simply because LD III bought
the property at foreclosure. Instead, there must be an agreement expressly assigning those rights
to LD III or the contractual rights must “run with the land.” Because neither of these
circumstances is present here, LD III’s contractual claims fail.
1. Utah Law Regarding the Transfer of Contract Rights in Property
A party’s acquisition of property that is the subject of an agreement does not, in and of
itself, make that party a successor or assignee of the agreement. For example, in West v. Case,
2006 UT App 325, 142 P.3d 576, Ms. Case became the trustee of a trust containing certain real
estate after her mother, Lamar West, died. Id. ¶¶ 2–4. Ms. Case was sued by the Wests, who
sought title to the property and attorney fees pursuant to an agreement between them and Lamar
West. Id. ¶ 5–6. While the Utah Court of Appeals agreed that Ms. Case and the Trust had an in
rem obligation to transfer the property to the Wests, it reversed the district court’s award of
attorney fees, concluding that Ms. Case was not an assignee or successor to the Lamar West
agreement and therefore could not be liable under that agreement. Id. ¶¶ 18, 21. The court noted
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

27

that “there was no formal assignment of the Agreement from Lamar West to Case or to the Trust,
and no assumption of the liabilities of the Agreement by the Trust or Case individually. Id. ¶ 19.
Although Ms. Case obtained ownership of the property via quit-claim deed, “a quit-claim grantee
merely becomes a successor to the grantor’s property interest,” not to any agreements concerning
that property Id. ¶ 20. “Accordingly, although Case had an in rem obligation to transfer the
Property to the Wests, we determine that neither Case nor the Trust became bound by any other
terms of the Agreement merely because they took ownership of the Property.” Id. ¶ 21.
Likewise, in Hansen v. Green River Group, 748 P.2d 1102 (Utah Ct. App. 1988), vendors
of certain real property, the Hansens, sought to enforce the terms of a real estate purchase
contract they had executed with Synvest Corporation (“Synvest”), by collecting payment from a
third party, the Green River Group (“Group”), which had entered into a separate real estate
purchase agreement regarding the same property with Synvest. Id. at 1103–04. The Court of
Appeals rejected the Hansens’ claim because Group was not an assignee or successor to the
Hansen-Synvest contract. While the Synvest-Group contract contained language wherein Group
“agrees to abide and be bound by the conditions that appear in all underlying contract [sic],” it
contained neither any reference to the Hansen-Synvest contract nor “the usual words of either an
assumption or an assignment, such as ‘assumes,’ ‘agrees to pay,’ ‘assigns,’ ‘transfers’ or
‘conveys.’” Id. at 1104. Instead, the Synvest-Group contract was simply “a subpurchase by a
subpurchaser by means of a subcontract. A subcontract by the purchaser, Synvest, to sell to
Group under an executory land contract is not an assignment, but a separate, independent
contract between them. The subpurchase creates between the original vendor (Hansens) and
subpurchaser (Group) privity of estate, but does not, in the absence of agreement, create privity
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

28

of contract between them.” Id. at 1104 n.2. The court noted that if the Synvest-Group agreement
was intended to be an assignment to Group of the Hansen-Synvest agreement, that intention
“should have and could have been stated in the [Synvest-Group] contract with specificity.” Id. at
1104. Without such a clear assignment agreement between Synvest and Group, the SynvestGroup contract “was not an assumption by Group of the obligation to make Synvest’s payments
to the Hansens or an assignment by Synvest of the Hansen-Synvest contract.” Id.
Finally, in Oquirrh Associates v. First National Leasing Co., 888 P.2d 659 (Utah Ct.
App. 1994), Oquirrh Associates (“Oquirrh”) sought to enforce the terms of a real estate purchase
contract it had reached with one party, the Loiselles, against a different entity, Forthcoming
Investments (“Forthcoming”), which had acquired the Loiselles’ interest in the property. Id. at
661–62. The basis for Oquirrh’s claim was that its agreement with the Loiselles provided that
“the stipulations aforesaid are to apply to and bind the heirs, executors, administrators,
successors and assigns of the respective parties hereto, and the benefits hereunder shall inure to
the Buyer [the Loiselles] or its assignees.” Id. at 663. Oquirrh asserted that the quitclaim deed
executed by the Loiselles in favor of Forthcoming made Forthcoming “a direct ‘successor’ of the
Loiselles in accordance with the terms of the Oquirrh-Loiselle contract and thus ... contractually
bound to the terms of that agreement.” Id. The court rejected this claim, holding that the
language “referring to ‘heirs, executors, administrators, successors and assigns’ of the parties can
only refer to those who succeed to one party's interest in the contract through inheritance,
assignment, or the like.” Id. And something more than a quit-claim deed is required to become
an assignee to contract rights: “Forthcoming merely became a successor to the Loiselles’
property interest, but, absent agreement to the contrary, the Loiselles retained their contractual
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

29

rights and obligations.” Id.
The principle of these cases is uniform and unmistakable: becoming a successor to a
party’s property interests does not, by itself, make one a successor or assign to any agreements
entered into by that party regarding the property. In the present case, the mere fact that LD III
obtained the Preserve at Mapleton’s property rights through foreclosure does not necessarily
mean that LD III obtained any of the Preserve at Mapleton’s contractual rights. There would
need to either be an assignment agreement between the Preserve at Mapleton and LD III
specifically assigning the Preserve at Mapleton’s contractual rights to LD III or the contractual
rights must “run with the land.” As discussed below, however, neither situation is present here.

contract rights to LD III and without involving the City in any way. LD III cannot claim the
rights of a contract to which it was never a party and never approved as an assignee.
3. The Covenants of the Development Agreement Do Not Run With the Land
Because LD III was never a valid assignee of the Development Agreement, the only way
it may claim the benefits of that Agreement—in particular the “vested right” to develop up to
136 lots—is if those benefits “run with the land.” As the Utah Supreme Court explained:
A covenant that "runs with the land" binds successive owners of the burdened or
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

33

benefited land. The rights and duties of a personal covenant, by contrast,
terminate when a covenanting party conveys the property to a subsequent owner.
A successor-owner claiming the benefit of a predecessor-owner's covenant must
demonstrate that the covenant meets [four] requirements: (1) the covenant must
touch and concern the land affected by the covenant, (2) the original parties to the
covenant must have expressly or impliedly intended the covenant to run with the
land, (3) there must be privity of estate, and (4) the covenant "must be in writing."
Stern v. Metro. Water Dist., 2012 UT 16, ¶ 40, 274 P.3d 935. There are two issues in this case:
first, because the development agreement was never recorded, it cannot create covenants that run
with the land. Second, the parties to the original Development Agreement never intended to have
the covenants of the Development Agreement run with the land to unrelated third parties.
In order for a covenant to run with the land, it must be recorded. Flying Diamond Oil
Corp. v. Newton Sheep Co., 776 P.3d 618, 628 (Utah 1989); Shunk v. Palm Beach Cnty., 420 So.
2d 394, 395 (Fla. App. 1982) (“[T]he weight of authority seems to be that the owner of land is
bound by restrictive covenants only if they appear in his own chain of title.”). Recording is
necessary because of Utah’s recording statute, which provides, “Each document not recorded as
provided in this title is void as against any subsequent purchaser of the same real property, or any
portion of it, if the subsequent purchaser purchased the property in good faith and for a valuable
consideration; and the subsequent purchaser's document is first duly recorded.” UTAH CODE §573-103. “A contract or deed that is void cannot be ratified or accepted, and anyone can attack its
validity in court.” Ockey v. Lehmer, 2008 UT 37, ¶ 18, 189 P.3d 51.
The Development Agreement was never recorded. The only recorded reference to the
Development Agreement is found in the 2007 Memorandum of Understanding between the City
and the Preserve at Mapleton, which was not recorded until September 2008. But LD III
acquired its property interest in the Preserve—the Trust Deed—in December 2006, long before
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

34

any reference to the Development Agreement was recorded. And LD III recorded its interest first
by recording the Trust Deed in April 2008, before the City recorded anything with regard to the
Preserve Property in September 2008. As such, the Development Agreement is void as to LD III
because LD III took its interest in the property in good faith and recorded that interest prior to
any recorded document regarding the Development Agreement. Thus, the Development
Agreement is void as to LD III and does not run with the land.
The second reason why the Development Agreement’s covenants do not run with the
land is because the parties to that agreement—Suburban Land Reserve and the City—did not
intend for them to do so. When interpreting the parties’ intent regarding whether the benefit of a
particular contract runs with the land or merely “creates a personal benefit that only extends to
those who qualify as members of the identified group,” the Court must use the usual tools of
contract interpretation. Gillmor v. Macey, 2005 UT App 351, ¶¶ 16, 19, 121 P.3d 57. In
particular, “a contract should be interpreted so as to harmonize all of its provisions and all of its
terms, which terms should be given effect if it is possible to do so.” Id. ¶ 19 (citation omitted). In
Gillmore, the Utah Court of Appeals was called on to decide whether an agreement’s grant of an
easement “for access by Gillmor and his immediate family to the first degree of consanguinity”
created a benefit that ran with the land or was personal to Mr. Gillmor and his immediate family.
Id. ¶ 15. The court noted that, “While the Agreement states, in several places, that the grants and
limitations in the Agreement are appurtenant to the land and run with the land to future
successors in interest, the Agreement also details a specific and limited class of individuals who
are to be benefitted by this specific grant of vehicular access.” Id. ¶ 17. The court held that the
agreement’s specification of a class of persons to be benefited by the agreement “undermines any
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

35

notion that this benefit is somehow intended to run with the land to the benefit of all future
owners of the Gillmore property.” Id. ¶ 17. Accordingly, the agreement’s benefits were therefore
“personal to Gillmor and the identified small class of his family members.” Id. ¶¶ 17, 19.
A similar situation is presented here. LD III points to paragraph 21 of the Development
Agreement, which states in part, “this Agreement or a memorandum providing public notice of
the existence of this Agreement shall be recorded immediately as a covenant running with the
Property herein described in order to put prospective purchasers or other interested parties on
notice as to the terms and provisions hereof." The first problem with this argument is that the
Development Agreement was never recorded. Regardless, the intent of paragraph 21 is clarified
by paragraph 10, which provides that “Owner’s [Suburban Land Reserve’s] rights under this
Agreement shall be personal to Owner and shall only run with the land so long as Owner or a
company which is affiliated with or under common ownership and control of Owner shall own
and be the Owner of the Property.” (emphasis added). Thus, just as in Gillmor, while one section
of the Development Agreement suggests in a general manner that the Agreement was to run with
the land, another, more specific section identifies a particular class of people that may benefit
from the Agreement. It is the more specific provision that controls. Reading these paragraphs
together, it is clear that the City and Suburban Land Reserve intended for the benefits of the
Development Agreement to run with the land only in limited circumstances where the owner of
the Preserve Property remained affiliated with Suburban Land Reserve. There is no allegation or
evidence suggesting that either the Preserve at Mapleton or LD III was or is “affiliated with or
under common ownership and control” of Suburban Land Reserve. LD III’s claim that the
Development Agreement runs with the land requires the Court to entirely ignore paragraph 10,
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

36

which specifically addresses whether and how the benefits of the Development Agreement may
pass to future owners of the Preserve Property.
Accordingly, the rights granted to Suburban Land Reserve did not run with the land to the
Preserve at Mapleton or LD III or anyone else, but rather were personal covenants of Suburban
Land Reserve that could only be assigned as set forth in the Development Agreement. And
because LD III was not and could not be a proper assignee of the Development Agreement, LD
III has no claim or entitlement to any of the rights found in the Development Agreement.
4. LD III Would Be in Breach of the Development Agreement
Finally, the City would note that if LD III were somehow designated as an authorized
successor to the Development Agreement, it still could not require the City to perform the
covenants of the contract because it would be in breach of that agreement. “[U]nder the first
breach rule a party first guilty of a substantial and material breach of contract cannot complain if
the other party thereafter refuses to perform. He can neither insist on performance by the other
party nor maintain an action against the other party for a subsequent failure to perform.” Cross v.
Olsen, 2013 UT App 135, ¶ 25, 303 P.3d 1030 (citation omitted). Paragraph 18 of the
Development Agreement requires the developer of the property to begin construction of
improvements or residences within 48 months of approval; otherwise “this Agreement shall
expire and shall have no further force or effect.” And paragraph 23 provides that time is “of the
essence with respect to the duties imposed on the parties under this Agreement. Unless a time
limit is specified for the performance of such duties each party shall commence and perform its
duties in a diligent manner.” It has been a decade and a half since this Agreement was executed.
It has been just under 10 years since LD III acquired the Preserve Property. Between 2008 and
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

37

2017, LD III never attempted to claim the benefits of the Development Agreement or perform
any of its supposed obligations thereunder. In no way can such an unreasonable delay be
considered timely performance. As such, even if LD III was a successor to the Development
Agreement, it has breached the agreement by sitting by for years without ever asserting any
rights under the contract or attempting to perform any of the obligations thereunder.
Accordingly, it cannot claim the benefits of the Development Agreement.
CONCLUSION
The City asks that the Court deny LD III’s motion for summary judgment and grant the
City’s motion for summary judgment. LD III’s claims are procedurally barred because it has
waited nearly a decade before challenging any of the City’s actions. It cannot sit by and
acquiesce to the City actions regarding the Preserve Property and then, years later, claim that its
rights have been impinged. Its claims fair no better substantively. The TDR-R overlay was
repealed through the application of the PRC-4 zoning that expressly limits density on the
Preserve Property and the adoption of official zoning maps that removed the TDR-R overlay. As
to contractual rights, the Development Agreement is clear that only City-approved purchasers
may acquire the benefits and obligations of that contract. LD III was never approved as an
assignee, and it cannot claim the benefits of the Development Agreement under any other theory.
Accordingly, the City is entitled to summary judgment on LD III’s claims.
