INTRODUCTION
In many respects, the present legal action is not a dispute between Plaintiff, LD III, LLC,
and Respondent, Mapleton City, but instead is a struggle between LD III and its neighbors, with
the City caught in the middle. LD III’s lead argument in its reply supporting its motion for
summary judgment and opposing the City’s cross-motion for summary judgment attacks the
November 2017 referendum election, which overturned the City’s rezoning action that would
have again zoned LD III’s property with a TDR-R overlay zone, allowing Transferrable
Development Rights to be used to develop LD III’s property at an increased density in the event
the underlying zoning designation were to also be changed, which it was not. In the first of a

neighbors of LD III rallied a majority of the voting Mapleton citizens to overturn the Council’s

The City did not breach any agreements by doing what the prior owner wanted. LD III was
never entitled to greater density. But, even if it once could have sued for greater density,
inaction by LD III, who owned the property for 9 years before seeking any density increases or
claiming it was entitled to more than 92 homes, precludes the relief it seeks now.

2007 REZONE ELIMINATED TDR-R OVERLAY
A. 2017 rezone to apply TDR-R Overlay was Referable
LD III opens its opposition to the City’s motion for summary judgment with what it
apparently believes to be its best argument - that the referendum election was improperly called
because only local laws are referable and pursuant to Section 20A-7-101(13)(b) of the Utah Code
a “local law’ does not include an individual property zoning decision.” While the code section
cited by LD III is accurately presented, controlling Utah Supreme Court precedent over-rides it.
In Krejci v. City of Saratoga Springs, 2013 UT 74, the Utah Supreme Court overturned a district
court for ruling that an individual property zoning decision was not referable. The district court
had relied on the exact statutory language cited by LD III (see Id. ¶23) and concluded that
rezoning 12 acres owned by a single property owner was not subject to referendum. The Utah
Supreme Court disagreed. It reasoned that the referendum right is constitutional and cannot be
defeated by an inconsistent legislative enactment. In a unanimous decision, the Utah Supreme
Court held that even a site-specific rezoning to increase density is “subject to referendum”. (Id.
4851-6973-0907 | MA001-001/The Preserve

3

¶38). In Krejci the Utah Supreme Court expressly overturned two prior Utah Supreme Court
decisions to the contrary. While it is true that the language of Section 20A-7-101(13)(b) remains
in the Utah Code, the chapter of the Utah Code addressing initiatives and referenda expressly
states that it is subject to Article VI, Section 1 of the Utah Constitution. See §20A-7-102 Utah
Code Ann. 1953, as amended. The Utah Supreme Court has clarified that as mandated by the
Utah Constitution even an individual property zoning decision is referable, despite the code
section cited by LD III. Mapleton merely followed binding Utah Supreme Court precedent.
If LD III disagreed that the TRR-R rezone action was referable, then it needed to lodge
that complaint in court before the election was held, not afterward. Section 20A-7-607(4) of the
Utah Code provides for any voter to challenge the certification of a referendum petition and the
Supreme Court may determine the petition is not legally sufficient and enjoin the election. This
necessarily must happen before the election, however. LD III was required to sue to stop the
election before ballots were cast. LD III gambled that it would prevail at the ballot box. But that
didn’t happen. It is too late for LD III to now complain that the vote never should have been
held. As with the other arguments from LD III, this argument is simply incorrect and entirely
too tardy to prevail.
B. 2007 Rezoning to PRC-4 Zone Caps Density at 92 Homes, irrespective of any
TDR-R overlay zoning.
In 2007, the immediate prior owner of the subject property requested a zone change in
writing, drafted the zoning language, and with the City’s approval, expressly restricted the

zoning map showing the removal of both the A-2 zone and TDR-R overlay zone merely reflected
the 2007 legislative actions of the Mapleton City Council when it adopted the PRC-4 zone. The
PRC-4 density restriction of 92 homes is binding with or without a TDR-R overlay.

C. LD III’s Claims Expired Before This Lawsuit Was Filed, If They Ever Had
Merit.
It is further undisputed that LD III failed to take any action to increase development
density for a full nine years after is acquired the property. If LD III ever had any rights to
develop more than 92 homes (which it didn’t), such rights necessarily expired unexercised before
2017, whether contractual or otherwise.
LD III acquired the property as it is currently zoned in 2008. For nine years, LD III did
not complain about or seek to change the zoning, the density, or the open actions of the City
capping development at 92 homes. If this density cap violated any property rights (which it
didn’t), then that violation necessarily occurred by 2007 when the rightful owner successfully
rezoned the property with lesser density. The reduced density was all a matter of public record
in 2008 when LD III acquired the property, as was the removal of the TDR-R overlay zone.

CONCLUSION
Not only did the referendum sponsors challenge the council’s rezoning and reinstatement
of the TDR-R overlay zone at the ballot box, but the referendum sponsors also filed a lawsuit
challenging that action in court. That lawsuit was not further pursued when the referendum
sponsors realized they would prevail at the ballot box. Mapleton City has done its best to walk a
tight rope between the combative interests of LD III and the referendum sponsors. Not only do
the referendum sponsors and LD III disagree politically, but they also insist that completely
different legal principles control the development of the property now owned by LD III.
