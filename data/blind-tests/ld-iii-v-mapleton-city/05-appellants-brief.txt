*1 INTRODUCTION
This is an appeal from summary judgment. It involves designation by Mapleton City (“Mapleton”) of real property (“Property”)
belonging to LD III (“LDIII”) located in Mapleton, Utah as a Transferable Development Rights Receiving (TDR-R) site. That
designation allows a property owner to use Transferable Development Rights (“TDRs”) to increase density above what is
otherwise allowable under a property's base zoning designation. TDRs are available for several base zones, including both
zoning designations (A-2 and PRC-4) LDIII's property received.
The Property was designated a TDR-R site in 2003. Mapleton refuses to recognize its TDR-R status. The Development
Agreement with Mapleton (“Development Agreement”) agrees it is a TDR-Receiving site that may be developed into 136
building lots. It also states the TDR-R designation and right for 136 building lots run with the land. Mapleton received 76 acres
as consideration for the Property's TDR-R designation and the vested right to 136 building lots for the Property. Mapleton kept
those 76 acres. The Mapleton City Council (“City Council”) explicitly recognized it approved the density and TDR-R overlay
zoning status in exchange for the 76 acres.
The trial court disregarded Development Agreement language stating it runs with the land. It decided Mapleton removed the
TDR-R overlay zoning status in 2006 when it changed the Property's base zoning from A-2 to PRC-4. There is no evidence
the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning was removed. There is no evidence Mapleton gave public notice of an intent to remove
the TDR-R overlay zoning status. There is no evidence Mapleton's *2 repeal of the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning was
discussed in any City Council meeting. There is no evidence the City Council addressed repealing the Property's TDR-R overlay
zoning in any ordinance or resolution.
In granting summary judgment to Mapleton, the trial court weighed evidence, ignored facts favorable to LDIII, made inferences
in Mapleton's favor and misconstrued and misapplied statutes and ordinances. Therefore, this Court should reverse the trial
court's summary judgment.

SUMMARY OF ARGUMENTS
This appeal is to correct the trial court's errors in making inferences and weighing evidence when deciding a motion for summary
judgment. All facts and all reasonable inferences from those facts must be construed in favor of the non-moving party. The trial
court erred in its interpretation of Utah zoning laws and of Mapleton City ordinances and failed to acknowledge the ambiguity
in the Development Agreement.
The trial court's error arose from a failure to consider facts offered by the LDIII in opposition to Mapleton's motion for summary
judgment. The trial court found the City Council voted to remove the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning. However, no notice
was ever given by Mapleton of a meeting in which the issue of removing the Property's TDR-R *14 overlay zoning would
be considered. The removal of the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning was never explicitly discussed or voted on by the City
Council. Rather the trial court wrongly inferred the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning was removed by a change to the Property's
base zoning.
The trial court further held under Mapleton City Code the Property could not have TDR-R overlay zoning when the base zoning
was changed from A-2 to PRC-4. However, Mapleton City Code expressly also permits property with PRC base zoning to have
a TDR-R overlay zoning status.

The trial court held Mapleton could remove the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning using Mapleton's general ordinance procedure
rather than complying with the notice and other requirements in the Utah Code. Utah Code requires Mapleton to give specific
notice when addressing zoning changes. Such notice was never given.
Further, the trial court held LDIII did not succeed to the vested development rights Mapleton granted to the Property when
Mapleton received approximately 76 acres of land in return for development rights. The trial court also concluded it could, as a
matter of law, interpret ambiguous terms of the Development Agreement. It made a determination the Development Agreement
does not run with the land, even though there is plain language in the Development Agreement stating it runs with the land and
the rights granted to the Property are vested.
The trial court also held LDIII is time barred from asserting the Property retained its TDR-R zoning overlay. It held LDIII
had at most one year to contest the adequacy of the *15 notice of the meeting in which the Property's undiscussed TDR-R
zoning overlay status was removed, which the trial court found occurred in 2006. However, Utah Code requires that notice of
a proposed zoning change actually be given, not implied. The trial court ignored that no notice was ever given of a meeting to
consider removing the Property's TDR-R overlay zoning. Further, the removal of the Property's TDR-R zoning overlay status
was never explicitly considered or decided on by the City Council.
ARGUMENT
I. The Summary Judgment Standard.
Summary judgment is permissible only if there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact, and if the moving party is entitled
to judgment as a matter of law based on the undisputed facts. All facts and all reasonable inferences drawn from the facts must
be construed in the light most favorable to a party opposing summary judgment. See Mind & Motion Utah Investments, LLC
v. Celtic Bank Corp., 2016 UT 6, ¶ 15, 367 P.3d 994.
“A party should not be deprived of the privilege of having … an adjudication of his claims unless it appears that even upon
the facts claimed by him he could not establish a basis for recovery. Moreover, when there is doubt about the matter, it should
be resolved in favor of permitting the party to go to trial.” Rees v. Albertson's, Inc., 587 P.2d 130, 133 (Utah 1978) (citations
omitted).
The Utah Supreme Court stated, “[e]ven absent a complete conflict as to certain facts, a dispute of the understanding, intention,
and consequences of those facts may defeat summary judgment.” USA Power, LLC v. PacifiCorp, 2010 UT 31, ¶ 32, 235 P.3d
749 *16 (citations omitted). It added “the trial court must recognize that a party's claim that there are no issues of fact relates
to that party's theory of the case and should not be construed as support for the adversary's argument or motion.” Id. The court
further explained:
In some cases, the parties may agree on the objective statement of the facts, but may fundamentally disagree on the reasonable
inferences to be made from those facts. In such instances, if we were to hold that the nonmoving party must present a specific
fact to controvert a statement of fact by the moving party, we would diminish the important role reasonable inferences play in a
district court's decision to grant summary judgment. Even if the moving party's objective statement of the facts are agreed upon,
reasonable inferences made from those undisputed facts can indeed create a genuine issue of material fact. That the objective
facts are undisputed does not mean that no genuine issues remain as to those facts.

Id. at ¶ 33.

IV. The Voter Referendum Was Invalid.
On June 7, 2017, even though it was unnecessary and redundant, the City Council again designated the Property as a TDR
receiving site. Subsequently, opponents of that decision were allowed to present a referendum on the November 2017 ballot
which, if passed, would reverse the City Council's decision. The referendum passed. It is Mapleton's position the action it took
was reversed and the Property does not possess TDR-R overlay zoning.
However, Mapleton should never have allowed the referendum vote to occur. It was illegal and can have no effect. Utah Code
§ 20A-7-601 deals with local referenda procedures. Utah Code § 20A-7-601(1) provides that “a person seeking to have a local
law passed by the local legislative body submitted to a vote of the people ....” (Emphasis added.) It is important this refers to a
“local law.” Utah Code § 20A-7-101(13) defines a “local law”: “(a)'Local law' includes: (i) an ordinance; (ii) a resolution; (iii)
a master plan; (iv) a comprehensive zoning regulation adopted by ordinance or resolution; or (v) other legislative action of a
local legislative body, (b) 'Local law' does not include an individual property zoning decision.” Utah Code § 20A-7-101(13)
(emphasis added).
*39 Mapleton has throughout its pleadings referred to TDR-R overlay as a zoning status. The action by the City Council to
apply TDR-R overlay zoning to the Property was an individual property zoning decision. It only applied to the Property. It was
not eligible for a referendum, and the 2017 action by the City Council approving TDR-R status for The Property is not affected
by the referendum. The referendum is invalid and not binding. Therefore, the Property retains its TDR-R overlay zoning.
The Utah Supreme Court recently decided this question in Baker v. Carlson, 2018 UT 59, 437 P.3d 333 (Sup.Ct.). In Baker, the
Court was tasked with determining whether two different resolutions in Holladay City regarding the development and use of the
former Cottonwood Mall were referable. Ultimately, they concluded one (2018-16) was, and the other (2018-17) was not. Id.
at 334. The decision turned on “[i]f the municipality's decision is ‘open-ended’ and made without reference to ‘fixed criteria,’
then the decision may be legislative.” Id. at 339 (citing Krejci v. City of Saratoga Springs, 2013 UT 74, ¶ 34, 322 P.3d 662.)
“But if the municipality's decision involves the ‘application of existing law to the facts presented by an individual applicant’ or
is ‘limited to the evaluation of specific criteria fixed by law,’ then the decision is administrative.” Id. Unlike here, Resolution
