*1 INTRODUCTION
Plaintiffs and Appellants Lucy Wallingford, Kiley Miller, John Rzeczycki, Carol Mayer, David Bodner, Meeche Bodner, Sarah
Stock, and Josephine Kovash (collectively, with Living Rivers, the “Appellants”) are real property owners, taxpayers and
residents of Moab whose properties are located less than one mile away from land owned by the Utah School and Institutional
Trust Lands Administration (“SITLA”) that SITLA and a developer, LB Moab Land Company, LLC (“Developer”), are trying
to develop into a 175-acre luxury hotel resort and commercial and mixed use development (the “Project” or “Development”).
The Project is located at the Slick Rock Trailhead near the ancient Lion's Back sandstone formation located on a bench above
Moab City on the east side of the Moab-Spanish Valley. Plaintiff and Appellant Living Rivers is a nonprofit corporation
whose members have interests they seek to protect and who will be adversely affected by the Project, which is situated upon
environmentally-sensitive lands in the City's Sensitive Area Resort (“SAR”) Zone.
In 2008, the Moab City Council granted conditional approval of a Preliminary Master Planned Development (“MPD”) for the
Project. Certain injured parties brought a suit against the City relating to the MPD. See Moab Local Green Party, et al. v. Moab,
2012 UT App 113, 276 P.3d 1230 (hereinafter “Lionsback I”). Following the resolution of Lionsback I, the Developer submitted
an amended MPD application to the City doubling the size of the building footprint(s) from 41,630 square feet to 81,668 square
feet, more than doubling the parking capacity from 104 spaces to 230 spaces, and changing the *2 accommodations from 50
dispersed commercial lodging units to a single 150-room hotel with nearly twice the total square footage (“Amended MPD
Application”).
The City initially took the position that these changes were “Major Changes” under the Moab Municipal Code (“MMC”)
§ 17.65.130 requiring planning commission review and approval after a public hearing, among other things. However, to
prevent the public from being able to engage in the public hearings required by Moab's ordinances, SITLA and the Developer
pressured the City to characterize the revisions as “Minor Changes” that would not require the same formal review processes
or a public hearing. The City succumbed to that pressure and, thereafter, SITLA and City staff drafted a “Zoning Status
Agreement” (“ZSA”) that provided, among other things, that the Amended MPD Application “is deemed to be a Minor
Amendment, which will be reviewed and acted upon by the Moab City planning department staff” without the requirement of a
public hearing, and that future land use applications would also be reviewed and approved by staff without planning commission
review or a public hearing. In short, the ZSA effectively removed the Development from future public scrutiny and public
participation through the normal public processes.

Without submitting the ZSA to the planning commission or holding a public hearing on the ZSA, the City Council adopted
Resolution No. 14-2017 in a City Council meeting. Resolution No. 14-2017, which authorized Moab's mayor to execute the
ZSA, passed by a 3-2 vote with one City Council member wanting to change her swing vote from approval to disapproval
immediately after the vote was taken. That City Council member was told *3 she couldn't change her vote unless there was
another City Council meeting, which never occurred.
Plaintiffs and Appellants filed an administrative appeal seeking, among other things, review of the City's determination that the
proposed changes were “Minor Changes” and could, therefore, bypass the requirement of planning commission review and a
public hearing. However, the City, through its attorney, notified the Appellants that the City's appeal authority would not hear
their appeal because the City's appeal authority lacked jurisdiction. The City claimed the ZSA was a settlement agreement that
was immune to review and beyond the scope of the appeal authority's jurisdiction. Having nowhere else to turn, the Appellants
brought this action in the district court.
The Appellants asked the trial court to declare that the City's enactment of Resolution No. 14-2017 approving the ZSA was
illegal because, among other things, it was enacted without a public hearing and never went before the planning commission
as required by City Code. Alternatively, Appellants sought a writ of mandamus requiring the Moab City Appeal Authority to
consider their administrative appeal. The trial court granted a summary judgment motion filed by SITLA and the Developer
and joined by the City. The trial court concluded, among other things, that the City had authority to enter into the ZSA without
planning commission review or a public hearing, that the court's review of the ZSA “needs to be very generous,” “is limited to
circumstances where it appears from the record that there's been collusion or bad faith between the parties to the agreement”
or “where it looks like there has been an attempt to use a settlement process to completely eviscerate the ordinances.” Without
giving the Appellants the chance to conduct discovery *4 or present evidence under this newly-created evidentiary standard,
which has never before been used by a Utah court in a land use case, the trial court dismissed the Appellants, holding that they
had not satisfied the newly-minted affirmative burden of proving collusion or bad faith.
This appeal seeks nothing more than to give the Appellants their day at city hall to have the required public hearing before a
large-scale luxury hotel development forever alters the Southeastern Utah landscape and watershed. Had the Appellants had
their chance to present evidence in the required public hearing, they believe they very likely would have persuaded that 3-2
swing vote to change her decision, thereby avoiding damage to the Appellants' property interests and long term degradation
of this environmentally-sensitive and beautiful area of Utah. At the very least, if the Appellants are not entitled to have the
ZSA reviewed at a public hearing or by the Moab City Appeal Authority, they ask the Court for at least the chance to conduct
discovery and present evidence to the trial court under the newly-created “collusion or bad faith” standard of review.
STATEMENT OF THE ISSUES
I. Did the trial court incorrectly conclude the City Council had authority to enact Resolution No. 14-2017 approving the ZSA,
which it held was immune to review absent a showing of bad faith or collusion to completely eviscerate the ordinances, when
the resolution was admittedly legislative action undertaken without notice and a public hearing?
A. Standard of Review: Appellate review of a trial court's grant of summary judgment is a de novo review for correctness with
no deference to the trial *5 court. See Bahr v. Imus, 2011 UT 19, ¶ 16, 250 P.3d 56, 62. Summary judgment is warranted only
“if the moving party shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment
as a matter of law.” Utah R. Civ. P. 56(a).
Review of a “land use regulation” under the Land Use Development and Management Act (“LUDMA”) is to “determine only
whether ... the land use regulation ... was enacted contrary to[] state or federal law; and ... [whether] it is reasonably debatable
that the land use regulation is consistent with [LUDMA.]” Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-801(3)(a). “A decision is illegal if the
decision is ... based on an incorrect interpretation of a land use regulation; or ... contrary to law.” Id. § 10-9a-801(3)(c)(ii). A

Questions of legality or illegality present questions of law, which the Court reviews for correctness. See, e.g., Cattani v. Drake,

II. Alternatively, did the trial court incorrectly hold that Resolution No. 14-2017 approving the ZSA was immune to review
absent a showing of bad faith or *6 collusion, ignoring whether the ZSA effected a “land use decision” within the meaning
of LUDMA, which provides a statutory right “to appeal a land use authority's decision to a separate appeal authority,” Utah
Code Ann. § 10-9a-302 (5)(b)(ii)?
A. Standard of Review: Appellate review of a trial court's grant of summary judgment is a de novo review for correctness
with no deference to the trial court. See Imus, 2011 UT 19, ¶ 16. Summary judgment is warranted only “if the moving party
shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”
Utah R. Civ. P. 56(a).
“[T]he Utah Supreme Court recently clarified the appropriate standard for reviewing a district court's disposition of a petition
for review of an administrative decision.” Checketts v. Providence City, 2018 UT App 48, ¶ 14, 420 P.3d 71, 76. This Court
should “review the district court's decision rather than an administrative body's decision directly,” id., and, in doing so, this Court
should “‘afford no deference to the [district] court's decision and apply the statutorily defined standard to determine whether the
court correctly determined whether the administrative decision was arbitrary, capricious, or illegal.”’ Id. (quoting McElhaney
v. City of Moab, 2017 UT 65, ¶ 26, ___ P.3d ___ Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-801(3)(a)(ii)). A land use “decision of a land use
authority” is reviewed to determine whether that decision was “arbitrary and capricious; or illegal.” Id. § 10-9a-801(3)(b).
B. Preservation: This issue was asserted in the Amended Complaint (R. at 60, ¶ 2; 61; ¶¶ 15-16; 62, ¶¶ 17-26; 69, ¶¶ 65-70; 72,
¶ 1), discussed in the briefing *7 (R. at 231-233; 439-447; 449-40; 1441; 1452; 1455-58; 1464; 1628-29; 1637-39; 1884-87),
and debated at oral argument (R. at 1960-62; 1965-67).
III. Alternatively (to the first and second issues), did the trial court incorrectly (1) create a new standard of judicial review
for land use agreements that requires an affirmative showing of bad faith or collusion to completely eviscerate the relevant
municipal ordinances, and (2) hold Appellants to this new standard without giving them the opportunity to conduct discovery,
present evidence, or identify disputed issues of fact under the new standard?
A. Standard of Review: Appellate review of a trial court's grant of summary judgment is a de novo review for correctness
with no deference to the trial court. See Imus, 2011 UT 19, ¶ 16. “[W]hether the trial court applied the proper legal standard is
a question of law that is reviewed for correctness.” Jacob v. Bate, 2015 UT App 206, ¶ 13, 358 P.3d 346 (alteration omitted)
(citation and internal quotation marks omitted). Summary judgment is warranted only “if the moving party shows that there is
no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” Utah R. Civ. P. 56(a).
B. Preservation: The issue of the appropriate standard of review was raised or discussed in the Complaint (R. at 1, ¶ 2; 8, ¶ 45;
10, ¶ 57; 11, ¶¶ 62-65), the Amended Complaint (R. at 60, ¶ 2; 67, ¶ 54; 68, ¶ 62; 70, ¶ 73; 71, ¶¶ 78, 81-83; 73, ¶ 2(a)), the
summary judgment briefing (R. at 1442, 1458; 1463-65; 1883-84), and at oral argument (R. at 1950-54; 1957-59; 1967).

I. Facts of the Case
Defendant Moab City is a political subdivision of the State of Utah. (R. at 61, ¶ 13; R at 96, ¶ 3.) Defendant Moab City Council
is the legislative body of the City. (R. at 61, ¶ 14; R at 96, ¶ 3.)1 SITLA is the owner of the real property located in Grand
County, Utah, that is the subject of this case (the “Subject Property”). (R. 213.) The Subject Property consists of 175 acres of
land located at the trailhead of the Slick Rock Trail near the ancient Lion's Back sandstone formation. (R. at 4, ¶ 25; 63, ¶ 34;
97, ¶ 10; 122, ¶ 34; 213; 264-67; 294-98; 300-01; 303-04; 306-34; 443.) It sits on a bench above Moab City on the east side of
the Moab-Spanish Valley. (Id.) SITLA and the Developer entered into the MPD with Moab City to develop and construct the
Development on the Subject Property. (R. 213; 264-67; 294-98; 300-01; 303-04; 306-34; 443.) The MPD included, in phased
development, residential subdivisions, commercial development and a luxury hotel resort that was originally limited to just 50
units. (R. at 214-15, 297, 326, and 355.)

In October, 2008, the City and the Developer, joined by SITLA, entered into a Pre-Annexation Agreement (Addendum - Ex. 1)
to annex the Subject Property into the City. (R. 213; 240-262.) The Agreement addressed the parties' development rights and
obligations, including the annexation process, land use and planning processes, subdivision improvements, water and sanitary
sewer services, utilities and roads, as well as drinking *9 water source protections. (R. 213.) The agreement was accompanied
by a detailed MPD plan. (R. 213.)
Upon annexation into the City, the Subject Property, being situated upon environmentally-sensitive lands, was assigned the
City's SAR zoning designation. Also, following annexation, the Developer and the City entered a Development and Phasing
Agreement (“Development Agreement”). (R. 213; 306-334.) The body of the Development Agreement (Addendum - Ex. 2)
limited the luxury resort component of the MPD to just 50 units. (R. at 214-15, 297, 326; 355.) As approved in the preliminary
plan, phase one of Lionsback included a 50-unit hotel of 41,360 square feet and a parking lot of 38,760 square feet with 104
spaces. (R. at 29, ¶ 7; 1328-30; 1445.)
The Development was delayed for several years due to the Great Recession and Lionsback I, among other things. After resolution
of the Recession and Lionsback I, SITLA, the Developer and the City began discussing potential revisions to the MPD to
include, among other things, the 150-room luxury hotel. (R. 214.) SITLA and the Developer claimed that because some of the
hotel/condo suites in the original approval had three bedrooms, the prior City approval of 50 units actually approved 150 units.
(See R. at 215-16.) SITLA and the Developer also claimed that certain members of the City staff agreed with their interpretation
and told them they would not need to submit a new development application to change the Project from its original approval
of 50 units to a 150-room hotel, only to be told by the City attorney at a later time (Addendum - Ex. 3) that they would have
to submit a formal application to obtain planning commission *10 approval for the modification, after a public hearing on the
modification was held. (R. at 215-16; 345-46.)
In response, the Developer filed the Amended MPD Application. (R. at 216; 348-387.) SITLA joined in the application. (Id.)
The proposed changes in the Amended MPD Application included, without limitation, nearly double the hotel footprint from
41,360 square feet to 81,688 square feet and more than double the parking lot from 104 spaces over 38,760 square feet to 230
spaces over 90,160 square feet. (R. at 378, 1445.) It also added “Retail Space,” a “Restaurant,” “Pool Area,” “Back of House
facilities,” a “Conference Center” and “separate Service Entry” to Phase One of the Development. (R. at 363.)
After filing the Amended MPD Application, SITLA and the Developer tried, once again, to secure a commitment from the
City staff to process the Amended MPD Application administratively, rather than subject it to the formal processes required
by the MMC, which would require another public hearing. (R. at 216-17.) On August 11, 2016, The Developer and SITLA
met with the mayor and city manager to discuss the processing of the Amended MPD Application. (R. at 216.) SITLA and the
Developer thought that the mayor and city manager had committed at this meeting that the Amended MPD Application would
be processed by the City as a “Minor” plan amendment under applicable provisions of the MMC, thereby avoiding a future
public hearing on the application. (R. at 216-217.)

have addressed the threshold procedural question of whether Resolution No. 14-2017 approving the ZSA was illegal. Because

ARGUMENT
I. PASSAGE OF RESOLUTION NO. 14-2017 WITHOUT A PUBLIC HEARING WAS ILLEGAL.
The trial court erred in concluding the City Council had authority to enact Resolution No. 14-2017 approving the ZSA, which it
held was immune to review absent a showing of collusion or bad faith, because the City effectively undertook legislative action
without notice and a public hearing when it passed Resolution No. 14-2017, thereby making Resolution No. 14-2017 and the
ZSA illegal. Passing Resolution No. 14-2017 without a public hearing was illegal for two reasons. First, it was a legislative
decision that required a public hearing under LUDMA and the MMC. Second, as a matter of public policy, a legislative body's
approval of a land use settlement agreement requires a public hearing.
A. LUDMA and the MMC require a public hearing.
Under LUDMA, the “adoption” or “any modification of a land use regulation” requires a “public hearing” preceded by “notice
of the date, time, and place of the first public hearing” to consider the modification. Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-205(1)(a).
State law is very clear. “Only a legislative body may amend [] ... any regulation of or within the zoning district; or any other
provision of a land use regulation[,]” id. § 10-9a-503(1)(b)-(c), *21 after a public hearing before the planning commission,
id. § 10-9a-503(2).5 A “land use regulation” under LUDMA “means a legislative decision enacted by ordinance, law, code,
map, resolution, specification, fee, or rule that governs the use or development of land[.]” Id. § 10-9a-103(29)(a) (emphasis
added). Thus, to invoke the requirement of a public hearing, LUDMA requires two things: (1) a “legislative decision” (2) to
modify a “land use regulation.”

B. Public policy requires a public hearing.
There's no doubt that Utah cities have the authority to enter into contracts and agreements. See Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-102(2).
It's also beyond dispute that the settlement of land use disputes is favored under Utah law and that Utah municipalities have
the authority to enter into land use development agreements. Id. “One reason public policy favors the settlement of disputes by
compromise is that this avoids the delay and the public and private expense of litigation.” Utah Dept. of Admin. Servs. v. Public
Service Comm'n, 658 P.2d 601, 613 (Utah 1983). However, since the settlement of land use disputes invariably results in de
facto land use regulations, adjustment of land use policies, decisions that affect neighboring properties, or impacts on the public,
in general, legislative approval of land use settlements requires that the public interest be vindicated through a public hearing.
As a starting point, the Utah Supreme Court has recognized that “[t]he policy in favor of settlements applies to controversies
before regulatory agencies so long as the *26 settlement is not contrary to law and the public interest is safeguarded by review
and approval by the appropriate public authority.” Id. at 613-14 (emphasis added). So, for example, the Utah Supreme Court
upheld a settlement agreement between the Public Service Commission, the Division of Public Utilities and several private
parties - but only after the settlement agreement was reviewed in multiple public hearings before the PSC. See id. at 604, 606,
614-15.
Land use regulation is perhaps the quintessential form of “public regulation.” Indeed, “[c]ity zoning is authorized only as an
exercise of the police power of the state.” Marshall v. Salt Lake City, 105 Utah 111, 119, 141 P.2d 704, 708 (1943). See Smith
Inv. Co. v. Sandy City, 958 P.2d 245, 252-53 (Utah Ct. App. 1998) (citing Western Land Equities, Inc. v. City of Logan, 617
P.2d 388, 390 (Utah 1980)); Town of Alta v. Ben Hame Corp., 836 P.2d 797, 803 (Utah Ct. App. 1992) (“Zoning ordinances
are governmental acts which rest upon the police power[.]”); Utah County v. Baxter, 635 P.2d 61, 65 (Utah 1981) (“Zoning
ordinances are governmental acts which rest upon the police power [.] ...”)7 *27 Hence, every zoning-related action - whether
undertaken by means of a development agreement, a settlement agreement, or the amending thereof - being derivative of the
state's police powers, must “protect the health, morals, peace and good order of the community, to promote its welfare in trade,
commerce, industry, and manufacture, and to carry out every appropriate object contemplated in the creation of the municipal
corporation.” State v. Hutchinson, 624 P.2d 1116, 1125 (Utah 1980).
That is why some courts have held development agreements or land use settlement agreements to be illegal, per se, reasoning
“that a municipality may not contract away the exercise of its zoning power by settlement[.]” Dacy v. Village of Ruidoso, 845
P.2d 793, 797 (N.M. 1992) (citing Judith W. Wegner, Moving Toward the Bargaining Table: Contract Zoning, Development
Agreements, and the Theoretical Foundations of Government Land Use Deals, 65 N.C. L. Rev. 976, 982-83 (1987)). See also
Warner Co. v. Sutton, 644 A.2d 656, 661-62 (N.J. Super. A.D. 1994). The common rationale seems to be that such an agreement
“denigrates the statutory process because it purports to commit itself to certain action before listening to the public's comments
on that action[]... to the possible detriment of affected landowners and the community as a whole.” Dacy, 845 P.2d at 797. See

That did not occur here. As admitted by the City, there was no public hearing to consider approval of Resolution No. 14-2017 or
the ZSA. (R. at 67, ¶ 55; R. at 99, ¶ 22.) As our Utah courts have recognized, “[i]t is beyond question [] ... that public hearings and
citizen comments are a legitimate source of information for city council members to consider in making legislative decisions.”
Bradley v. Payson City Corp., 2003 UT 16, ¶ 28, 70 P.3d 47, 55 (citing Harmon City, Inc. v. Draper City, 2000 UT App 31, ¶
26, 997 P.2d 321 (noting that “a city may rely on the concerns of interested citizens when performing legislative functions”).
In this case, “Plaintiffs would have presented evidence at a public hearing regarding impact to water recharge rates for springs
which are the primary source for Lucy Wallingford's water right, water quality issues, aquifer contamination, storm water
runoff impacts, drainage impacts, ground water concerns, water conservation issues, drinking water contamination, sewer issues,
increased contamination and leaching risks from the nearby landfill, recreational impacts, traffic flow and congestion impacts,
road usage issues, public health and safety impacts, and construction impacts based on the proposed changes to MPD.” (R. at
127-28, ¶ 63.) Thus, the Appellants have been prejudiced because there is a very reasonable likelihood that, if given a chance to
hear these and other public concerns at a public hearing, the City Council would have voted differently on Resolution 14-2017,
which approved the ZSA on a 3-2 vote with one member of the City Council *31 wanting to reverse her vote immediately
after it was taken. (R. at 1425; 1616.) See, e.g., Potter v. South Salt Lake City, 2018 UT 21, ¶ 2, P.3d (“we conclude that a party
can establish prejudice by showing a reasonable likelihood that the error changed the land use authority's decision”).9
In short, the serious deprivation of the right to public participation, coupled with concerns about the abdication of the police
power, have led many courts to conclude that agreements like the ZSA are illegal and void altogether. The Appellants, however,
don't think this Court should go so far as to entirely ban such agreements due to the important and countervailing public policy
interests served by the resolution of land use disputes through settlement or development agreements.
The proper balance between the right to public participation and the expediency of development agreements or land use
settlement agreements has been achieved by those jurisdictions that have judicially or legislatively required all such agreements
to be subjected to a public hearing prior to approval. The structure of LUDMA is consistent with those jurisdictions. The
Appellants would therefore urge this Court to follow the approach taken in those jurisdictions, and outlined in LUDMA, and
hold that the ZSA is illegal because it was approved through Resolution No. 14-2017 by the City's legislative body without a
public hearing. Because the trial court incorrectly held that the City Council had *32 authority to enact Resolution No. 14-2017
approving the ZSA (and that it was immune to review absent a showing of collusion or bad faith) this Court should remand
Resolution No. 14-2017 for a public hearing.
II. ALTERNATIVELY, THE TRIAL COURT ERRED IN CONCLUDING THAT RESOLUTION
NO. 14-2017 WAS IMMUNE FROM REVIEW BY THE MOAB CITY APPEAL AUTHORITY
BECAUSE RESOLUTION NO. 14-2017 WAS A “LAND USE DECISION” UNDER LUDMA.
There is no such thing as a nondecision under LUDMA. Land use applications, such as the Amended MPD Application, are
resolved either through legislative or administrative decisions. The former are subject to a public hearing, the latter to an appeal
to the appeal authority. Here, it is undisputed that the Amended MPD Application was resolved by City Council passage of
Resolution No. 14-2017, which authorized the mayor to sign the ZSA. If that was not a “legislative decision” that “governs the
use or development of land” or modifies or amends a “land use regulation” by “resolution” within the meaning of LUDMA,
as argued in Part I, above, then it was an administrative “land use decision” by the City Council acting in its capacity as the
“land use authority” and subject to appeal to the Moab City Appeal Authority. See Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-103(29)(c)(i). The
trial court erred when it held that Resolution No. 14-2017 was beyond LUDMA classification and immune to review absent a
showing of bad faith or collusion because, as the following analysis demonstrates, Resolution No. 14-2017 effected a “land use
decision” entitling Appellants to an administrative appeal under the plain language of LUDMA.

The “nondecision” method of placing parties affected by a land use authority's decisions into an unappealable, unreviewable
administrative no man's land under LUDMA *33 was squarely rejected by the Utah Supreme Court in Gillmor v. Summit
County, 2010 UT 69, ¶ 21, 246 P.3d 102, 107 (“the noncompliance of Gillmor's applications does not render the County's
rejections ‘nondecisions' and does not shield these rejections from ... review”). Rather, under the plain language of LUDMA a
“land use decision” is “an administrative decision of a land use authority or appeal authority regarding: (a) a land use permit;
(b) a land use application; or (c) the enforcement of a land use regulation, land use permit, or development agreement.” Utah
Code Ann. § 10-9a-103(27). The ZSA met all three touchstones for constituting a “land use decision.”
First, it decided that “with respect to future final plats, permits and other reviews required to be undertaken by the City pursuant
to the City Code” that those reviews and permits “will occur administratively by the City Planning Department, without a
requirement for a public hearing.” (R. at 574, ¶ 7 (emphasis added).) Second, it decided that the Amended MPD Application
proposed Minor Amendments, not “Major” ones. (R. at 573, ¶ 2 (emphasis added).) Third, it decided not to enforce MMC §
17.65.130's requirement that “[a]mendments that change the character, basic design, building density and intensity, open space
or any other requirements and conditions contained in the MPD shall not be permitted without prior review and approval by
the planning commission.” It also waived the enforcement of other land use regulations, such as the processes governing plat
submissions and other land use approvals, as explained above.
Under LUDMA, the Appellants have a statutory right “to appeal a land use authority's decision to a separate appeal authority,”
Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-302(5)(b)(ii). The establishment of an appeal authority is not optional. Rather, “[e]ach municipality ...
*34 shall ... establish one or more appeal authorities to hear and decide ... appeals from decisions applying the land use
ordinances” whereby “each adversely affected person shall timely and specifically challenge a land use authority's decision, in
accordance with local ordinance.” Id. § 10-9a-701 (1)-(2).
As required by LUDMA, the City has created an appeal authority and enacted multiple ordinances providing for appeals to that
appeal authority. For example, MMC § 17.65.080.C. provides, “[n]o person may challenge in district court any decision under
this chapter until that person has exhausted all administrative remedies and a final decision is issued by the appeal body [,]”
suggesting that “any decision” under Chapter 17.65 may be appealed by any person. Id. See also, e.g., id. § 17.67.090.B (site
plan appeals go the appeal authority); id. § 17.72.100 (setting out an appeal authority matrix). The MMC expressly provided
that “any. person. adversely affected by a decision administering or interpreting a zoning ordinance may appeal that decision
applying the zoning ordinance ...” to the Appeal Authority. (R. 232; 1637 (quoting former MMC § 17.72.150).)10
As shown above, Resolution No. 14-2017 was certainly a “land use decision” (Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-103(27)) “applying” (id.
§ 10-9a-701(1)(b)) or “interpreting” ( MMC *35 § 17.72.150) the provisions of the MMC. After all, by virtue of the ZSA,
Section 17.65.130 of the MPD ordinance was interpreted so that the Developer's Amended MPD Application “is deemed to
be a Minor Amendment” instead of a Major Amendment, among other things. (R. at 573.) As such, LUDMA and the MMC
give Appellants the right to an appeal.
These texts cannot be casually avoided merely because this land use decision was couched in the language of the ZSA, which
the Appellees and the trial court refer to as a settlement agreement (even though it wasn't called a settlement agreement in the
ZSA, itself, and didn't have many of the hallmarks of a settlement agreement such as a general release, covenant not to sue,
etc.). Rather, in determining whether particular government action constitutes a “land use decision” this Court has interpreted
the phrase “land use decision” broadly, looking to the reality of what the particular municipal action accomplished. Our courts
do not believe in “nondecisions” under LUDMA.
For example, in Green v. Brown, 2014 UT App 155, 330 P.3d 737, this Court held that even though a planner may have lacked
authority to issue a building permit, the planner's decision nonetheless started the appeal process for appeals to the appeal
authority. Id. at ¶ 18. Importantly,
The planner's decision to issue a building permit allowing the ROW to be used as a driveway is not rendered a “nondecision”
by Brown's argument that the planner lacked the authority to issue the permit. Put another way, Brown's belief that the planner

lacked the proper authority to make that land use decision did not alter the reality that a land use decision had been issued and
that the decision, properly issued or not, had adversely affected her property interests.

Id. at ¶ 16. Likewise, the City's decision to wrap its land use decisions in the swaddling clothes of a “Zoning Status Agreement”
also “did not alter the reality that a land use *36 decision had been issued and that the decision, properly issued or not, had

Moreover, to prevent parties from eviscerating by agreement important statutory processes such as public notice, the right of
public participation, and third-party rights to appeal, courts look to the substance of the settlement or stipulation to determine
if the decision, if made through the normal land use processes, would have been a “land use decision.”
For example, in Murphy Citizens Advisory Committee v. Josephine County, 878 P.2d 414 (Or. 1994), the Oregon Supreme Court
held that a stipulation between the county and the applicant to settle a mandamus action was appealable by a citizen group to
the Land Use Board of Appeals (LUBA) as a “land use decision” under a statutory land use scheme not unlike Utah's LUDMA.

The County had a choice between agreeing to the terms of the stipulation and continuing its defense of the mandamus action. The

Because the stipulation between the County and Copeland was a final “land use decision” within the meaning of ORS
197.015(10)(a)(A), LUBA [the Land Use Board of Appeals] has subject matter jurisdiction over MCAC's appeal from that

*37 Id. Likewise, because the ZSA was a final “land use decision” under LUDMA, the Moab City Appeal Authority has
subject matter jurisdiction over Appellants' appeal from that document. See MMC § 17.72.130.A.
Aside from the fact that the Appellees' contention that land use settlements are immune from review as “land use decisions”
ignores the text of LUDMA and the MMC, the concept that development agreements, zoning status agreements or land use
settlement agreements are somehow immune to the processes mandated by state and municipal law, such as public notice,
public hearing and the right to appeal, is also fundamentally flawed for all of the same reasons that other jurisdictions have
determined that such agreements are ultra vires and illegal per se or, at a minimum, must be subjected to the requirement of
a public hearing. See Part I.
Finally, the concept that such agreements are immune from the requirements of LUDMA and municipal appeal authority
ordinances such as MMC § 17.72.150 has no legislative authorization or support under Utah case law. The position advanced by
Appellees - that cities can avoid land use appeals by the mere expedient of shrouding land use decisions within the encasements
of development or settlement agreements - is completely uncharted territory in Utah.
In sum, the “reality” - to borrow from Green v. Brown - is that the ZSA was a “land use decision” under the text of LUDMA.
As explained above, because it “applied a land use regulation to a particular land use application, person or parcel” it “may be
appealed to an appeal authority.” Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-707(6). Consequently, the trial court lacked subject matter jurisdiction
and erred when it “leap-frogged over” the appeal processes *38 mandated by LUDMA and the MMC. See Patterson v.

III. THE TRIAL COURT INCORRECTLY CREATED AND THEN
MISAPPLIED AN ERRONEOUS STANDARD OF JUDICIAL REVIEW.
Even if the law did not require a public hearing before the legislative enactment of Resolution No. 14-2017 as argued in Part
I, and even if the law didn't entitle Appellants to administrative review of the City Council's administrative action to the Moab
City Appeal Authority, as alternatively argued in Part II, the trial court still erred when it created a standard of judicial review
for land use agreements that has never been applied before by any Utah court and then upheld the ZSA under that standard
without giving Appellants the opportunity to conduct discovery, present evidence or identify disputed issues of fact under the
newly-minted standard. See Utah R. Civ. P. 56(a). As the following analysis demonstrates, these errors were fundamentally
flawed, unfair and should be reversed.
*39 A. The trial court created and used the wrong standard of review.
The “collusion or bad faith” standard created by the trial court is nowhere to be found in Utah land use case law or LUDMA.
There was no land use case law from any other jurisdiction cited in the trial court's decision to support this standard of review.
In fact, there was no such case law cited or discussed by the parties in their briefing below that utilized or applied this standard
of review in a land use context. This standard appeared for the first time in the trial court's decision with no explanation as
to where it came from.
Counsel exhaustively searched for, but never found, case law outside of Utah requiring parties challenging a municipal land use
settlement to demonstrate that the settlement was the product of “collusion or bad faith between the parties to the agreement ...
to completely eviscerate the ordinances.” (R. at 1920, ¶ 4.)12 Instead, counsel's search for such a standard revealed the case law
holding that settlement of land use disputes is an illegal abdication of the police power or, alternatively, that such agreements
must be subjected to normal land use processes such as the requirement of a public hearing and/or appeal, as explained above.
*40 Thus far, the way Utah courts appear to have treated similar land use agreements is to focus on the modality by which they
were entered, classifying them as either legislative acts or administrative acts, and to then apply the appropriate, statutorilymandated standard of review, depending upon whether the action was legislative or administrative. For example, the Court in
Suarez, 2012 UT 72, first determined that passage of Ordinance 454 approving amendments to a development agreement was a
“legislative” act and then applied the LUDMA-dictated standard of review. See id. at ¶ 57 (quoting Utah Code § 17-27a-801(3)
(a)(ii) (the county version of LUDMA). Cf. id. § 10-9a-801(3)(b) (the same provision in the municipal version of LUDMA).
As explained above, the plain language of LUDMA only gave the trial court authority to do one of two things: (1) treat Resolution
No. 14-2017 as a “land use regulation” (or modification of a “land use regulation”) under Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-801(3)(a)
and “determine only whether[] ...[it] was enacted contrary to[] state or federal law; and ... [whether] it is reasonably debatable
that the land use regulation is consistent with [LUDMA;]”; or (2) treat Resolution No. 14-2017 as a land use “decision of a land
use authority” under Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-801(3)(b) and determine whether that decision was “arbitrary and capricious; or
illegal.” While the trial did purport to conclude “that the settlement agreement is not ‘arbitrary, capricious or illegal’ as Plaintiffs
allege[,]” (R. at 1920, ¶ 5), it do so only after applying its “collusion or bad faith” standard instead of the statutory criteria set
forth in Utah Code Ann. § 10-9a-801(3)(c).
That section of LUDMA provides that “[a] decision is arbitrary and capricious if the decision is not supported by substantial
evidence in the record.” Id. § 10-9a-801(3)(c)(i). *41 It also provides that “[a] decision is illegal if the decision is ... based

on an incorrect interpretation of a land use regulation; or ... contrary to law.” Id. § 10-9a-801(3)(c)(ii). The trial court did not
have the authority to ignore these statutorily-mandated and prescribed standards of review or to superimpose upon them the
additional burden of demonstrating “collusion or bad faith.”
Had the Appellants known that they would be required to show “collusion” between SITLA, the Developer and the City or
their “bad faith” in order to satisfy the requisite standard of review, the Appellants would have handled this case with an

Appellants had no way of knowing that they would be required to show “collusion” or “bad faith” until after the trial court's
decision was rendered. By then, of course, it was too late for Appellants to make their case. Simply put, the trial court shouldn't
have created a new standard to begin with and it certainly shouldn't have held the Appellants to that evidentiary standard without
first giving Appellants notice this standard would be applicable, followed by an opportunity to conduct discovery and present
evidence under that new standard.
*42 B. The trial court incorrectly applied this new standard.
Assuming, for purposes of argument, that the trial court's standard of collusion or bad faith is, in fact, the standard that Appellants
were required to meet in order to show that Resolution No. 14-2017 was arbitrary, capricious or illegal, the trial court erred in
concluding “there has been no collusion between the parties in entering into the ZSA” (R. at 1920, ¶ 5) “to use a settlement
process to completely eviscerate the ordinances.” (R. at 1920, ¶ 4.)
“Collusion is defined as ‘an agreement between two or more persons to defraud another under the forms of law, or to
accomplish an illegal purpose.”’ City of Kansas City v. Ku, 282 S.W.3d 23, 32 (Mo. App. W.D. 2009) (quoting Stark Liquidation
Co. v. Florists' Mut. Ins. Co., 243 S.W.3d 385, 400 (Mo.App. E.D.2007) (quotation marks and citation omitted)). Accord
Commonwealth v. Parisi, 873 A.2d 3, 9 (Pa. Cmwlth. 2005) (quoting Black's Law Dictionary 281 (8th Ed. 2004) (“The term
‘collusion’ is defined as ‘[a]n agreement to defraud another or to do or obtain something forbidden by law.”’).)
There's no dispute that the ZSA was an agreement between two or more persons. And the record, when viewed in a light most
favorable to Appellants, as is required on summary judgment, shows that SITLA and the Developer wanted to accomplish the
illegal purpose of, among other things, avoiding a public hearing for this and any future land use approvals or decisions. (See,
e.g., R. at 389, 499, 525-26, 532, 558, 1364, & 1389-90.) The record shows that the Developer's desire to avoid a public hearing
is what was “driving” the entire ZSA. The Developer unabashedly stated “the concern is this potential reopening of the whole
thing” (R. at 532) and the City Attorney said “[t]hat's really been *43 the main bone of contention.” (R. at 558.) So, to avoid
the requirement of a public hearing, the Appellees agreed to a process in the ZSA that eliminated all public hearings and altered
the processes for approval otherwise required by law. Collusion to avoid statutorily-required public participation is repugnant
to the very foundations of democracy and the legitimate exercise of the police power. It is illegal, as demonstrated above.
The Appellees claim that the ZSA resolved larger concerns and issues between them, such as the City's power to regulate
SITLA altogether. Being an agency of the State of Utah, the argument goes, SITLA could have pulled the Project from the
City's jurisdiction any time it wanted. However, this contention actually helps establish - rather than avoid - the presence of
collusion because SITLA couldn't have withdrawn from the City's jurisdiction had it wanted to. Hence this argument appears
to be nothing more than elaborate pretext for the collusion to avoid public participation.
SITLA had agreed to allow the property to be annexed into Moab City in the Pre-Annexation Agreement. (See R. at 239-262.)
That agreement provided, “SITLA has joined in this Agreement to evidence its consent to the terms and conditions of this

In sum, while the Appellants dispute that the trial court's “collusion or bad faith” standard was the correct standard for reviewing

In fact, the ZSA is nothing more than a written memorialization of the concerted effort of the Appellees to avoid LUDMA, the
MMC and the legal requirement of a public hearing, as is amply demonstrated in the record. The record is tainted with their

ZSA. Thus the trial court erred when it refused to construe this evidence in a light most favorable to the Appellants, which

dispute as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law”).

govern the people so that private parties may gain vested rights in them.” United States v. Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints,
5 Utah 361, 369, 15 P. 473, 477 (1887). Consequently, at the very least, such agreements should receive some level of public scrutiny
as a predicate to their validity to vindicate the public interest inherent in this somewhat treacherous exercise of the police power.
