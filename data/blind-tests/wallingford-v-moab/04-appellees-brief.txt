*1 INTRODUCTION
This matter is an appeal of an action filed pursuant to U.C.A. § 10-9a-801 seeking review of local government land-use decisions.
The Appellants (collectively referred to as Living Rivers) appeal the grant of summary judgment dismissing their claims against
the City of Moab (City of Moab), the Utah School and Institutional Trust Lands Administration (SITLA or the Agency), and
the land developer, Moab LB Land Company, LLC (Moab LB or the Developer).
Living Rivers opposes the development of the Lionsback Resort, a development planned on 175 acres of land owned by SITLA
on Sand Flats Road within the City (the Development). SITLA and Moab LB propose a mixed-use development including a

hotel, single family homes, guest facilities, employee housing, and open space. Land use entitlements for the Lionsback Resort
were originally granted by the City in 2008, but development was delayed during a prior appeal by another group of plaintiffs,
which included Living Rivers. The prior litigation was resolved in favor of the City. Moab Local Green Party, et al. v. City of
Moab, 276 P.3d 1230 (Utah App. 2012), cert. den. 288 P.3d 1045 (Utah 2012) (Lionsback I).
Following the conclusion of Lionsback I the Developer sought to proceed with the development of the site. In the Spring of 2016
the Developer approached the City with a new plan to reconfigure the design to eliminate a series of “casitas” and consolidate
them into a hotel with a total of 150 rooms.
In the course of discussions between the Developer and City staff a dispute arose as to the extent of existing land use entitlements
for the Development. On June 30, 2016, *2 the Developer submitted an application to amend the Lionsback project to
include a hotel with 50 three-bedroom units, or a total of 150 rooms. On September 12, 2016, the City, through undersigned
counsel, informed the Developer that the change application would constitute a major change under the City's Master Planned
Development Ordinance (MPD), thereby triggering the review processes outlined in the ordinance.
In October, 2016, SITLA became involved on behalf of the Developer and informed the City that it might invoke its statutory
exemption from local land use jurisdiction - thereby removing the Lionsback project from any local zoning review. The City and
SITLA then engaged in a series of negotiations through the end of 2016 to resolve the dispute. The result of those negotiations
was a document, the Zoning Status Agreement (ZSA), by which the City, SITLA, and the Developer agreed to a number of
substantive and procedural changes to the Development and the review of same. Following a series of briefings and reviews at
public City Council meetings the ZSA was approved by the Moab City Council on February 28, 2017.
Living Rivers then commenced an action in the trial court alleging, inter alia, that the ZSA was unlawful, arbitrary, or capricious.
The trial court subsequently granted summary judgment in favor of SITLA, Moab LB, and the City. The instant appeal followed.
STATEMENT OF THE ISSUES
1. Did Living Rivers fail to preserve its appeal argument that the approval of the *3 ZSA was a legislative act, and therefore
illegal under the Utah Land Use Development and Management Act (LUDMA) because it was not approved pursuant to a
public hearing process?
Standard of Review: To consider an issue on appeal the litigant must have raised and litigated the issue before the trial court;
if a party fails to do so the issue will be deemed waived on appeal. State v. Johnson, 416 P.3d 443, ¶15 (Utah 2017).
Preservation: Living Rivers consistently argued before the trial court that the City acted arbitrarily and capriciously, in violation
of its Master Planned Development Ordinance, by agreeing in the ZSA that the Lionsback change application would be
processed as a minor change. The current appeal presents an issue never raised or litigated before the trial court, as discussed
infra; R. 1440-1465 (Living Rivers' Response to Motion for Summary Judgment).
2. Was the approval of the ZSA, a municipal settlement agreement, lawful, supported by substantial evidence, and not arbitrary
or capricious?
Standard of Review: In the appeal of a local government decision the appellate court reviews the trial court decision, according
it no deference, and applying the statutorily defined standard of review to determine whether the decision was arbitrary,
capricious, or illegal. McElhaney v. City of Moab, 423 P.3d 1284, ¶ 26. A local government legislative decision is valid if it is
reasonably debatable that it would uphold the general welfare. Bradley v. Payson City Corp., 70 P.3d 47, ¶ 14 (Utah, 2003).
Questions of law are reviewed for correctness, with no deference to legal conclusions below. Snyder v. Murray City Corp.,
73 P.3s 325 ¶ 16, 17 (Utah 2003). Decisions of a local government *4 to enter into a contract are lawful and will be upheld

SUMMARY OF THE ARGUMENT
First, the argument that the approval of the ZSA was legislative and subject to the public hearing requirements of LUDMA
was never raised by Living Rivers before the trial court, and was not preserved for review. Living Rivers actually argued the
opposite: that the decision was an administrative one, and that it was arbitrary and capricious under Moab ordinances. The
omission is fatal to its core argument.
The City and SITLA/Developer had a bona fide dispute as to the scope of 2008 era land use entitlements applicable to the
Lionsback project. That dispute precipitated efforts to arrive at a settlement agreement, which took the form of the ZSA. The
decision to approve the ZSA was indeed a legislative act informed by forward looking considerations of: a) retaining City land
use jurisdiction in the face of a threat by the State of Utah to withdraw from local zoning; b) improving on historical land
use entitlements for the benefit of the public welfare; and c) avoiding the expense and uncertainty of litigation with SITLA.
The agreement was approved in good faith, via arms-length bargaining, and after lengthy public briefings and debate by the
City Council.
*10 The City possesses independent authority to legislate and act in accordance with its elected leaders' conception of the
public interest. That authority is not derived from LUDMA, particularly where, as here, the LUDMA provides that the cities
have no jurisdiction to regulate state lands.
There is strong public policy favoring settlement of disputes. And review of a legislative decision is highly deferential; courts
will uphold the decision if it is reasonably debatable. In the context of public contracting, Utah courts uphold the decision of a
local government absent evidence of fraud, collusion, or bad faith by the parties.
Utah law also allows local governments and state agencies substantial flexibility to waive or modify permitting requirements
when they determine it is in their interest to do so. This authority to shape public obligations is not constrained by any public
hearing requirements in LUDMA, and the City was therefore not obliged to seek public testimony prior to approving its
agreement with SITLA. Similarly, the City was not constrained by its ordinances, and could engage in some modification of
permitting procedures because it was contracting with SITLA.
The approval of the ZSA was not within the jurisdiction of the City's administrative Appeal Authority, which hears local zoning
appeals. As noted above, the decision was legislative and therefore not subject to review before an inferior adjudicatory body,
whose jurisdiction is limited to review of questions arising under Moab ordinances. The decision to approve the ZSA is weighted

with state law considerations as to the extent of local government contracting power; the ability to waive permitting obligations
under the Interlocal Cooperation Act; and the scope of SITLA's exemption from local zoning. *11 These considerations rest on
questions of state law, not the interpretation of local zoning ordinances, and are therefore beyond the jurisdiction of the appeal

a state agency (such as SITLA) are inherently political questions vested in the discretion of the City Council and not subject
to administrative review.
The decision to approve the ZSA was therefore proper, supported by substantial evidence, and not illegal. And that same decision
was not subject to review before the Moab Appeal Authority.
ARGUMENT
1. LIVING RIVERS NEVER ARGUED BEFORE THE TRIAL COURT THAT THE APPROVAL
OF THE ZONING STATUS AGREEMENT WAS A LEGISLATIVE ACT SUBJECT TO
LUDMA AND, THEREFORE, FAILED TO PRESERVE THE ISSUE FOR REVIEW.
In its opening brief Living Rivers belatedly adopts the position that the approval of the ZSA was a legislative act that must
be approved in conformity with the public hearing requirements of the Utah Land Use Development and Management Act
(LUDMA), U.C.A § 10-9a-l 01 et seq. This position was never advanced before the trial court, rendering the current argument
defective for failure to preserve the issue for appellate review.
To consider an issue on appeal the litigant must have raised and litigated the issue before the trial court; if a party fails to
do so the issue will be deemed waived on appeal. State v. Johnson, 416 P.3d 443, ¶15 (Utah 2017). To preserve an issue for
appellate review is must be supported by evidence and relevant legal authority and raised *12 by the party asserting error in
such a manner that the trial court has an opportunity to rule on the question. Id. (citations omitted). The sole exceptions to the
preservation rule are plain error, ineffective assistance of counsel, and exceptional circumstances. Id. ¶ 20-24. Where an issue
is not preserved for review the party advancing the issue must establish that an exception to the preservation rule applies. Id. at
¶ 27. Last, exceptional circumstances, as an exception to the preservation rule, is applied “sparingly,” reserving it only for the
most unusual circumstances where failure to consider the issue would have resulted in “manifest injustice.” Id. at ¶ 29.
In this case Living Rivers failed to argue before the trial court that the decision of the City was legislative and therefore subject
to a LUDMA public hearing. The claims advance in the trial court were that: a) the City erred by refusing to convene an
administrative appeal authority to review Living Rivers' claim that the Developer's change application was a “major change”
under the MPD ordinance; and b) the City acted arbitrarily, capriciously, and illegally, in violation of the MPD ordinance when
it entered into the ZSA. See R. 59-73; Amended Complaint ¶ 45-49; 54; 58; and 73.5
Nowhere in the trial court did Living Rivers advance the argument that the decision to approve the ZSA was legislative and
subject to the LUDMA public hearing requirement. Living Rivers' sole argument was that the Moab MPD ordinance compelled
a classification of the Lionsback change application as a “major change” *13 requiring a public hearing. See R. 1460; Plaintiffs'
Memorandum in Opposition to Intervenor Defendant's Motion for Summary Judgment; p. 21. The argument that the City
violated U.C.A. § 10-9a-205 by approving the ZSA without a public hearing was not made before the trial court, and that
omission results in the failure to preserve the issue for review here.
Likewise, no exception to the preservation rule applies. The parties argued as to the correct standard of review, with Living
Rivers asserting that the action under review was administrative, and therefore subject to an arbitrary and capricious level of
review. R.71. This was clearly a calculated tactical decision designed to persuade the trial court to apply a less deferential
“administrative” standard of review, rather than the “reasonably debatable” standard applicable to legislative decisions. See
Citizens for a Better Community v. City of Springville, 979 P.2d 332 ¶ 24 (Utah 1999). This tactical decision not to pursue a
particular legal theory in no way implicates ineffective assistance, plain error, or exceptional circumstances; nor has Living

Rivers established any facts that would bring it within the ambit of those exceptions.6 Concurrently, this is not an instance of a
rare procedural anomaly that would have prevented Living Rivers from making this claim. As noted, Living Rivers amended its
Complaint once, and it had a full opportunity to litigate these types of claims, which are purely legal in nature, at *14 summary
judgment. Although it argued for a public hearing under the Moab ordinance, it failed to advance the claim under U.C.A. §
10-9a-205, thereby preventing the City from meeting that argument head on and the trial court from ruling on the question.
2. THE AUTHORITY OF A MUNICIPALITY TO SETTLE
CLAIMS IS NOT CONDITIONED UPON A PUBLIC HEARING.
Setting aside for the moment Living Rivers' failure to preserve the issue, the argument that the ZSA approval was void for lack
of a public hearing under U.C.A. § 10-9a-205(1)(a) fails on the merits. The City agrees that a decision to settle a dispute is
committed to the policy judgment of the legislative body. However, Living Rivers misses the mark because such a decision is
not made under the enabling authority of LUDMA.
First, municipalities have the authority to enter into contracts and to sue and be sued. U.C.A § 10-1-202. The power of local
governments to sue implicates the right to settle disputes:
Unless forbidden by charter of applicable general law, a municipal or other public corporation has the power to settle and
compromise disputed claims in its favor or against it before or after suit has begun on it ... Power to compromise disputed
claims may be expressly conferred or may be implied, as to the power to sue and be sued, or as a necessary incident of its
power to contract.

17 McQuillin Mun. Corporations, § 48.20 (3rd Ed.). In Utah, local governments are deemed to have independent authority,
separate from specific grants in enabling legislation, to enact local laws deemed to be in the public interest, and courts will
not interfere in those local choices unless it is arbitrary or directly prohibited by other law or constitutional provisions. State
v. Hutchinson, 624 P.2d 1116, 1126 (Utah 1980).
*15 In this context there are no express statutory or common law rules that require local governments to engage in any particular
process prior to the settlement or resolution of legal claims.7 Local governments in Utah routinely settle land related disputes
with developers and others, and there is no express requirement for a public hearing prior to approval of those settlements,
either in LUDMA8 or under general municipal law. Thus, the argument advanced by Living Rivers in this appeal is plainly an
attempt to stretch the public process rules for land use ordinances beyond their original purpose - and it should be rejected on
that basis. Furthermore, the argument runs contrary to other applicable statutes and doctrines.
a. Settlement of Disputes is Favored.
Settlement of disputes is favored by public policy because compromise avoids delay and the private and public costs of litigation.
Utah Dept. of Admin Services v. Public Service Commission, 658 P.2d 601, 613-14 (Utah 1983). Provided that a settlement is
not contrary to law and the public interest is safeguarded by approval by the appropriate public authority, the settlement will be
approved. Id. A court's role in reviewing a challenge to a public settlement is limited and confined to determining *16 whether
the action was reasonable when measured against the applicable legislation and public policy. Id. at 616.
b. Public Entities Have the Authority to Waive or Modify Permitting Requirements by Contract.
Living Rivers argues at length that municipalities must comply with their own land use ordinances, and that the ZSA represents
a violation of Moab's land use ordinances. While it is true that cities must comply with their local laws, Utah law allows public
entities significant leeway to modify their ordinances as they pertain to agreements with other governmental agencies. State

© 2026 Thomson Reuters. No claim to original U.S. Government Works.

9

Lucy WALLINGFORD, Kiley Miller, John Rzeczycki, Carol..., 2018 WL 10399913...

and local governments have express authority to enter into contracts under the Interlocal Cooperation Act (ICA), U.C.A. §
11-13-1301 et seq. The statute governs contracts between “public agencies,” which include local governments and the “state or
any department, division, or agency ...” U.C.A. § 11-13-103(19)(a) and (b). Under the ICA governmental entities may enter into
contracts they deem appropriate for joint or cooperative action. U.C.A. § 11-13-202(1). This contracting power includes the
authority of a party to an agreement to exempt another public agency from “permit or fee requirements” that might otherwise
be applicable. U.C.A. § 11-13-202(3)(a)(ii). The contracting power under the ICA is construed broadly in the absence of other
countervailing legislation. Utah County v. Ivie, 137 P.3d 797, ¶14-15 (Utah 2006).
In this case the ICA authorizes a local government, such as Moab, to modify its permitting or land use processes insofar as
it deems doing so to be reasonable for an agreement with another governmental entity, such as SITLA. In fact, the ZSA does
*17 modify permitting rules in a manner consistent with the flexibility accorded by the ICA. Even if U.C.A. § 10-9a-205(1)
(a) were held to apply here, Section 202(1) of the ICA authorizes the City of Moab to alter or streamline the permitting process
pursuant to its agreement with SITLA.
c. SITLA is Exempt from Local Land Use Jurisdiction.
LUDMA makes clear that:
[u]nless otherwise provided by law, nothing in this chapter may be construed as giving any municipality jurisdiction over
property owned by the state or the United States.

U.C.A. § 10-9a-304(1); see also U.C.A. § 63A-5-206(6)(d)(exempting state development from local zoning authority).9 The
SITLA enabling statute further provides for the development of state trust lands outside of the processes otherwise required
by local governments for private development. See U.C.A. § 53C-4-102 (authorizing the subdivision and sale of state lands).
Thus, state lands are generally not subject to any degree of local regulation, either under LUDMA or other local ordinances.
i. Under LUDMA, Local Ordinances Affecting State Lands are not Subject to a Public Hearing.
At its core, the argument of Living Rivers rests on in incorrect interpretation of LUDMA. In the interpretation of a statute a
reviewing court presumes that the legislature *18 used each term advisedly, according to its usually accepted meaning. Marion
Energy, Inc. v. KFJ Ranch Partnership, 267 P.3d 863, ¶ 14 (Utah 2011). When interpreting conflicting statutory provisions a
court seeks to avoid an interpretation that creates absurd results. Id. at ¶ 26.
The state lands zoning exemption in U.C.A. § 10-9a-304, which itself is part of LUDMA, states that “nothing contained in this
chapter” gives local governments jurisdiction over state land. But, Living Rivers relies on another provision of that same chapter
of LUDMA, U.C.A. § 10-9a-205(1)(a), as the purported authority to assert jurisdiction over state lands through a public hearing
requirement. Though enactment of a general land use ordinance under the authority of LUDMA requires a public hearing, the
language quoted above suggests that ordinances affecting state lands are beyond the reach of LUDMA. If local government
action affecting state lands is to have validity, it rests on sources other than LUDMA.
In this case Moab's zoning jurisdiction over the Lionsback Resort is predicated upon the various agreements entered into with
SITLA, not the exercise of land use police power under LUDMA. Thus, a proper reading of Sections 205 and 304 is that if a
local government enacts or modifies legislation specifically applicable to a government agency (such as the ZSA here), it need
not comply with the general public hearing requirements of section 205. To hold otherwise here would be to render the zoning
exemption in section 304 meaningless by conditioning its' exercise on planning commission and public hearing review (with the
attendant possibility that review might result in a denial). Indeed, the view advanced by Living Rivers leads to an absurd result
because the zoning *19 exemption is: a) either subject to public hearing review, and therefore not an exemption at all; or b)

10

subject to public hearing review which would be no more than an empty gesture because, regardless of the result of the hearing,
the state is exempt from local control. This presents an absurd interpretation of LUDMA which should be rejected out of hand.
d. The Zoning Status Agreement is a Lawful and Reasonable Compromise Between Governmental Entities.
The record in this case establishes that there was a genuine dispute between the parties as to the scope and extent of the 2008
Lionsback entitlements relative to the changes proposed by SITLA and the Developer in 2016. SITLA and the Developer
point to a number of purported concessions made by City staff as to the interpretation of existing entitlements. R. 11395
(SITLA testimony regarding developer reliance upon staff statements). In response the City noted that neither SITLA nor the
Developer could reasonably rely upon any purported staff statements because the underlying development agreements required
modifications to be in writing and approved by the City Council. R. 1135; R. 345; See also R. 312 (amendments to Development
and Phasing Agreement required to be in writing signed by both parties). Both sides pointed to portions of the 2008 approvals
as supporting their respective positions on the scope of those entitlements. And SITLA acknowledged that the 2008 approval
documents contained “ambiguities.” R. 1395 (testimony of John Andrews, counsel for SITLA).
*20 Although assertions by SITLA and the Developer that the City intended to “kill” the Lionsback project are incorrect,10
it is correct that the City informed the Developer that the 2016 change application was a “major change” to the project under
the City's MPD ordinance. R. 393-394.
Although it is obvious that SITLA and the Developer brought the Lionsback development into the City and subjected it to
City ordinances, it was unclear whether SITLA retained the right to withdraw from the City and proceed with the development
without any local land-use control. Moab could have fought an effort to withdraw or exempt the project from local control.
Doing so would likely have resulted in litigation with SITLA, and the associated delay, cost, and uncertainty of results.11 If
litigation turned out to be unsuccessful there was the risk that development of the Lionsback project could proceed under state
auspices without any local control. Instead, the City elected to negotiate a compromise that would result in the Lionsback
Development continuing to be subject to local zoning regulation and review.
i. Living Rivers has no Standing to Contest Moab's Legal Strategy.
In its brief Living Rivers argues that the City should have contested SITLA's attempt to pull out of local government jurisdiction
because it had submitted its property to City land use jurisdiction. Opening Brief, pp. 43-44. Again, this issue *21 was never
preserved for review. State v. Johnson, 416 P.3d 443 ¶ 15, supra. Regardless, Living Rivers has no standing to question the
litigation strategy employed by the City, or its policy judgments. Ultimately, both the Pre-Annexation Agreement and the
Development and Phasing Agreement between the parties, both of which would be in issue in any such litigation, contain
provisions expressly disclaiming any third-party beneficiary rights. See R. 258, 313. Simply because Living Rivers would have
embarked on a different path does not mean that the City's decision, which is influenced by broader local concerns, is invalid.
ii. The City and SITLA Bargained at Arms-Length.
Living Rivers argues that the decision to enter into the ZSA was tainted by collusion between the City and Moab. Opening
Brief, p. 43. In fact, and as the trial court correctly found, there was a genuine dispute between the parties and the ZSA was

The record in this case shows that the ZSA went through multiple drafting iterations that were the product of negotiations
between counsel for SITLA and the City. R. 420-427; R. 428 (changes regarding traffic studies); R. 434 (change regarding
indemnity provision); R. 436 (changes regarding traffic study and inclusion of other SITLA lands); R. 1156-1162 (redline draft);
R. 1166-1172; R. 1191-1197. These emails and revised drafts of the document show that there was bargaining on a number

The ZSA is a fair and reasonable compromise. The document affirms the validity of all prior land use approvals and the absence

SITLA further agreed to indemnify the City with respect to the possibility of future legal challenges pertaining to the

3. THE TRIAL COURT CORRECTLY CONCLUDED THAT DEFERENCE
IS OWED TO LOCAL GOVERNMENT SETTLEMENT DECISIONS.
Living Rivers contends that the trial court erroneously created and misapplied the relevant legal standard for review of the ZSA.
Opening Brief, pp. 38-45. Admittedly, there are no Utah cases which directly address the standard to be applied to a thirdparty *26 challenge to a local government settlement agreement.15 The City noted as much before the trial court. R. 1631.
Nevertheless, the trial court's reasoning and formulation of a standard was appropriate.
As noted before the trial court, in analogous administrative settings the decision to settle a dispute is not reviewable at all because
the government agency is best able to make a determination as to whether a legal action is likely to succeed or whether resources
are best spent in litigation. Association of Irritated Residents v. E.P.A., 494 F.3d 1027, 1031, 1035 (D.C. Cir. 2007) (settlement
agreements between EPA and industry groups not reviewable in suit by public interest groups); Heckler v. Chaney, 470 U.S.
821, 831-832 (1985) (decision by FDA not to take enforcement action not reviewable under Administrative Procedures Act). At
most, review of a public agency settlement is highly deferential, and avoids attempts to adjudicate the merits of the underlying
legal claims. Citizens for a Better Environment v. Gorsuch, 718 F.2d 1117, 1126 (D.C. Cir. 1983).
In the local government land use context, other courts have taken a highly deferential approach to land use settlements. State
ex rel. Obojski v. Perciak, 866 N.E.2d 1070, 1075 (Ohio 2007) (city has discretion to determine whether settlement agreement
is in the best interests of its citizens and courts will not overturn same in the absence of fraud, duress, overreaching, or undue
influence). *27 Pike Indus., Inc. v. City of Westbrook, 43 A.3d 707, 714-718 (Maine 2012) (courts will defer to reasonable
judgments of settling parties, provided that settlement agreement's effect on regulatory scheme is no greater than needed to
achieve the objectives of the agreement).
While there are admittedly cases from other states that apply a more exacting standard in different settings (or statutory schemes),
the trial court's formulation is within this thread of precedent, and closely aligns with analogous Utah precedent. In Clayton v.
Salt Lake City, 387 P.2d 93 (Utah 1963), the court considered a challenge to the award of a public contract and stated:
... the public authority must have a wide latitude in which to exercise its judgment as to the best means of accomplishing its
objective. The court is reluctant to interfere with the administrative function and would do so only if facts were shown to indicate
dishonesty, fraud, collusion, or lack of good faith in the performance of the duty mentioned ... Id. at 94.

This standard comports with the “reasonably debatable” standard for legislative enactments.

Here, the trial court established the standard this way:
... the City must be afforded flexibility to resolve issues in a grey area by negotiation and, emphatically more so in the
circumstances presented by this case where the City's authority to legislate and regulate at all is dubious; therefore, the Court's
review needs to be very generous. The Court's review recognizes the City's discretion to resolve grey areas and that the Court's
review is limited to circumstances where it appears from the record there's been collusion or bad faith between the parties to the
agreement. The Court's review is limited to those cases where it looks like there has been an attempt to use a settlement process
to completely eviscerate the ordinances ... R. 01920 (Order Granting Defendants' Motion for Summary Judgment).

This formulation falls within the scope of the standard established in Utah under Clayton v. Salt Lake City. And, as the preceding
portions of this brief show, there was substantial *28 evidence to support the trial court's conclusion that the decision to enter
into the ZSA was not arbitrary, capricious, or illegal. R. 01920.
a. Living Rivers Was Given the Opportunity to Explore the Context of
the Settlement and the Administrative Record Included those Materials.
In their objection to the standard employed by the trial court, Living Rivers asserts that Plaintiffs were denied the opportunity to
engage in discovery seeking, for example, emails between the City, SITLA, and the Developer seeking evidence of collusion.
Opening Brief, p. 41. Even if Living Rivers would be entitled to discovery, which is not under U.C.A. § 10-9a-801(8)(a)
(i)(review is limited to the administrative record), the fact is that Plaintiffs submitted to the City a requests under the Utah
Governmental Records Access and Management ACT (GRAMA) requesting just such information. U.C.A. § 63G-2-101, et
seq. And, in fact, the City produced responsive emails and communications, including those between the attorneys for SITLA
and the City involving the negotiation of the ZSA. Those communications, which are in the record, squarely rebut the claim
that there was dishonesty, fraud, or collusion in the negotiation of the agreement. See R. 1128-1135; R. 406-419; R. 420-436
(email chains between the City and SITLA describing multiple issues and revisions to the ZSA prior to final approval); R.
1427 (privilege log of documents withheld from production). If anything, it is clear that the parties engaged in back and forth
bargaining, the exact opposite of collusive behavior. Additional discovery would have proved nothing helpful to Living Rivers,
and it was in no way prejudiced by the court's application of the legal standard.
4. THE MOAB APPEAL AUTHORITY LACKED JURISDICTION TO REVIEW THE ZSA.
*29 In its brief Living Rivers contends that its appeal to the Moab Appeal Authority, the lay administrative body formerly
established by the City to adjudicate zoning appeals, was improperly dismissed as a non-decision under LUDMA. Opening
Brief, p. 32, et seq. The argument fails because the Appeal Authority lacked jurisdiction to decide the issues under review for
the reasons stated above.
A local appeal authority is enacted pursuant to U.C.A. § 10-9a-701 to serve as an adjudicatory body and interpret local zoning
ordinances. At the time of the appeal by Living Rivers the Moab appeal authority ordinance provided that:
The powers and duties of the Appeal Authority shall include: A. Hear (sic) appeals on decisions applying to the zoning ordinance
of Moab City ... MMC § 7.72.140(A).16

In addition, the ordinance provided that:
Any person or entity adversely affected by a decision administering or interpreting a zoning ordinance may appeal that decision
applying the zoning ordinance by alleging that there is error in any order, requirement, decision, or interpretation of the zoning
ordinance. MMC § 17.72.150(A)(1).

© 2026 Thomson Reuters. No claim to original U.S. Government Works.

14

Lucy WALLINGFORD, Kiley Miller, John Rzeczycki, Carol..., 2018 WL 10399913...

Finally, the ordinance provided that the only decisions “... applying to the zoning ordinance ...” may be appealed, and that the
Appeal Authority may not “... consider any zoning ordinance amendments.” MMC § 17.72.150(B)(1)(2).
Here, Living Rivers' administrative appeal was properly rejected because the ZSA involved matters that went well beyond the
interpretation of the Moab ordinances. R. *30 0449-0450. The decision to approve the ZSA involved multiple policy and
legal judgments grounded in state law, litigation strategy, and policy questions as to relations with an arm of the State of Utah.
These judgments are truly legislative in character, involving the discretion of the elected governing body and, as such, are not
reviewable by an inferior body of limited jurisdiction. Sandy City v. Salt Lake County, 827 P.2d 212, 220 (Utah 1992)(board of
adjustment had no jurisdiction to enact a zoning classification; any such decision would be an invalid delegation of legislative
authority). Forward looking decisions based on the weighing of broad competing policy considerations are legislative in
character and, when a decision is at least “arguably legislative” courts give understandable deference to the governmental body
making the decision. Suarez v. Grand County, 296 P.3d 688 ¶19 (Utah 2012).
As stated above, the Appeal Authority has no jurisdiction to review whether it was appropriate for the City to settle a dispute
with SITLA. It had no jurisdiction to decide if litigation was appropriate to enforce the various agreements with SITLA, or if it
was better on balance to reach a deal resolving the dispute. The Appeal Authority had no jurisdiction to weigh whether SITLA
had a valid exemption from local zoning under U.C.A. § 10-9a-304, or whether the City could properly waive or modify its
permitting requirements under the Interlocal Cooperation Act. U.C.A. § 11-13-202. Similarly, it had no jurisdiction to decide
the extent of the City Council's contracting power under U.C.A. § 10-1-202, or whether the ZSA was fair and reasonable.
Even if the Appeal Authority had convened and agreed with Living Rivers that the application should be processed as a “major
amendment” under the MPD ordinance, such *31 a decision would have been nothing more than advisory, and not binding
on the City Council, which had broader concerns and legal authorities. Even the subject MPD ordinance provides that the City
Council has the authority to make a determination if a particular change was minor or major. MMC § 17.65.130(A).
The City correctly concluded that this was not a matter to be decided by the Appeal Authority. That decision was supported by
substantial evidence and legal authority, and the basis for that decision was clearly communicated to Living Rivers.
Finally, precedent announced subsequent to the trial court's decision does not alter the conclusions here. In Baker v. Carlson,
2018 WL 6239919, (Utah, Nov. 28, 2018)(not yet released for publication), the Utah Supreme Court considered the legislative/
administrative question as to local government decision making in the context of whether a matter was referable for referendum.
The court held that the City of Holladay considered broad policy considerations when it approved a site development master
plan for a mall property. Id. at ¶ 20, 21. These considerations were forward looking and open ended, rather than an application
of fixed criteria to existing facts. Id. at ¶ 26.
The ZS A here exhibits the same marks of legislative action as the master plan in issue in Baker. The ZSA acknowledges its
purpose to specify the manner in which “future land use applications concerning the property and the Project would be handled
by the parties.” R. 0572, ZSA, subsection O. The agreement contains important substantive changes as to future review of traffic
and road infrastructure concerns, operation of sanitary sewer systems, and building code applicability. Id. at 0573-0574 *32
(Sections 4, 5, and 7). These changes will be applicable generally throughout the Lionsback development when it is subject to
future review. Accordingly, Baker supports the conclusion that Moab acted correctly under legislative auspices.17
