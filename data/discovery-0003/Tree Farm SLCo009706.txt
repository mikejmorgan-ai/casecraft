ARGUMENT

I. SAVE OUR CANYONS INTERVENTION IS PROPER
Utah Administrative Code R641-103-100 allows a party to intervene if its legal interests
“may be substantially affected by the formal adjudicative proceedings” and the “interests of
justice and the orderly and prompt conduct of the adjudicative proceedings will not be materially
impaired by allowing the intervention.” Here, Save Our Canyons meets each requirement.
Save Our Canyons has hundreds of members along the Wasatch Front who will be
impacted by the proposed mine. Its members have many concerns about the proposed mine’s
impacts to their homes, canyon traffic, properties, recreational use of the Wasatch Mountains,
health, air quality, and wildlife, to name a few. See Exhibits A & B attached. The interests of its
members will be substantially affected by a decision by the Board allowing mining to commence
under the SMO wherein the applicant is skirting the public process requirements of the required
LMO filing prior to commencement of mining activities. In addition, Save Our Canyons intends
to participate in the public review of the Large Mining NOI and had been focusing its efforts on
reviewing and identifying numerous deficiencies with the same. Now that there is a public
review process of Tree Farm’s Small Mining NOI Request for Agency Action, Save Our

3
1780542.1

Canyons believes it is an appropriate party to the administrative review process of Applicant’s
request and wishes to be involved in the review process to present its support of the Division’s
decision and protect its members interests prior to mining commencing under the Small Mining
NOI.
In addition, the adjudicative proceedings will not be materially impaired by allowing the
intervention. The Division recently agreed to continue this matter to its February 23, 2022 board
meeting in order to review a Petition for Intervention filed by Millcreek City. Save Our
Canyon’s interests are similar to those of Millcreek City and also support the Division’s decision
to deny the Small Mining Operation NOI. Save Our Canyons has raised additional legal
arguments below that may also prove helpful to the Board in supporting the Division’s actions
pertaining to the SMO. Accordingly, Save Our Canyons will be prepared for the February 23,
2022 meeting and does not require additional time outside of the continuance already approved
by the Division to participate in this process.
II. THE DIVISION OF OIL GAS & MINING HAS AUTHORITY TO DENY A
SMALL MINING OPERATION NOTICE OF INTENT THAT IS INCOMPLETE
AND AGAINST PUBLIC POLICY
A. THE PROPER PROCESS FOR A PLANNED LARGE MINING OPERATION IS TO
APPLY FOR AN LMO NOI UNDER UTAH LAW
Tree Farm’s filing of a Small Mining NOI is procedurally infirm causing the legal
predicament facing the Board. When reviewing the Applicant’s filings as a whole, it is clear
the intention of the Applicant is to commence a large mining operation in phases. The
Division’s rules require a review of the applicant’s complete information before authorizing
mining operations. Specifically, the “Notice of Intention” “must provide the complete

4
1780542.1

information required for authorization to conduct mining operations, and includes any
amendments or revisions thereto.” Utah Admin R647-1-106. Here, the complete information
includes the intention of Tree Farm to commence mining operations in phases and disturb 634
acres, which is significantly more disturbed land than allowed for under a Small Mining NOI.
The Applicant explicitly states that it intends to mine in phases from north to south and the area
covered under the Small Mining Operation would consist of its first stage. Large Mine NOI, at
106.2. Tree Farm’s filings also admit to disturbance of 634 acres in Salt Lake County. See
Large Mine NOI Table 106.3-1. These admissions remove the ability of the Division to process
the application under the SMO provisions because the complete information reveals the
Applicant doesn’t qualify as an SMO. In this case, the Division’s interpretation of its own
governing statutes should be given considerable weight and requiring the applicant to process
its application under the Large Mining NOI process should be upheld. See LPI Servs. V.
McGee, 2009 UT 41, ¶8, 215 P.3d 135; Wells Fargo Armored Serv. Corp. v. Pub. Serv.
Comm’n of Utah, 626 P.2d 450, 451 (“The time honored rule of law is that the construction of
statutes by governmental agencies charged with their administration should be given
considerable weight…”).
This is not to say a mining operation can never change its status under the Rules, but
simultaneous processing of two conflicting applications is not contemplated. In the
administrative rules dealing with both the LMO and the SMO applications, there are provisions
allowing an SMO to enlarge its mining operations if, after receiving an approved NOI for an
SMO, the operator then decides to exceed the SMO threshold and enlarge the mine. See R6473-114. Similarly in the LMO rules, there are provisions allowing an LMO to revise its plans to
5
1780542.1

become an SMO if it decreases its acreage to the necessary SMO acreage requirements after
commencement of an LMO. See R647-4-118(3). But where a decision has already been made
to exceed the thresholds for a Small Mining NOI, the only proper course of conduct by the
Division is to treat the applicant as an LMO and follow the outlined public process required
under Utah law. This is in line with the legislature’s intent to require mining to be done “in
such a way as to minimize undesirable effects on the surroundings” Utah Code Ann. §40-8-2.
Furthermore, the Board has authority to review all of the filings of the Application and “take all
actions necessary to enforce the provisions of the Mining Reclamation Act.” See Utah Code
Ann. §40-8-6(4). This includes requiring a proposed SMO and LMO for the same mine to
follow the more rigorous approval process under the LMO regulations. Simply stated, if it
walks like an LMO and qualifies as an LMO, the proper procedure is to process the proposed
mine as an LMO.

B. SAVE OUR CANYONS JOINS IN THE ARGUMENTS RAISED BY MILLCREEK
CITY’S PETITION FOR INTERVENTION AND THE DIVISION’S RESPONSE IN
OPPOSITION TO PETITITIONER’S REQUEST FOR AGENCY ACTION
In an effort to avoid duplication, Save Our Canyons herein incorporates by reference and
joins in the legal arguments raised in the Division’s Response in Opposition to Petitioner’s
Request for Agency Action and Millcreek City’s Petition for Intervention.
Save Our Canyons specifically finds the arguments with regard to the Division’s policy
of disallowing “double permitting” of sites compelling and in line with its arguments raised in
Section A above that the applicant cannot simultaneously apply for both a Large and Small
Mining NOI when it is clear the operator intends to commence an LMO at the outset. Under

6
1780542.1

Utah law, courts “read the plain language of the statute as a whole, and interpret its provisions in
harmony with other statutes in the same chapter and related chapters.” Bryner v. Cardon
Outreach, LLC, 2018 UT 52, ¶10, 428 P.3d 1096). Reading the Division’s Administrative Rules
as a whole, allowing this unusual administrative process to go forward would create a situation
that would make the enlargement or revision sections of the Division’s Administrative Rules
cited above superfluous, because mining operators could just apply for and receive permitting for
both types of operations at the same time. Utah Courts do not allow this type of statutory
construction. See Turner v. Staker & Parson Cos, 2012 UT 30, ¶12, 284 P.3d 600 (wherever
possible, we must give effect to every word, avoiding any interpretation which renders parts or
words in a provision inoperative or superfluous). Instead, the Division’s interpretation of the
administrative rules gives effect to all of the provisions therein by requiring a single NOI to be
filed using the complete information from the applicant, which indicates a Large Mining NOI is
required, and then revising the NOI if the actual on the ground conditions change after mining
operations have commenced.
III. CONCLUSION
Save Our Canyons respectfully requests the Board grant its Petition to Intervene and
uphold the Division’s decision to deny Applicant’s Small Mine NOI. Save Our Canyons is
deeply concerned with regard to the precedent created by a mining applicant seeking to
circumvent required public processes for mining operation approvals in this manner by creating a
“phased” implementation that avoids the rigorous process the State of Utah has put in place to
commence mining operations for a mine that will disturb 634 acres of land. Accordingly, it is in
line with the legislature’s intent and authority granted to the Division, as well as Division’s Rules

7
1780542.1

governing both Large and Small Mining Operations, to deny an application that does not qualify
as an SMO and allow the public to be involved in the approval process of what is actually an
LMO.
