# CLAIM 1: CIM PREEMPTION -- ORDINANCE ILLEGALLY PROHIBITS CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE MATERIALS OPERATIONS

## BINDER SECTION -- TRIAL NOTEBOOK

---

## I. CASE THEORY SUMMARY

Salt Lake County's 2022 amendment to Section 19.12.030 of the County Code eliminated mineral extraction and processing as a conditional use in the Forestry and Recreation (FR) zone and the Foothills and Canyons Overlay Zone (FCOZ). This ordinance directly violates Utah Code Section 17-41-402(6), enacted through H.B. 288 (the Critical Infrastructure Materials Act, or "CIM Act"), which expressly prohibits any county from adopting, enacting, or amending a land use regulation that would "prohibit, restrict, regulate, or otherwise limit critical infrastructure materials operations." Limestone, sand, gravel, and rock aggregate -- the precise materials Tree Farm, LLC sought to extract -- are statutorily defined as critical infrastructure materials. The County's ordinance does exactly what the CIM Act forbids: it prohibits CIM operations where they were not prohibited before.

The County's anticipated defense is that extraction was "already excluded" from the FR zone, and therefore the ordinance did not change the legal landscape. This defense is demolished by the County's own documentary record. Internal emails from County staff -- including Michael Shea's briefing memo to Mayor Wilson, Gregory Baptist's correspondence with citizens, and the County DA office's own letter from Ralph Chamness -- uniformly confirm that mineral extraction was a conditional use available under Section 19.12.030(J) before the ordinance was passed. The Mayor herself publicly stated the County was "eliminating" extraction as a conditional use. You cannot eliminate what does not exist. Every one of these admissions is a party admission usable at trial.

Moreover, the County received formal, specific, written notice that its ordinance violated the CIM Act before it was finalized. Attorney Brent Bateman put the County on notice in February 2022, quoting the statutory text verbatim. The State Property Rights Ombudsman accepted Tree Farm's mediation request, finding the preemption issues raised "may be appropriate for mediation and/or arbitration." County staff member Christie Morgan internally acknowledged the ordinance "may conflict" with the 2019 CIM law, and Councilwoman Aimee Winder Newton responded by asking about "state legislative workarounds." The County proceeded anyway, with full knowledge that it was violating state law. This record negates any good-faith defense and supports an award of attorneys' fees.

The complete enrolled copy of House Bill 288, the Critical Infrastructure Materials Act of 2019. *"'Mining protection area' means land where a vested mining use occurs, including each surface or subsurface land or mineral estate that a mine operator with a vested mining use owns or controls."*
